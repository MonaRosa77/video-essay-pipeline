# Philosophy & Ethics Analysis

Generates structured analyses of philosophy, ethics, metaphysics, and epistemology lectures and discussions.

## Activation

Use this skill when analyzing transcripts about:
- Western philosophy (ancient, medieval, modern, contemporary)
- Ethics and moral philosophy
- Metaphysics, epistemology, philosophy of mind
- Political philosophy and social theory
- Religious philosophy and theology

Topic slugs: `philosophy`, or any philosophy-related topic.

## Analysis Template

Generate the analysis using this exact structure:

### Header
```markdown
# Analysis: <Descriptive Title>

- **Video**: <youtube_url>
- **Channel**: <channel_name>
- **Analyzed**: <YYYY-MM-DD>

---
```

### Central Claim
One substantial paragraph (150-300 words) identifying:
- The speaker's **core thesis** — not just the topic, but the specific *claim* being made
- How this thesis is framed and what makes it distinctive
- The scope and ambition of the argument

### Supporting Arguments
Numbered list (aim for 5-10). Each argument gets:
- **Bold title** summarizing the argument in one phrase
- A paragraph explaining the argument's logic, evidence used, and rhetorical function
- How it connects to the central claim

Assess the **logical structure**: Is the argument deductive, inductive, analogical, or rhetorical? Does each step follow from the previous?

### Evidence Quality Assessment
Split into **Strengths** and **Weaknesses**:

**Strengths** — What is well-supported:
- Primary text familiarity and accuracy of citations/paraphrases
- Historical and contextual framing quality
- Original interpretive contributions

**Weaknesses** — What is thin or problematic:
- Missing citations or specificity
- Oversimplifications of historical claims
- Gaps in philosophical engagement (e.g., skipping technical content)
- Over-reliance on rhetoric vs. close argumentation

### Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots
**Minimum 5 counter-arguments.** Each should:
- Name the specific claim being challenged
- Provide the counter-evidence or counter-argument with sources where possible
- Assess severity (does this undermine the thesis, or is it a minor qualification?)

Look specifically for:
- Historical claims that are contestable or oversimplified
- Philosophical positions presented uncritically
- Missing engagement with major scholarly debates
- Unstated assumptions or ideological commitments
- Comparisons that obscure real differences

### Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing
Number each (aim for 8-12). For each:
- The quote or concept (attributed correctly)
- Why it's worth developing — what essay or argument could grow from it
- Connections to other thinkers or traditions

### Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas
Three clearly separated sections:

**Ideas from [Primary Thinker] (via the text/lecture)**
- List ideas that belong to the subject being discussed

**Ideas from Other Thinkers (cited by the speaker)**
- Each attributed with context: who said it, in what work, and how the speaker uses it
- Flag any attributions that are imprecise or contested

**Speaker's Own Interpretive Contributions**
- What is original to this speaker — their unique readings, metaphors, framings
- These are the most valuable parts for essay development

### Factual Notes Requiring Verification
Numbered list of any claims that should be checked:
- Historical dates, attributions, biographical claims
- Philosophical positions attributed to specific thinkers
- Quotes that may be paraphrased rather than exact

## Quality Criteria

- Every counter-argument must engage a *specific* claim, not offer vague criticism
- The "Original vs. Cited" section must not confuse the speaker's ideas with those of the thinker being discussed
- Factual claims about historical events, dates, or philosophical positions must be verified before presenting — flag anything uncertain
- The analysis should be useful for essay development: someone should be able to read it and identify 3-5 essay topics

## Core Strategy Premise

> "The basic premise of this strategy is the initiative that gets trapped and had to cover fast."

This is a scalping reversal strategy focused on fast move-to-move targets, lasting **"no longer than a couple of minutes from initiation to exit."**

Primary tool: Price ladder for extracting market edge, combined with price action and order-flow execution.

---

## Key Concepts

### High Volume Node (HVN)
A price level where significant volume has congregated, typically at market extremes. On a volume profile, this shows as a wide horizontal bar. In this strategy, the HVN forms AT the extreme where the failed breakout occurs. It represents the "trap" -- all that volume is trapped participants.

### Low Volume Node (LVN)
The gap or thin zone left behind after an impulsive move away from the HVN. After the reversal "jump" occurs, the LVN is the area price moved through quickly (low participation), confirming the impulsive nature of the reversal. LVN behind the move = confirmation that the reversal has genuine force.

## The Setup Framework

1. **Identify an extreme** -- local high/low or daily extreme
   - Rule: **"Don't pick any low or high, but add extra clues to maximize your trading edge."**
   - Need additional context (prior support/resistance, exhaustion pattern, volume profile feature)

2. **Watch for HVN to form at that extreme** -- volume concentrates, participants committing to the move

3. **Wait for failed follow-through** -- the key signal
   - On the price ladder: volume transacting at the extreme but price not extending
   - Sellers (at a low) or buyers (at a high) cannot push further despite the volume commitment

4. **Entry trigger: the "flick back up" (or down for shorts)**
   - Sharp, impulsive reversal off the HVN
   - > "That jump after sellers can't push any lower is what we want to see."
   - Enter on this jump move, not before it

5. **Target: the "point of origin of the last swing leg"**
   - The price level where the most recent move down (or up) began
   - > "Our goal is not to take this to any outstanding targets."
   - Capturing the short squeeze / cover move, nothing more

6. **Stop: just below the HVN at the lows** (for longs) or just above HVN at highs (for shorts)
   - The HVN is your invalidation -- if price returns through that volume concentration, the "trapped" thesis is wrong

## S&P 500 Trade Example (Long Reversal)

1. Price pushes to a new low during the session
2. Sellers attempt to push lower but fail -- volume concentrates at the low, forming an HVN
3. Price "flicks back up" -- impulsive move off the lows
4. An LVN zone is left behind (price moved quickly through this area, confirming impulsiveness)
5. **Entry:** On the flick/jump back up, after confirming the HVN held
6. **Stop:** Just below the HVN at the lows
7. **Target:** The point of origin of the last downswing leg (where the sell-off started)
8. **Duration:** Couple of minutes maximum

4. "Jump move" after exhaustion -- entry signal
5. Trapped sellers from the failed breakdown must cover, fueling the reversal

## How to Identify Trapped Participants on the Price Ladder

- **Volume concentrating at the extreme** without price extension = participants committing but failing
- **Failed follow-through** = trades executing at bid (for a low) but bid holds or price ticks up
- **The "jump" or "flick"** = sudden shift from selling to buying pressure (or vice versa), visible as rapid upticks on the ladder
- **LVN left behind** = price moves through multiple levels quickly with low volume, confirming trapped traders are rushing to cover rather than new initiative entering

---

## What Distinguishes This From Regular Mean Reversion

This is NOT statistical mean reversion (price is X standard deviations from mean, so fade it). This is **microstructure-driven**: you read real-time order flow to confirm specific participants are trapped and must exit. The edge comes from the **urgency of the cover** (trapped traders exit fast, creating a sharp move), not from a statistical tendency to revert.

## Rules

1. Hold time: strictly short -- couple of minutes max. If the move hasn't happened by then, something is wrong.
2. Stop at the HVN -- structurally defined, not arbitrary.
3. Target is modest -- origin of last swing, not an extended move.
4. **"Have a clear process of how you identify these extremes"** -- don't trade every reversal.
5. **"Watch your risk."**

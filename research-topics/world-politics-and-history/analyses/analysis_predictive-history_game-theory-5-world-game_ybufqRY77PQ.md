# Analysis: Game Theory #5 — The World Game

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ybufqRY77PQ
- **Channel**: Predictive History (Prof. Jiang Xueqin)
- **Series**: Game Theory #5

---

## Summary

In this lecture, Jiang Xueqin poses the central question of why states, countries, and nations rise and fall, arguing that conventional metrics like population, resources, and technology are insufficient to predict which group will come to dominate a region. Drawing on examples from the Chinese Warring States period, the Greek city-states, the Aztec Empire, and ancient Sumer, he argues that throughout history, it is consistently the marginalized, peripheral peoples — not the wealthy, established centers — who conquer and build empires.

Jiang introduces three metrics for measuring a society's dynamism: **energy** (motivation and willingness to work hard), **openness** (humility, adaptability, resilience), and **cohesion** (group solidarity and willingness to sacrifice for one another). He explicitly draws on **Ibn Khaldun's concept of asabiyyah** (group solidarity) to argue that peripheral peoples maintain these qualities precisely because of their poverty and marginalization, while wealthy established societies become insular, corrupt, and divided.

The lecture then traces the game-theoretic stages of civilizational development: an initial cooperative phase built around energizing religion; the emergence of hereditary elites and elite overproduction; colonization and the Warring States period of open cooperative competition (the height of creativity); the formation of equilibrium through elite intermarriage; the rise of court politics, factional struggle, and secret societies; and finally the decay that opens the door to conquest by peripheral groups who were often initially invited in as mercenaries.

Jiang illustrates his framework with a classroom simulation called "the World Game," where teams assigned countries with unequal resources compete to generate wealth. He claims the team starting with nothing (Pakistan) consistently outperforms wealthier teams because it is forced to be creative, energetic, and cohesive. He concludes with geopolitical predictions: that Germany, Japan, and Israel will become great empires based on their histories of defeat and humiliation, while the US and China are in decline. He also provocatively suggests that North Korea has a better future than China, and that international development organizations exist partly to prevent the emergence of leaders who might unify marginalized peoples.

---

## Key Arguments

1. **Conventional metrics fail**: Population, resources, and technology do not reliably predict which state will conquer and build an empire. Throughout history, it is the poorest and most marginalized groups that prevail.

2. **Three metrics of dynamism**: Energy (motivation/work ethic), Openness (humility/adaptability), and Cohesion (group solidarity/willingness to sacrifice) are the true predictors of a society's success.

3. **Asabiyyah as an iron law**: Drawing on Ibn Khaldun, Jiang argues this pattern — marginal groups conquering wealthy centers — is so consistent it constitutes an "iron law of history."

4. **Civilizational life cycle as a game-theoretic sequence**: Societies pass through predictable stages: cooperation (religion-driven) --> hereditary elite formation --> elite overproduction --> colonization/Warring States --> equilibrium/empire --> factional struggle via secret societies --> decay and conquest by outsiders.

5. **The Warring States period is the peak of creativity**: Open cooperative competition between city-states produces maximum innovation (e.g., classical Greece, Chinese hundred schools of thought).

6. **Secret societies as the mechanism of imperial decay**: Once equilibrium is reached, factions form secret societies that solve problems of secrecy (through hierarchy/compartmentalization), trust (through transgression/ritual sacrifice), and coordination (through eschatology/mythology). These factions become insular, corrupt, and divided.

7. **Mercenaries as the mechanism of conquest**: Decaying empires invite peripheral peoples in as mercenaries; those mercenaries eventually take over.

8. **Empires never recover once fallen**: Once a group loses its position, it does not regain it. A new group may use the old name, but it is fundamentally different people.

9. **Geopolitical prediction**: Germany, Japan, and Israel will become great empires. The US and China are finished. North Korea may have a better future than China.

10. **International development as suppression**: NGOs, the UN, and international aid function to prevent the emergence of unifying leaders in marginalized regions like Africa.

## Theoretical Framework

Jiang synthesizes several game theory and social science concepts:

- **Ibn Khaldun's Asabiyyah**: The core theoretical anchor. Asabiyyah describes group solidarity that is strongest among marginal/nomadic peoples and decays with urbanization and wealth. Jiang faithfully represents the cyclical element of the theory.

- **Elite Overproduction**: The concept that hereditary privilege creates more elite claimants than positions of power can absorb, leading to factional conflict. This concept is associated with Peter Turchin's work in cliodynamics, though Jiang does not credit Turchin by name.

- **Game-Theoretic Stages**: Jiang frames each phase of civilizational development as a distinct "game" with different players, strategies, incentives, and equilibria. The game shifts from cooperation to competition to factional struggle.

- **Nash Equilibrium (implicit)**: The concept of "equilibrium" when intermarrying elites reach a stable arrangement where no faction can unilaterally improve its position maps loosely onto game-theoretic equilibrium concepts.

- **Principal-Agent Problems in Secret Societies**: The three challenges of secret societies (secrecy, trust, coordination) correspond to well-known problems in mechanism design and organizational theory.

- **Open Cooperative Competition**: The Warring States period is framed as a scenario where competing units share innovations through trade while competing militarily — a concept related to competitive federalism and the European "states system" thesis (popularized by scholars like Eric Jones in *The European Miracle*).

## Historical & Factual Claims

1. **Qin dynasty unified China from a position of marginality** (~250 BCE). Jiang describes Qin as mountainous, poor, isolated, and far from rivers and trade routes.

2. **Greek city-states numbered in the thousands, with populations averaging about 1,000 people each.** Athens at its height had about 50,000 people.

3. **Athens had the greatest navy, controlled Aegean trade routes, and was the wealthiest city-state.**

4. **Macedonia was poor, isolated, uncultured, and uncivilized** when it conquered the Greek city-states.

5. **Alexander the Great conquered the entire Persian Empire in about 10 years** after landing a small force across the Aegean.

6. **The Persian Empire was "the first great empire in human history"** and encompassed Egypt, Anatolia, Mesopotamia, the Zagros mountains, and Persia.

7. **The Romans were "poor backward tribal people"** who conquered the Italian peninsula while it was being colonized by the Greeks, then went on to conquer Greece and Carthage.

8. **The Aztecs migrated from North America as a poor, starving tribe** around the 15th-16th century, were hired as mercenary warriors, were isolated onto a marsh, and turned it into farmland before building an empire.

9. **Hernan Cortes arrived with about 500 conquistadors in 1519** and conquered the Aztec Empire in a short time.

10. **Uruk was the center of the world and the first great world city** because of its position at the meeting place of the Euphrates and Tigris, providing access to the Persian Gulf, India (Indus Valley Civilization), Egypt, and Anatolia.

11. **The Sumerians were the first great civilization** and Uruk set up colonies, which became competing city-states.

12. **The Akkadians were tribal mercenaries from an isolated region** who conquered the Sumerian city-states and built "the first empire."

13. **Ibn Khaldun ("Ibin Kudan") was a Muslim scholar/historian** who proposed the idea of asabiyyah (group solidarity/cohesion).

14. **The Plaza Accord ("Plastic Courts")**: America forced Japan to destroy its economy by making Japan spend too much money and stop saving.

15. **Germany's Nord Stream pipeline was destroyed by America**, and America forced Germany out of the China market and the Russia energy market.

16. **Japan and Germany were forced to buy US Treasury bonds**, making them vassal states of America.

17. **American firebombing of Japan killed more people than the atomic bombs.**

18. **In STEM education workshops, the team ranked last on day one consistently finished first on day two**, with results perfectly reversed.

## Fact-Check & Verification

1. **Qin as mountainous, poor, and isolated**: **QUESTIONABLE**. This is a significant oversimplification. The Qin state was based in the **Guanzhong Plain** (Wei River Valley), one of the most fertile agricultural regions in ancient China. While Qin was geographically peripheral and looked down upon culturally by Central Plains states (they absorbed Rong and Di peoples), calling it "mountainous" and "poor" misrepresents its actual resource base. The Guanzhong Plain was flat, fertile, and well-irrigated. Qin's military reforms under Shang Yang and its legalist administrative system — not mere energy and cohesion — were decisive in its rise. The description better fits the state of Yan or early Chu than Qin.

2. **Greek city-states averaging 1,000 people; Athens at 50,000**: **QUESTIONABLE**. The number of Greek city-states (poleis) is roughly correct — scholars estimate 1,000-1,500 existed. Some smaller ones may have had populations around 1,000. However, Athens at its height (5th century BCE) had an estimated total population of **250,000-300,000** (including citizens, metics, women, children, and slaves), with about 30,000-50,000 adult male citizens. The figure of 50,000 drastically understates Athens's actual size by a factor of 5-6x. Even the citizen body alone was larger than 50,000 when families are included.

3. **Athens had the greatest navy and controlled Aegean trade**: **VERIFIED**. Athens's thalassocracy (naval dominance) in the 5th century BCE is well-established. The Delian League gave Athens control over Aegean trade routes.

4. **Macedonia as poor, isolated, uncultured, uncivilized**: **PARTIALLY VERIFIED / OVERSIMPLIFIED**. Macedonia was indeed considered peripheral and semi-barbarous by many Greeks. However, Macedonian kings claimed Greek descent (Argead dynasty), and the court at Pella was culturally Greek — Euripides spent his final years there, Aristotle tutored Alexander. Calling them "uncivilized" overstates the case. They were peripheral but not uncivilized.

5. **Alexander conquered the Persian Empire in about 10 years**: **VERIFIED**. Alexander crossed into Asia in 334 BCE and completed his conquests by approximately 324 BCE, roughly 10 years.

6. **The Persian Empire as "the first great empire in human history"**: **QUESTIONABLE**. The Achaemenid Persian Empire (founded c. 550 BCE) is often called the first "world empire," but earlier empires include the Akkadian Empire (c. 2334 BCE), the Third Dynasty of Ur, the Egyptian New Kingdom, and the Assyrian Empire. Confusingly, Jiang himself mentions the Akkadian Empire elsewhere in the lecture as "the first empire." The Achaemenid Empire is better described as the first truly multi-continental empire or the largest empire up to that point.

7. **Romans as "poor backward tribal people"**: **PARTIALLY VERIFIED / OVERSIMPLIFIED**. Early Rome was indeed a small settlement in Latium, and the Italian peninsula had significant Greek colonies (Magna Graecia). However, Rome's early history shows considerable institutional sophistication (the Republic was established c. 509 BCE), and the conquest of Italy took centuries — this was not a sudden barbarian takeover.

8. **Aztec/Mexica migration and mercenary role**: **VERIFIED**. The Mexica did migrate from the north (from the semi-legendary Aztlan) in the early 13th century, served as mercenaries for established powers around Lake Texcoco, were exiled to a marshy island, and founded Tenochtitlan (1325 CE), which they developed using chinampas (floating gardens). This is broadly accurate.

9. **Cortes arrived with ~500 conquistadors in 1519**: **VERIFIED**. Historical sources confirm approximately 500 soldiers (plus about 100 sailors) landed with Cortes in 1519.

10. **Uruk at the meeting point of Euphrates and Tigris**: **QUESTIONABLE**. Uruk was located on the Euphrates River in southern Mesopotamia, not at the confluence of the Euphrates and Tigris. The two rivers meet further south. However, Uruk's identification as the first great city is well-supported — by ~3100 BCE it had a population of 40,000-80,000 and was the largest urban center in the world.

11. **Sumerians as the first great civilization**: **VERIFIED**. Widely accepted in scholarship, though some scholars argue for Egyptian or Indus Valley civilizations as contemporaneous.

12. **Akkadians as tribal mercenaries from an isolated region**: **QUESTIONABLE**. Sargon of Akkad rose from relatively humble origins (legend says he was a cupbearer), but the Akkadians were a Semitic-speaking population living alongside Sumerians in Mesopotamia, not isolated outsiders. Sargon built the first professional standing army rather than leading mercenaries. The characterization of Akkadians as isolated tribal mercenaries overstates their marginality.

13. **Ibn Khaldun and asabiyyah**: **VERIFIED**. Ibn Khaldun (1332-1406) was a North African Arab historian/scholar who developed the concept of asabiyyah in his *Muqaddimah*. The cyclical theory of peripheral groups with strong solidarity conquering decadent urban civilizations is faithfully represented.

14. **The Plaza Accord**: **PARTIALLY VERIFIED / MISLEADING**. The Plaza Accord (1985) was a multilateral agreement between the US, Japan, West Germany, France, and the UK to depreciate the US dollar relative to the yen and Deutsche Mark. It did contribute to the Japanese asset bubble and subsequent "Lost Decade." However, characterizing it as America simply ordering Japan to "destroy its economy" is a drastic oversimplification. It was a multilateral negotiation, and Japan's bubble was also driven by domestic monetary policy decisions by the Bank of Japan. The name is "Plaza Accord," not "Plastic Courts" (likely a transcription error from the auto-generated captions).

15. **America destroyed Nord Stream**: **UNVERIFIED / DISPUTED**. The Nord Stream pipelines were sabotaged in September 2022. Investigations have pointed in various directions including Ukrainian operatives, but no government has been officially charged. Germany issued arrest warrants for Ukrainian suspects. Attributing the sabotage definitively to America is not supported by confirmed evidence, though it remains a theory promoted by some commentators (including journalist Seymour Hersh).

16. **Japan and Germany forced to buy US Treasury bonds**: **PARTIALLY VERIFIED**. Japan is historically one of the largest holders of US Treasury bonds, and there is scholarly debate about the degree to which this reflects voluntary investment versus structural dependence within the US-led financial order. Calling them "vassal states" is an interpretive/polemical characterization, not an established fact.

17. **American firebombing killed more Japanese than the atomic bombs**: **VERIFIED**. The firebombing of Tokyo alone (March 9-10, 1945) killed an estimated 80,000-100,000 people in a single night. The broader strategic bombing campaign killed far more than the combined death tolls of Hiroshima (~70,000-80,000 immediate deaths) and Nagasaki (~40,000 immediate deaths).

18. **STEM workshop: last-place team consistently finishes first on day two**: **NEEDS VERIFICATION**. This is presented as personal anecdotal experience. There is no way to verify this claim independently, and the assertion that results are "perfectly reversed" every time sounds like an idealized anecdote shaped to fit the theory. Research on underdog effects and growth mindset exists, but the specific claim of perfect reversal is likely exaggerated.

## Controversial or Potentially Misleading Claims

1. **"North Korea has a better future than China"**: This is an extremely provocative claim that applies the asabiyyah framework far too mechanistically. Poverty alone does not generate the energy-openness-cohesion triad, as Jiang himself acknowledges when a student pushes back. North Korea's extreme totalitarianism, information isolation, and economic stagnation make this comparison deeply problematic. The framework ignores institutional quality, technological capacity, and the nature of the regime.

2. **"The Americans are done" / "China is done"**: These sweeping civilizational death sentences are stated without qualification beyond the theoretical framework. They ignore the possibility of institutional renewal, reform, or adaptation — phenomena that do occur in history (e.g., Japan's Meiji Restoration, China's own post-Mao reforms).

3. **International development as deliberate suppression**: The claim that NGOs, the UN, and international aid exist primarily to prevent the emergence of unifying leaders in Africa is conspiratorial in tone. While there is legitimate scholarship on how foreign aid can create dependency and distort local power structures, framing it as a deliberate imperial strategy to keep Africa down is a strong interpretive leap.

4. **Secret societies as universal mechanism of imperial politics**: While secret societies and factions have played roles in many empires, presenting them as a universal mechanism — identical from ancient Sumer to China to the Aztecs — is an overgeneralization. The specific mechanisms of factional struggle vary enormously across cultures and periods.

5. **Qin as a classic marginalized-outsider conquest**: As noted in fact-checking, Qin was one of the most powerful and resource-rich Warring States before unification. Its triumph owed much to Shang Yang's legalist reforms, the Dujiangyan and Zhengguo irrigation projects, and systematic military professionalization. Framing it as a poor mountain tribe that succeeded through sheer cohesion distorts the historical record.

6. **"Poverty leads to creativity"**: While there is some truth to the idea that constraint breeds resourcefulness, this is stated as a near-universal law. It ignores the many cases where poverty leads to stagnation, brain drain, and collapse rather than creative breakthrough.

7. **The Plaza Accord as America unilaterally destroying Japan's economy**: As noted, this was a multilateral agreement, and Japan's subsequent bubble was also driven by domestic policy choices. The framing strips Japan of agency.

## Original vs. Cited Ideas

### Ideas drawn from other thinkers:
- **Ibn Khaldun's asabiyyah**: Jiang explicitly credits this to Ibn Khaldun. The cyclical theory of peripheral solidarity overthrowing decadent urban centers is faithfully borrowed.
- **Elite overproduction**: This concept, though Jiang does not credit Peter Turchin, closely mirrors Turchin's work in *Ages of Discord* and *End Times*. The specific term "elite overproduction" and the mechanism of too many heirs competing for too few positions is Turchin's contribution to cliodynamics.
- **The World Game (classroom simulation)**: Jiang presents this as a game he played in high school in Canada. Variants of this simulation (sometimes called the "Trading Game" or "Starpower") have been used in development education for decades, originally created by organizations like Christian Aid.

### Jiang's original framing and synthesis:
- **The three-metric framework (Energy, Openness, Cohesion)**: While rooted in asabiyyah, the specific operationalization into these three metrics appears to be Jiang's own formulation.
- **The game-theoretic staging of civilizational development**: The specific sequence (religion --> hereditary elite --> elite overproduction --> colonization --> Warring States --> equilibrium --> secret societies --> decay) is Jiang's own synthesis, combining elements from multiple thinkers.
- **Secret societies as the solution to secrecy/trust/coordination problems**: The framing of secret societies through the lens of mechanism design (hierarchy for secrecy, transgression for trust, eschatology for coordination) appears to be Jiang's original contribution.
- **The geopolitical predictions** (Germany, Japan, Israel as future empires): These specific applications are Jiang's own extrapolations.
- **The STEM workshop anecdote**: Personal experience used to illustrate the theory.
- **The insight that mercenaries are invited in by internal factions**: While historically documented in many cases, the framing as a universal mechanism of imperial collapse is Jiang's synthesis.

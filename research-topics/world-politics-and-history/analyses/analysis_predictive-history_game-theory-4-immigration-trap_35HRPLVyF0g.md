# Analysis: Game Theory #4 --- The Immigration Trap

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=35HRPLVyF0g
- **Channel**: Predictive History (Prof. Jiang Xueqin)
- **Series**: Game Theory #4

## Summary

In this lecture, Jiang Xueqin examines immigration through a game-theoretic lens, asking who "wins" and who "loses" the game of immigration. He begins by presenting data on income disparities among ethnic groups in the United States, noting that Indian Americans and East Asians earn above average while Latinos and Black Americans earn below average. He then introduces PISA test scores as a predictor of future economic performance, observing that East Asian countries dominate the rankings.

However, Jiang argues that academic and economic success does not translate into status for East Asian men in the United States. He presents data on CEO representation and dating market statistics to argue that East Asian men, despite playing by the rules --- excelling in school, getting good jobs, staying out of trouble --- are systematically disadvantaged in terms of social status and mate selection. He cites OkCupid data showing racial preferences in dating and a study claiming that an Asian man must earn roughly $247,000--$300,000 per year to be as attractive to a white woman as a white man earning $62,000.

Jiang then introduces what he calls "the immigration trap": a game-theoretic argument that playing by the host nation's rules is a losing strategy for immigrants, because those rules are designed to extract value from them (analogized to a casino). He argues the superior strategy is to refuse to assimilate, maintain ethnic and religious cohesion, and have many children --- as he claims Latino and Muslim immigrant communities do --- because demographic replacement is the only path to ultimately "setting the rules of the game." He supports this with Pew Research projections of growing Muslim populations in Europe and Hispanic populations in the United States.

The lecture concludes with a historical excursus on the Proto-Indo-Europeans and the Yamnaya steppe migration as an example of population replacement, and a discussion of how modern immigration norms arose as a "historical accident" of British colonialism and post-WWII American hegemony. Jiang draws on his personal experience as a Chinese-Canadian Yale graduate who moved to China to argue that the immigration game is structurally rigged against East Asian men, and that contributing to one's home country is the more rational choice.

## Key Arguments

1. **Selection effects explain immigrant income disparities**: Indian Americans earn the most because only the "best and brightest" from India's competitive educational system immigrate to the US, not because Indians are inherently superior.

2. **PISA scores predict national economic futures**: East Asian countries dominate PISA rankings, which the OECD uses to forecast economic competitiveness 10-30 years out.

3. **Academic success does not equal status for East Asian men**: Despite high incomes and educational achievement, East Asian men are underrepresented as CEOs and face significant disadvantages in the dating and marriage market.

4. **The "bamboo ceiling" is real**: East Asian men lack the traits (assertiveness, risk tolerance, emotional intelligence, articulateness) that American corporate culture rewards for leadership positions.

5. **The immigration game is rigged like a casino**: If a nation invites you to play by its rules, those rules are designed so you lose --- otherwise, why would they invite you?

6. **The optimal immigrant strategy is non-assimilation**: According to game theory, the winning strategy is ethnic cohesion, high fertility, and refusal to play by the host nation's rules, because demographic weight eventually lets you set the rules.

7. **Population replacement is the historical norm**: The Proto-Indo-European/Yamnaya displacement of Neolithic European farmers shows that incoming groups replacing existing elites is a recurring pattern.

8. **Modern immigration is a historical accident**: The American model of welcoming immigrants as citizens arose from specific colonial needs (displacing Native Americans, populating vast territories) and was imposed on Europe after WWII.

9. **Brain drain harms source nations**: The best and brightest leaving China (or other countries) for the US benefits America at the expense of those nations, which Jiang frames as another dimension of the rigged game.

10. **Immigration as a system is unsustainable**: As US hegemony declines and other powers rise, the immigration model will break down, leading to inter-ethnic conflict over who "sets the rules."

## Theoretical Framework

Jiang invokes "game theory" throughout the lecture, though his usage is loose and primarily metaphorical rather than formally rigorous. The key game-theoretic concepts he applies:

- **Rigged games / asymmetric rule-setting**: The idea that the entity who designs the rules of a game always has an advantage. Immigration is framed as a game where the host nation sets rules that extract value from immigrants while preventing them from reaching elite status. The casino analogy is the central metaphor.

- **Dominant strategy**: Jiang argues that non-assimilation (ethnic cohesion + high fertility) is the "dominant strategy" for immigrants, because assimilation leads to a structural ceiling on status.

- **Demographic game / evolutionary strategy**: High fertility and group cohesion are framed as a long-term strategy where the "payoff" is generational control over the rules of the game. This resembles evolutionary game theory concepts (group selection, reproductive fitness) more than classical game theory.

- **Status competition**: The lecture frames male motivation through a status-seeking lens --- men pursue status to access better mates, and immigration is evaluated through whether it improves or reduces status.

Notably absent is any formal game-theoretic modeling (payoff matrices, Nash equilibria, iterated games, or mechanism design). The "game theory" framing is more of a strategic/rational-actor heuristic applied to macro-sociological trends.

## Fact-Check & Verification

1. **Indian Americans have the highest income among ethnic groups in the US.**
   - **VERIFIED.** US Census data consistently shows Indian Americans have the highest median household income of any ethnic group in the US --- approximately $150,000+ in recent years. This is well-established.

2. **White Americans earn about $70,000/year on average.**
   - **QUESTIONABLE.** As of 2024 Census data, non-Hispanic White median household income is approximately $92,530. The $70,000 figure is significantly outdated or incorrect. Hispanic median household income ($70,950) is closer to this number. Even in earlier years, White median household income was above $70,000.

3. **PISA is administered to 14-year-olds.**
   - **FALSE.** PISA tests 15-year-olds (specifically students aged 15 years and 3 months to 16 years and 2 months), not 14-year-olds. The OECD explicitly states this. The rest of the description (every three years, math/science/reading, organized by OECD) is correct.

4. **East Asian countries top the 2018 PISA rankings.**
   - **VERIFIED.** In PISA 2018, the top positions were dominated by East Asian economies: China (B-S-J-Z provinces) ranked 1st in all three subjects, followed by Singapore, Macao, and Hong Kong. Japan, South Korea, and Chinese Taipei also ranked highly. Vietnam performed well for its income level.

5. **East Asian men are underrepresented as new CEOs.**
   - **VERIFIED.** Research by Zweigenhaft and Domhoff ("The New CEOs") and Goldman Sachs confirms the "bamboo ceiling." Asian Americans hold ~13% of professional positions but only ~6% of senior management roles. The S&P 500 CEO share has stalled at ~2%. US-born Asian American men with graduate degrees are 15% less likely to hold executive positions than comparably educated white men.

6. **OkCupid response rate data (22% for Asian men, 29% for white men when messaging Asian women).**
   - **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** OkCupid published multiple blog posts on race and dating (2009, 2014) showing Asian men received among the lowest response rates. The general pattern Jiang describes is consistent with the data, but the specific percentages (22% vs. 29%) could not be independently confirmed at this exact level. The directional claim is well-supported.

7. **White women show least interest in Asian men on dating platforms.**
   - **VERIFIED.** OkCupid's published data (2009-2014) consistently shows that Asian men received the fewest responses from white women compared to men of other racial groups. This finding has been corroborated by multiple academic studies and replicated across platforms.

8. **20% of East Asian women marry white men; only 9-10% of East Asian men marry white women.**
   - **PARTIALLY VERIFIED.** Pew Research (2017) reports 36% of Asian newlywed women vs. 21% of Asian newlywed men intermarry (all races, not just white). The gender gap is real and significant. The specific 20% and 9-10% figures for white partners specifically are roughly in the right range --- 11% of all intermarried couples are white male/Asian female vs. 4% white female/Asian male --- but Jiang's exact numbers are hard to pin to a specific source.

9. **75% of East Asian men under 25 are single.**
   - **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** Census data shows that the singlehood rate for all Asian Americans aged 18-29 is approximately 77%, which is close to Jiang's claim. However, this figure applies to the entire 18-29 age group and all genders, not specifically "East Asian men under 25." The general direction of the claim is plausible, but the specific demographic framing may be imprecise. High singlehood rates for young men in general (all races) in this age bracket are common.

10. **An Asian man needs to earn ~$300,000 to be as attractive to a white woman as a white man earning $62,000.**
    - **PARTIALLY VERIFIED.** The underlying study (Fisman et al., Columbia University speed-dating study, and/or Hitsch, Hortacsu & Ariely's online dating study) found an "income penalty" for Asian men. The commonly cited figure in academic literature is that an Asian man needs approximately $247,000 more in annual income than a white man to be equally desirable. Jiang inflates the white man's baseline to $62,000 and the Asian man's required income to $300,000 (a gap of $238,000), which is roughly consistent but not precisely stated. The claim is directionally correct but the specific numbers are loosely rendered.

11. **Muslim population projections for Europe by 2050 (Sweden X%, UK 17%, France 17%, Germany 11%, Italy 12%).**
    - **PARTIALLY VERIFIED.** Pew Research Center (2017) published three scenarios. Under the HIGH migration scenario: Sweden 30.6%, UK 17.2%, France 18%, Germany 19.7%, Italy 14.1%. Under the MEDIUM scenario, figures are lower. Jiang's UK and France figures roughly match the high-migration scenario, but his Germany (11%) and Italy (12%) figures are LOWER than even the medium scenario projections. Sweden's percentage was not stated in the transcript but is far higher (30.6%) in the high scenario. The numbers are partially correct but inconsistently drawn from different scenarios or inaccurately recalled.

12. **By 2050, whites will no longer be the US majority; Hispanic population is the main growth driver.**
    - **VERIFIED.** US Census Bureau projects the US will become "minority white" around 2045. The Hispanic population is projected to grow from ~15% to ~30% by 2050. These are well-established demographic projections.

13. **Proto-Indo-European steppe pastoralists displaced Neolithic European farmers; genetic studies confirm male replacement.**
    - **VERIFIED.** Ancient DNA studies (Haak et al. 2015, Allentoft et al. 2015, and many subsequent papers) confirm massive Yamnaya-related ancestry in Bronze Age Europeans. The Corded Ware people traced ~75% of ancestry to steppe populations. Y-chromosome evidence does suggest significant male-lineage replacement, though the process was likely more gradual and complex than a simple "kill all men, take all women" narrative suggests. The broad claim is supported; the characterization is somewhat simplified.

14. **The US welcomed immigrants specifically to displace Native Americans.**
    - **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** While westward expansion and settler colonialism certainly displaced Native Americans, the motivation for immigration policy was multifaceted --- labor needs, economic development, Manifest Destiny ideology, and population growth. Framing it solely as a strategy to "displace the natives" is reductive, though it was certainly one consequence and, arguably, one implicit function of the policy.

15. **Post-WWII, America told Europe to become open societies and welcome immigrants.**
    - **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** The US promoted liberal democratic values in post-WWII Europe (Marshall Plan, NATO, occupation governance), but the specific claim that America dictated European immigration policy is an oversimplification. European immigration policies developed for their own economic reasons (labor shortages, guest worker programs in the 1960s-70s). The framing is suggestive but not historically precise.

16. **Jiang Xueqin attended Yale.**
    - **VERIFIED.** Jiang Xueqin graduated with distinction from Yale College in 1999 with a degree in English literature. This is confirmed by multiple biographical sources including Wikipedia and institutional profiles.

## Controversial or Potentially Misleading Claims

1. **"According to game theory, the best strategy for immigrants is not to assimilate."** This is the lecture's central thesis, but it is NOT a standard result from formal game theory. No specific game-theoretic model is cited. The argument conflates demographic trends with strategic optimality. It ignores that non-assimilation carries enormous individual costs (poverty, marginalization, lack of social mobility) even if it hypothetically benefits the group over multiple generations. The framing treats ethnic groups as unitary rational actors, which is a major analytical error.

2. **Treating ethnic groups as monolithic strategic agents.** Jiang speaks of "Hispanics," "Muslims," "East Asian men," and "whites" as though each group coordinates strategy collectively. In reality, individual immigrants make individual decisions based on personal circumstances, not collective game-theoretic strategy. A Mexican immigrant choosing not to pursue higher education is not executing a "dominant strategy" --- they may face structural barriers (language, documentation status, poverty, discrimination) that limit options.

3. **"The game is rigged like a casino."** While structural racism and the bamboo ceiling are real, the casino analogy implies intentional, systematic design to exploit immigrants. Immigration policy is shaped by many competing interests, not a single game-designer. The analogy obscures the genuine opportunities immigration does provide to many individuals.

4. **Framing Muslim non-integration as strategic success.** Jiang presents Muslim communities in Europe as successfully executing a demographic-replacement strategy. This frames complex socioeconomic challenges (discrimination, lack of integration support, economic marginalization) as deliberate strategy, which is misleading. It also echoes "Great Replacement" conspiracy theory language, a framework associated with white nationalist movements.

5. **"Muslims will control Europe in 25 years."** Even under the most aggressive Pew Research projection (high migration scenario), Muslims would constitute ~14% of Europe's population by 2050 --- far from a majority or "control." This claim dramatically overstates the demographic projections.

6. **Sweeping generalizations about East Asian male personality traits.** Jiang presents stereotypes (cautious, not articulate, individualistic, obedient) as established facts about East Asian men, without qualifying these as stereotypes or noting the enormous variation within any ethnic group.

7. **Conflating correlation with causation throughout.** Income disparities, PISA scores, dating preferences, and CEO representation are presented as causally linked through a single game-theoretic framework, when each has distinct and complex causal factors.

8. **"Immigration is not natural."** Jiang claims it is natural for people to stay in their birth community and unnatural to migrate. This contradicts the deep history of human migration and mobility. Homo sapiens is a species defined by migration. The claim serves his rhetorical purpose but lacks anthropological support.

9. **Selective use of Pew/Census projections.** Jiang mixes figures from different projection scenarios (high migration, medium migration) without noting that these represent different assumptions. He presents the most dramatic numbers as foregone conclusions.

## Original vs. Cited Ideas

**Ideas Jiang draws from other sources:**
- OkCupid dating data (Christian Rudder / OkTrends blog posts, 2009-2014)
- Columbia University speed-dating / online dating income study (likely Fisman, Iyengar, Kamenica, Simonson, and/or Hitsch, Hortacsu, Ariely)
- PISA 2018 rankings (OECD)
- US Census income data by ethnicity
- Pew Research Center projections on Muslim population in Europe and Hispanic population in the US
- Zweigenhaft and Domhoff's research on CEO diversity ("The New CEOs")
- Ancient DNA studies on Yamnaya/Proto-Indo-European migrations (Haak, Allentoft, Lazaridis, Reich et al.)
- General knowledge of British colonial history and post-WWII geopolitics

**Jiang's original framing and synthesis:**
- The "immigration trap" as a game-theoretic concept --- the idea that assimilation is a losing strategy because the host nation's rules are structurally designed for immigrant loss. This synthesis is Jiang's own.
- The casino analogy applied to immigration.
- The argument that non-assimilation + high fertility constitutes a "dominant strategy" from game theory. No game-theoretic literature makes this specific argument in this form.
- Linking Proto-Indo-European population replacement to modern immigration dynamics as evidence of a universal historical pattern.
- His personal narrative as a Chinese-Canadian Yale graduate who chose China over the US as a "status optimization" decision.
- The framing of post-WWII American hegemony as the origin of modern immigration norms, with immigration as a tool of American resource extraction (including "talent extraction").

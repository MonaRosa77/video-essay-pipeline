# Analysis: Game Theory #7 — America's Game

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ijnkCt1QK6k
- **Channel**: Predictive History (Prof. Jiang Xueqin)
- **Series**: Game Theory #7

## Summary

This lecture picks up where Game Theory #6 (on the British Empire) left off. Jiang identifies three structural limitations of the British Empire — its ethnic/racial identity, the gold standard as a ceiling on wealth, and its small geographic size — and frames America's rise as the resolution of all three. He argues that the American founding fathers consciously designed the nation as a "game" rather than an ethnic or territorial entity, one characterized by openness (the melting pot), fairness (meritocracy), and clarity (rule of law via the Constitution). This game-theoretic framing is the central conceit of the lecture and the broader series.

The lecture then traces how America scaled this domestic game to the entire world through the post-WWII institutional architecture: the Bretton Woods system (pegging currencies to the dollar, which was pegged to gold), the World Bank and IMF (to spread the game to developing countries), GATT/WTO (free trade), SWIFT (interbank transfers), and the Bank for International Settlements (central bank coordination). Jiang presents these institutions not as neutral international cooperation but as deliberate instruments of American hegemony designed to make the world dependent on the US dollar.

The narrative then tracks the system through its crises. Nixon's 1971 delinking of the dollar from gold is cast as transforming the dollar into a "Ponzi scheme" sustained by manufactured demand — first through the petrodollar arrangement with Saudi Arabia, then through an extraordinarily generous deal with China (market access, FDI, technology transfer, education, military protection) all in exchange for China using US dollars. The Soviet collapse in 1991 allowed America to impose a "new world order" with a global price hierarchy (resources at the bottom, finance at the top), assigning roles to different regions: Russia/Africa/South America for resources, China for manufacturing, Europe for knowledge, and America for finance.

The lecture concludes with the system's current instability: the 2008 financial crisis (which Jiang argues was resolved by China's massive infrastructure spending), China's subsequent demand for equal status, the US-China trade war under Trump (2016 onward), and Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine as a challenge to the resource layer of the hierarchy. Jiang frames the present moment as a "game reset" — the American-designed global game is under existential threat from both China and Russia.

Throughout the lecture, Jiang also provides a mini-survey of political philosophy — Locke's social contract (life, liberty, property), Rousseau's general will, Kant's categorical imperative, and the Prussian blood-and-land conception of nationhood — as background for understanding why America's "nation as game" was a novel departure.

## Key Arguments

1. **America resolved the three structural limitations of the British Empire**: racial/ethnic identity (replaced with the melting pot), the gold standard (replaced with the fiat US dollar), and small geographic size (America controls the Western Hemisphere with vast resources and population).

2. **The American founding fathers designed the nation as a "game" rather than an ethnic or territorial entity**, characterized by openness, fairness (meritocracy), and clarity (rule of law through the Constitution).

3. **The American game is inherently expansionist**: the Civil War was a contest between the game (free labor in the North) and an incompatible system (slave labor in the South); the game won and kept expanding.

4. **Capitalism's endgame problem**: when the frontier was settled and expansion stopped, wealth concentrated in fewer hands, leading to the Great Depression — which forced America to expand the game globally via WWII.

5. **The post-WWII Bretton Woods architecture was designed to scale America's domestic game worldwide**: the dollar as reserve currency, the World Bank/IMF to spread the game, GATT/WTO for free trade, SWIFT for dollar-denominated transfers, and the BIS for central bank coordination.

6. **Nixon's 1971 delinking of gold turned the dollar into a "Ponzi scheme"** sustained by manufactured demand — first through the petrodollar deal with Saudi Arabia, then through the strategic empowerment of China.

7. **America deliberately made China rich** — providing market access, FDI, technology, education, and military protection — for the sole purpose of making China dependent on and a user of US dollars.

8. **After the Soviet collapse (1991), America imposed a global price hierarchy**: resources (Russia, Africa, South America) at the bottom; manufacturing (China) above; knowledge (Europe) above that; finance (America) at the top.

9. **The 2008 financial crisis was caused by America's over-financialization** and was only saved by China's massive infrastructure spending, after which China demanded equal status — triggering the US-China trade war under Trump.

10. **Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine is a challenge to the resource layer** of the American-designed price hierarchy; the current moment is a "game reset" with both China and Russia challenging the system.

## Theoretical Framework

Jiang applies several game-theoretic and political-economic concepts, though loosely rather than formally:

- **Nation as Game**: The central metaphor. America is not a land, ethnicity, or social contract in the traditional sense — it is a competitive game with rules (the Constitution), players (citizens), and a goal (wealth maximization). This is Jiang's original synthesis, drawing on but departing from standard political theory.

- **Game Design Principles**: Openness (anyone can play), fairness (merit determines outcomes), and clarity (clear rules via rule of law). These map loosely onto mechanism design theory — the idea that institutional rules shape player behavior and outcomes.

- **Expansionist Dynamics**: The game is framed as inherently expansionist because capitalism concentrates wealth over time, requiring new players and new markets to prevent collapse. This echoes world-systems theory (Wallerstein) and theories of imperialism (Lenin, Luxemburg), though Jiang does not cite these.

- **Price Hierarchy / Division of Labor**: The global allocation of roles (resources, manufacturing, knowledge, finance) echoes dependency theory and world-systems theory's core-periphery model, with America as the core extracting value from the periphery.

- **Ponzi Scheme Dynamics**: The post-1971 dollar is described as valuable only because others believe it is valuable — a self-referential system requiring constant expansion of demand. This resembles Minsky's financial instability hypothesis and Kindleberger's work on manias.

- **Social Contract Theory**: Jiang surveys Locke (rights-based contract) and Rousseau (general will) as predecessors to the American model, plus the Prussian blood-and-soil nationalism as a contrast.

- **Categorical Imperative**: Kant's moral framework is introduced as the theoretical foundation for Rousseau's general will, with three components: universality, free will, and human beings as ends in themselves.

## Fact-Check & Verification

1. **Britain triumphed over rivals due to Bank of England, soft power, navy**: VERIFIED in broad strokes. This is a standard interpretation in imperial history (Niall Ferguson, *Empire*; John Darwin, *After Tamerlane*), though it simplifies a complex multi-century process.

2. **Bank of England absorbed foreign capital, protected by law**: VERIFIED. The Bank of England (est. 1694) and English property rights were key to attracting Dutch and other foreign capital. Well-documented in economic history.

3. **British soft power still rules the world today**: NEEDS VERIFICATION. This is a defensible but subjective claim. Britain's cultural influence remains significant but "rules the world" is hyperbolic given the dominance of American soft power since WWII.

4. **British navy controlled sea lanes for 100 years**: VERIFIED. *Pax Britannica* (roughly 1815-1914) is the standard periodization of British naval dominance. The "100 years" figure is approximately correct.

5. **Brexit as British provincialism**: VERIFIED as event (June 23, 2016 referendum). The interpretation that Brexit reflects ethnic insularity is one perspective among many; it is a legitimate but contested analytical framing.

6. **English people claimed their brains are hardwired for English only**: This is personal anecdote, not a factual claim to verify.

7. **Gold limited participation in the economic game**: VERIFIED as a general characterization of the constraints of the gold standard, though this is a simplification.

8. **Locke: life, liberty, and property**: VERIFIED. These are indeed Locke's three natural rights as articulated in *Two Treatises of Government* (1689). However, Jiang characterizes the third as "the pursuit of property wealth" — Locke actually says "property," not "pursuit of property." The phrase "pursuit of happiness" comes from Jefferson's adaptation in the Declaration of Independence, not Locke. Also, Jiang says these are rights "that God gave us" — Locke does ground them in natural law/God, so this is approximately correct.

9. **Rousseau's general will as substitute for God**: NEEDS VERIFICATION. This is an interpretive claim. Rousseau's general will (*The Social Contract*, 1762) is the collective will of the body politic, not explicitly framed as a "substitute for God." This is Jiang's interpretive gloss, which has some support in scholarship (e.g., the "civil religion" chapter of *The Social Contract*) but is not Rousseau's own framing.

10. **Kant's categorical imperative has three components: universality, free will, and humans as ends in themselves**: QUESTIONABLE. Kant's three formulations of the categorical imperative (from *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*, 1785) are: (1) the Formula of Universal Law (act only according to maxims you could will as universal law), (2) the Formula of Humanity (treat humanity always as an end, never merely as a means), and (3) the Formula of the Kingdom of Ends (act as a legislating member in a kingdom of ends). Jiang's "free will" is not one of the standard formulations — it conflates Kant's concept of moral autonomy with a general notion of free will. His description of universality and humans-as-ends is roughly correct, but the tripartite structure he presents does not match Kant's actual three formulations.

11. **French Revolution: France defeated coalitions from the entire continent**: VERIFIED. The French Revolutionary Wars (1792-1802) saw France fight and largely defeat coalitions including Britain, Austria, Prussia, Spain, the Netherlands, and others. The claim that "people were energized to fight for their nation" aligns with the standard *levee en masse* interpretation.

12. **Prussian/German nationhood based on "iron and blood"**: VERIFIED. "Blood and Iron" (*Blut und Eisen*) comes from Bismarck's 1862 speech to the Prussian Diet. Jiang's characterization of German nationalism as based on language, heritage, and land aligns with the *Volk* tradition (Herder, Fichte).

13. **1870 Franco-Prussian War founded modern Germany**: VERIFIED. The Franco-Prussian War (1870-1871) led to Prussian victory and the proclamation of the German Empire on January 18, 1871 at Versailles. However, Jiang calls it the "Franco-Russian war" at one point (likely a slip of the tongue in lecture — the transcript reads "FrancoRussian") — the correct name is the Franco-Prussian War.

14. **Around the 1700s, America had more people and was wealthier than Britain**: QUESTIONABLE. By the mid-1700s, the American colonies had roughly 1.5-2 million people versus Britain's approximately 6-7 million. America did not surpass Britain in population until well into the 19th century. The colonies were growing rapidly and were wealthy per capita, but the claim that America was "wealthier" than Britain in the 1700s is misleading.

15. **In 1789, America created the Constitution**: QUESTIONABLE. The Constitution was drafted in 1787 (Philadelphia Convention) and ratified on June 21, 1788, when New Hampshire became the ninth state to ratify. The new government under the Constitution began operating on March 4, 1789. So "1789" is defensible as the year the Constitution took effect, but "created" is imprecise — it was created in 1787 and ratified in 1788.

16. **Three branches of US government**: VERIFIED. Standard civics — the legislative (Congress), executive (President), and judicial (Supreme Court) branches are established in Articles I, II, and III of the Constitution.

17. **Civil War dates: 1861-1864**: FALSE. The American Civil War lasted from April 12, 1861 to April 9, 1865 (Lee's surrender at Appomattox) or May-June 1865 (final Confederate surrenders). Jiang says "1861 to 1864," which is off by a year.

18. **Great Depression resulted from wealth concentration after the frontier was settled**: NEEDS VERIFICATION. This loosely echoes the Turner Frontier Thesis (Frederick Jackson Turner, 1893) combined with underconsumption theories of the Depression. There is scholarly support for the view that wealth inequality contributed to the Great Depression, but the causal chain Jiang draws (frontier closed -> concentration -> Depression) is an oversimplification. The Depression's causes are multi-factorial (monetary policy failures, banking panics, Smoot-Hawley tariffs, etc.).

19. **Bretton Woods conference: July 1944, New Hampshire, established dollar as reserve currency pegged to gold**: VERIFIED. The United Nations Monetary and Financial Conference was held July 1-22, 1944 at the Mount Washington Hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire. 44 nations participated. The dollar was pegged to gold at $35/ounce, and other currencies were pegged to the dollar.

20. **Bretton Woods created the World Bank and IMF**: VERIFIED. The International Monetary Fund and the International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (later the World Bank Group) were both established by the Bretton Woods agreements.

21. **GATT created in 1947, became WTO in 1995**: VERIFIED. GATT was signed October 30, 1947 and took effect January 1, 1948. The WTO replaced GATT on January 1, 1995.

22. **SWIFT established in 1973**: VERIFIED. SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication) was founded on May 3, 1973, in Brussels, Belgium, initially supported by 239 banks in 15 countries. However, Jiang describes it as "an agreement among banks on how to transfer money" — SWIFT is actually a cooperative organization and messaging network, not merely an agreement. It went live in 1977.

23. **BIS operates privately and secretly**: QUESTIONABLE. The Bank for International Settlements (est. 1930, Basel, Switzerland) does serve as a forum for central bank coordination, and its meetings are not public. However, characterizing it as operating "in secret" with "no one knows about them" is an exaggeration — the BIS publishes annual reports, research papers, and its existence and role are well-documented. This framing leans toward conspiratorial.

24. **America overspent on Vietnam, space race, Great Society; France withdrew gold**: VERIFIED. The US fiscal deficits from Vietnam War spending, Great Society programs, and the space race did strain the Bretton Woods system. In 1965, President de Gaulle announced France's intention to exchange US dollar reserves for gold and sent the French Navy to repatriate gold. This is well-documented.

25. **Nixon ended dollar-gold convertibility in 1971**: VERIFIED. On August 15, 1971, Nixon announced the suspension of dollar convertibility to gold (the "Nixon Shock").

26. **Nixon created the petrodollar with Saudi Arabia as the "largest oil exporter"**: NEEDS VERIFICATION. The petrodollar arrangement was negotiated in 1974 (by Kissinger and Prince Fahd, not Nixon directly), not immediately by Nixon after the 1971 shock. The claim that Saudi Arabia was the "largest oil exporter in the world" was approximately true in the 1970s. However, there was no single formal "petrodollar agreement" — the arrangement emerged from the 1974 Joint Commission on Economic Cooperation. By 1975, all OPEC countries were trading oil in US dollars.

27. **Nixon visited China and established a framework for massive US concessions in the 1980s**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. Nixon visited China February 21-28, 1972. However, the specific concessions Jiang describes (market access, FDI, technology transfer, education, military protection) developed over subsequent decades under multiple administrations, not as a single framework established by Nixon. The normalization of US-China relations under Carter (1979) and Deng Xiaoping's reforms created the conditions Jiang describes. The claim that "America gives all this technology to China" and "all these military secrets... for free" is a significant exaggeration of the actual technology transfer that occurred.

28. **All US concessions to China were solely to make China use US dollars**: QUESTIONABLE. This is a monocausal explanation for a complex geopolitical relationship. US engagement with China was motivated by multiple factors: Cold War strategy against the Soviet Union, opening new markets, promoting democratization (at least rhetorically), and yes, integrating China into the dollar-denominated global economy. Reducing it to "one answer and only one answer" oversimplifies dramatically.

29. **Soviet Union collapsed in 1991**: VERIFIED. The Soviet Union officially dissolved on December 26, 1991.

30. **Post-1991 global price hierarchy**: This is Jiang's original analytical framework, not a factual claim to verify per se. It is a defensible schematic of the post-Cold War global division of labor, though it oversimplifies the roles of each region.

31. **2008 crisis resulted from speculative finance**: VERIFIED in broad terms. The 2008 Global Financial Crisis was triggered by speculation in mortgage-backed securities and derivatives. Characterizing the US economy as focused on "gambling" is colorful but directionally correct.

32. **Central banks met at BIS in Basel and decided China must spend to save the economy**: NEEDS VERIFICATION. There were extensive G20 and international coordination efforts during the 2008-2009 crisis. The BIS was involved in discussions, but the claim that central banks specifically met at the BIS and "decided China must spend money" oversimplifies the decision-making process. China's 4 trillion yuan ($586 billion) stimulus was announced by China's State Council on November 9, 2008 — it was a sovereign Chinese decision, not simply an order from the BIS.

33. **China's infrastructure spending saved the global economy**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. China's massive 4 trillion yuan stimulus (November 2008) did play a significant role in stabilizing the global economy, particularly through demand for commodities. However, US TARP, European Central Bank interventions, and coordinated G20 stimulus also played major roles. The Belt and Road Initiative (2013) came later and was not part of the 2008 crisis response.

34. **China demanded equal status, leading to Trump's trade war (2016)**: VERIFIED in broad outline. China's growing assertiveness (e.g., "Made in China 2025," South China Sea expansion, Huawei's global expansion) did contribute to US-China tensions. Trump launched tariffs against China in 2018, escalating into a trade war. The characterization is simplified but directionally correct.

35. **Russia and Ukraine control one-third of the world's "carbohydrates" (grain)**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. Jiang uses "carbohydrates" when he presumably means grain/cereals. Russia and Ukraine together account for approximately 28-30% of global wheat exports, and about 20% of corn exports. The "one-third" figure is approximately correct for wheat exports. The claim that "Africa, the Middle East starves to death without Russian and Ukrainian grain" is hyperbolic but directionally true — many developing countries in the Middle East and Africa are heavily dependent on Black Sea wheat.

36. **Russia invaded Ukraine to control resources and challenge the price hierarchy**: NEEDS VERIFICATION. Ukraine's agricultural and mineral resources are one factor among many in Russia's invasion. Geopolitical factors (NATO expansion, Russian national security concerns, historical/cultural claims) are generally considered more central by most analysts. Jiang's resource-focused interpretation is one lens among several.

## Controversial or Potentially Misleading Claims

1. **"America gave China everything... all these military secrets... for free"**: This is a significant exaggeration. While the US did provide substantial technology transfer and military cooperation (especially in the 1980s as a counter to the Soviet Union), the characterization that America gave China "all its military secrets for free" is hyperbolic and misleading. There were always restrictions, and the relationship was strategic rather than philanthropic.

2. **"The only purpose was to make China use US dollars"**: This monocausal explanation ignores Cold War geopolitics (the Sino-Soviet split made China a strategic asset against the USSR), ideological goals (promoting market liberalization and, ostensibly, democratization), and commercial interests (US corporations wanted access to the Chinese market). Reducing it to dollar hegemony alone is reductive.

3. **"The US dollar becomes a Ponzi scheme"**: While the fiat dollar does rely on confidence and demand (as all fiat currencies do), calling it a "Ponzi scheme" implies fraud and inevitable collapse. This is a polemical characterization, not a neutral economic description. A Ponzi scheme pays existing investors with new investors' money; the dollar system, while dependent on continued demand, also provides genuine utility (liquidity, store of value, unit of account).

4. **"The BIS operates in secret, no one knows about them"**: This leans toward conspiracy-theory framing. The BIS is a well-known institution that publishes extensive research and annual reports. Its meetings are not public, but this is true of many central banking forums.

5. **The claim that America controls "the entire Western Hemisphere" and has "unlimited population, unlimited resources, unlimited land"**: This elides the sovereignty of every other country in the Western Hemisphere. While the Monroe Doctrine established US influence, characterizing the entire hemisphere as essentially belonging to America is a major oversimplification.

6. **"In America, if you work really hard, if you're really talented... they will respect that"**: While meritocracy is a real aspect of American culture, presenting it uncritically ignores extensive literature on structural inequality, racial discrimination, and the limitations of the American Dream (e.g., Raj Chetty's research on declining social mobility).

7. **The Civil War as purely a contest between "the game" (free labor) and slavery**: This oversimplifies the complex causes of the Civil War, including states' rights debates, tariff disputes, and territorial expansion politics, reducing them to Jiang's game-theory framework.

8. **The Great Depression caused by wealth concentration after the frontier closed**: This collapses multiple complex historical processes (the Gilded Age, WWI, the Roaring Twenties speculative bubble, monetary policy failures) into a simplistic frontier-thesis narrative.

9. **"Nowhere else is merit appreciated except America and England"**: This sweeping generalization dismisses meritocratic traditions in many other countries and cultures. It also contradicts Jiang's earlier point about systemic racism in America.

## Original vs. Cited Ideas

**Jiang's Original Framing:**
- The concept of "nation as game" — America as a game with players (citizens) seeking to maximize wealth, with game design principles (openness, fairness, clarity). This is Jiang's central original metaphor for the series.
- The "price hierarchy" framework (resources < manufacturing < knowledge < finance) as the organizing principle of the post-Cold War world order.
- The framing of the US dollar as a "Ponzi scheme" sustained by manufactured demand.
- The interpretation that ALL US engagement with China was motivated solely by dollar hegemony.
- The characterization of the current moment as a "game reset."
- The comparison of three models of nationhood (French social contract, Prussian blood-and-land, American game) as competing "game designs."

**Ideas Drawn from Other Thinkers (often without explicit citation):**
- **John Locke**: Social contract theory, natural rights (life, liberty, property) — cited by name.
- **Jean-Jacques Rousseau**: The general will, social contract — cited by name.
- **Immanuel Kant**: The categorical imperative — cited by name (as "Emilion Kant" in the auto-generated transcript, likely "Immanuel Kant").
- **Bismarck**: "Iron and blood" conception of nationalism — implied but not cited by name; attributed to "the Prussians or the Germans."
- **Frederick Jackson Turner**: The frontier thesis (that the closing of the American frontier created internal pressures) — not cited but clearly influences the argument.
- **World-Systems Theory (Immanuel Wallerstein)**: The core-periphery model and global division of labor — not cited but clearly influences the price hierarchy framework.
- **Dependency Theory**: The idea that international financial institutions (World Bank, IMF) serve to integrate developing countries into a system that benefits the core — not cited but clearly echoed.
- **Joseph Nye**: The concept of "soft power" — used without attribution (Nye coined the term in 1990).
- **Standard International Relations scholarship**: Bretton Woods institutions, petrodollar, Nixon shock — presented as narrative rather than attributed to specific scholars.

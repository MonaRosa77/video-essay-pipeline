# Analysis: Game Theory #8 — Communist Specter

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=axqDLhWs93Q
- **Channel**: Predictive History (Prof. Jiang Xueqin)
- **Series**: Game Theory #8

## Summary

In this lecture, Prof. Jiang Xueqin presents a provocative reinterpretation of the relationship between capitalism and communism. Rather than treating them as polar opposites (a "false dialectic"), he argues that communism functioned as a tool — even a deliberate creation — of capitalist and aristocratic elites to undermine the real threats to their power: monarchy, theocracy, nationalism, and democracy/socialism. His central claim is that communism served to discredit and fracture the natural coalition of bourgeoisie, middle class, and workers that threatened the old order, particularly after the 1848 revolutions across Europe.

Jiang traces this argument through the biography of Karl Marx (his residence in Britain, his funding by Engels's industrialist family, his radicalization of socialism into communism), the 1848 revolutions that terrified the European nobility, and the Bolshevik Revolution of 1917. He argues the Bolsheviks were financed by Wall Street, paid foreign mercenaries with looted Russian wealth, and that Allied intervention served capitalist debt-collection interests rather than genuine anti-communist goals. He points to the execution of the Romanov family as motivated by seizing their foreign bank deposits.

The lecture positions China as the ultimate proof of this thesis: communism, via the Cultural Revolution, destroyed the traditional social structures (religion, tradition, cultural identity) that would have resisted capitalism, and China then transitioned seamlessly to hyper-capitalism. Jiang frames Mao as primarily a peasant military leader who used communism as a facade for national sovereignty and industrialization, rather than as a genuine ideological communist.

Throughout, Jiang repeatedly caveats that these are speculative frameworks for thinking, not established truths. He draws on game theory reasoning (cui bono analysis, strategic alliance-building, divide-and-conquer logic) rather than conventional Marxist or liberal historiography. The lecture ends by previewing the series' shift toward applying these historical frameworks to current events.

## Key Arguments

1. **The capitalism-communism dialectic is false**: Communism and capitalism are more similar than different; communism is better understood as a weapon of capitalism rather than its opposite.

2. **Communism's four shared enemies with capitalism**: Both oppose monarchy (redistribution by kings), theocracy (religion denouncing money), nationalism (barriers to capital flow), and democracy/socialism (workers voting to redistribute wealth).

3. **Communism was engineered to destroy socialism**: By radicalizing the moderate demand for redistribution (socialism) into the extreme demand for abolition of property (communism), the ruling class discredited the entire reform movement.

4. **The 1848 revolutions forced the elite's hand**: The bourgeoisie-middle class-worker coalition threatened to overthrow the old order; communism was deployed to divide this coalition by redefining the primary conflict as class war between bourgeoisie and proletariat.

5. **Marx was a tool of British/capitalist interests**: His residence in Britain, his funding by Engels's capitalist family, and his focus on fomenting revolution in Germany (Britain's rival) all suggest he served British strategic interests.

6. **The Bolshevik Revolution was financed by Wall Street**: Western capitalists funded the Bolsheviks to destroy Russia as an economic competitor and loot its wealth.

7. **The Romanovs were killed for their foreign bank deposits**: Their assassination ensured billions in foreign banks could never be reclaimed.

8. **China proves the thesis**: Communism destroyed the traditional structures that resisted capitalism, after which China became hyper-capitalist — demonstrating communism's function as capitalism's advance guard.

9. **Social democracy, not communism, is capitalism's real enemy**: The natural evolution of industrialized societies is toward social democracy (redistribution), which genuinely threatens elite power.

## Theoretical Framework

Jiang applies several game theory concepts, though often implicitly rather than formally:

- **Cui bono (who benefits) analysis**: Repeatedly asks who benefits from communism's actions — and concludes it is capitalists and the old aristocratic order, not workers.

- **Divide-and-conquer strategy**: The core thesis — that the elite used communism to split the bourgeoisie-middle class-worker coalition that emerged after industrialization.

- **False flag / controlled opposition**: Communism as a deliberately extreme version of socialism, designed to discredit the broader reform movement from within.

- **Alliance theory**: The lecture maps shifting coalitions — old order vs. new order, then the fracturing of the new order through the injection of communism.

- **Revealed preferences vs. stated preferences**: Jiang contrasts what communist ideology claims to want (worker liberation) with what it actually achieves (destruction of religion, tradition, and national identity — clearing the path for capitalism).

- **Strategic misdirection**: The Communist Manifesto is framed as a "secret document" that conveniently reveals a world conspiracy, giving authorities justification to crack down on all reform movements.

## Fact-Check & Verification

1. **China's "seamless" transition**: QUESTIONABLE. While China avoided a Soviet-style collapse, the transition was far from conflict-free. The Tiananmen Square protests of 1989 were a direct consequence of the political tensions created by economic reform without political reform. The characterization as "seamless" significantly understates social disruption.

2. **Violence of feudalism-to-capitalism and Catholic-to-Protestant transitions**: VERIFIED. The English Civil War, French Revolution, and Wars of Religion (including the Thirty Years' War) support this general claim.

3. **Kings canceling debts upon taking power**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. This practice is well-documented in the ancient Near East (Mesopotamian "clean slate" edicts), as documented by economic historian Michael Hudson. However, it was not a universal practice of all monarchies throughout history, making the generalization overstated.

4. **All religions teach money is evil; Catholic Europe was poor**: QUESTIONABLE. This is a vast oversimplification. Catholic Europe was not uniformly poor — Italian city-states and the medieval Champagne fairs were major commercial centers. The relationship between religion and economic activity is far more complex than stated. Max Weber's Protestant ethic thesis, which Jiang seems to echo, has itself been extensively critiqued.

5. **Marx in Britain and the British Museum**: VERIFIED. Marx lived in London from 1849 until his death in 1883 and famously worked in the Reading Room of the British Museum (seat G7), where he researched and wrote *Das Kapital*.

6. **Jenny von Westphalen as a "German aristocrat"**: VERIFIED. Jenny von Westphalen (1814-1881) was from a recently ennobled Prussian family. Her father, Ludwig von Westphalen, was a government official with noble status. "Aristocrat" is slightly generous — she was from the petty nobility (Kleinadel), not the high aristocracy.

7. **Marx never made money but lived middle class**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. Marx was chronically in financial difficulty and dependent on Engels. However, the family's lifestyle varied considerably — they experienced genuine poverty at times (three of their children died in infancy, partly due to poor living conditions in Soho), though Engels's support eventually provided more stability. "Middle class" with children in "private school" somewhat overstates their consistent standard of living.

8. **Engels's father was an industrialist who funded Marx through Engels**: VERIFIED. Friedrich Engels Sr. owned textile factories in Barmen, Germany, and was a partner in the Ermen & Engels cotton mill in Manchester, England. Friedrich Engels Jr. worked in the Manchester factory and used proceeds to support Marx financially for decades.

9. **Communist Manifesto written "after the 1848 revolutions"**: FALSE. The Communist Manifesto was published in late February 1848, just days *before* the French Revolution of February 24, 1848 erupted. It was written in January 1848 and sent to the printer in London weeks before the revolutions began. Jiang gets the causal order reversed — the Manifesto preceded the revolutions, not the other way around. This is a significant factual error that undermines part of his narrative framing.

10. **England spared 1848 revolutions because of colonies/emigration**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. Emigration to colonies did function as a "safety valve," and this is a recognized historical argument. However, historians identify multiple additional factors: prior Reform Acts (1832), the Chartist movement's non-violent character, earlier industrialization giving Britain a more developed middle class, and more flexible political institutions. The colonies-only explanation is reductive.

11. **Seven Napoleonic Wars and "tens of millions" of deaths**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. There were indeed seven Coalition Wars (1792-1815). However, total military and civilian deaths from the French Revolutionary and Napoleonic Wars combined are estimated at 3-6 million, not "tens of millions." The claim of "tens of millions" is a significant exaggeration.

12. **Concert of Europe created after Napoleon's defeat**: VERIFIED. The Concert of Europe emerged from the Congress of Vienna (1814-1815) and functioned as a system of great-power diplomacy to maintain the post-Napoleonic order.

13. **Bolsheviks received 23% in the Constituent Assembly election; socialists got 17 million votes to Bolsheviks' 10 million**: APPROXIMATELY VERIFIED. In the November 1917 election, the Bolsheviks received approximately 24.9% of the vote (about 10 million votes). The Socialist Revolutionaries received approximately 40-45% (about 17 million votes). Jiang's figures are broadly accurate.

14. **Romanovs in power for hundreds of years**: VERIFIED. The Romanov dynasty ruled Russia from 1613 to 1917, a span of approximately 304 years.

15. **17-18 Romanov family members assassinated**: APPROXIMATELY VERIFIED. The immediate Romanov family (7 people) plus 4 servants were killed at Yekaterinburg on July 16-17, 1918. Over the subsequent months, 14 additional Romanov relatives and 13 members of their entourage were killed. The number of actual Romanov family members killed was approximately 18-21, depending on how one counts extended family, which aligns with Jiang's claim.

16. **Romanovs had "billions and billions" in foreign banks**: NEEDS VERIFICATION / QUESTIONABLE. While the Romanovs were extraordinarily wealthy and did have foreign bank deposits, the claim of "billions" (in contemporary currency terms) is debated. Grand Duke Alexander Mikhailovich claimed approximately 20 million pounds was invested in London merchant banks. Historians like William Clarke (*The Lost Fortune of the Tsars*) have investigated these claims extensively, and there is significant debate about the actual amounts. Nicholas II reportedly closed his English accounts during WWI and repatriated funds. The claim as stated — billions sitting unclaimed that banks simply seized — is a popular legend that lacks solid historical documentation.

17. **200,000 foreign mercenaries fighting for the Bolsheviks**: APPROXIMATELY VERIFIED. Historical sources confirm tens of thousands of Latvian, Chinese, and Hungarian fighters served with the Red Army. The Latvian Riflemen were especially important. Estimates of Chinese fighters range from tens of thousands, with up to 200,000 Chinese living in Russia at the time (though not all fought). The 200,000 figure may conflate total foreign residents with active combatants. Also, calling them "mercenaries" is misleading — many (especially the Latvians) were ideologically committed, and many Chinese were already living in Russia and joined for various reasons including survival.

18. **Wall Street and City of London financed Bolshevik mercenaries**: NEEDS VERIFICATION / QUESTIONABLE. This claim derives primarily from the work of Antony Sutton (*Wall Street and the Bolshevik Revolution*, 1974) and Richard Spence. While some financial connections between Western interests and various Russian factions existed, the mainstream historical consensus does not support the claim that Wall Street systematically financed the Bolsheviks' military forces. Sutton's work has been criticized by academic reviewers for "unsubstantiated allegations" and "unwarranted conclusions" (Virgil D. Medlin, Oklahoma City University).

19. **Red Terror as fundraising mechanism for Wall Street repayment**: NEEDS VERIFICATION / QUESTIONABLE. The Red Terror (1918-1921) is a well-documented historical event involving mass executions and property confiscation. However, framing it primarily as a mechanism to repay Wall Street loans is a specific interpretive claim from the Sutton/Spence school that is not part of mainstream historiography.

20. **$450 million in sequestered valuables by 1921**: NEEDS VERIFICATION. This figure is attributed to Richard Spence's *Wall Street and the Russian Revolution* (2017). Spence is a legitimate professor of history at the University of Idaho, but this specific claim would need verification against primary sources.

21. **Richard Spence's book**: VERIFIED. Richard B. Spence, professor at the University of Idaho, published *Wall Street and the Russian Revolution: 1905-1925* in 2017. The book is a real academic work, though it follows in the tradition of Antony Sutton's more controversial earlier work on the same topic.

22. **Allied armies went to Russia to collect debt and then attacked the whites**: QUESTIONABLE. The Allied intervention in Russia (1918-1920) had multiple stated motives: securing war supplies, rescuing Czech Legion troops, reopening the Eastern Front against Germany, and later supporting the White movement. While debt recovery was a factor (the Bolsheviks had repudiated 12 billion gold francs in debt in February 1918), mainstream historians do not characterize the intervention as primarily debt collection. The claim that the Allies "attacked the whites" is unsupported — they generally supported the White forces, though their support was inconsistent and ultimately insufficient.

23. **Marx excluded Russia and China from his theory**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. Marx's primary focus was on industrialized Western Europe, especially Germany and England. However, Marx did write about Russia later in life, particularly regarding the Russian commune (obshchina), and he corresponded with Russian revolutionaries like Vera Zasulich. The claim is a simplification but captures his general orientation.

24. **Lenin's vanguard party innovation**: VERIFIED. Lenin's contribution to Marxist theory — that a disciplined vanguard party could accelerate revolution in less-industrialized societies — is a standard historical characterization of Leninism.

25. **Mao's peasant-based revolution**: VERIFIED. Mao Zedong's adaptation of Marxism-Leninism to rely on the peasantry rather than the urban proletariat is well-documented and is a defining feature of Maoism.

26. **Mao tried to work with the United States**: PARTIALLY VERIFIED. There were contacts between the CCP and American diplomats (the "Dixie Mission" of 1944), and Mao expressed interest in a working relationship. The US ultimately backed Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalists, and the subsequent embargo did push China closer to the Soviet Union. However, the relationship was more complex than a simple rejection narrative.

27. **Sino-Soviet split in 1957**: QUESTIONABLE. While tensions began emerging around 1957-1958, the Sino-Soviet split is conventionally dated to 1960, when Khrushchev withdrew Soviet technicians and the rift became public. The 1957 date may refer to early friction, but placing the "split" there is premature by most historical accounts.

## Controversial or Potentially Misleading Claims

1. **Communism as a deliberate capitalist conspiracy**: This is the lecture's central thesis and its most controversial claim. While it is presented as speculative ("I'm not saying this is true"), the cumulative rhetorical effect is to present it as a plausible historical explanation. Mainstream historians generally explain the rise of communism through ideological development, social conditions, and political contingency — not as a deliberate conspiracy by elites. The thesis cherry-picks evidence that fits the conspiratorial frame while ignoring contradictory evidence (e.g., genuine capitalist terror of communist revolution, the Cold War arms race, Western anti-communist interventions).

2. **Communist Manifesto as a post-1848 document**: This factual error (it was published just *before* the revolutions) significantly weakens Jiang's narrative that the Manifesto was a reaction designed to co-opt the revolutionary movement. The actual timeline suggests the opposite: Marx and Engels were trying to provide a theoretical framework for a revolution they anticipated was coming.

3. **"All religions teach money is evil"**: This is a sweeping generalization that ignores prosperity theology in Christianity, Islamic finance's embrace of trade and commerce, and Judaism's complex but generally positive relationship with economic activity. It confuses specific ascetic traditions with religion as a whole.

4. **Anti-Semitic tropes**: Jiang references "Jews" in the context of communist conspiracies (around 24:11 in the transcript). While he frames this as describing what historical propagandists said, the invocation of Jewish conspiracy in the context of communism echoes the "Judeo-Bolshevism" trope — one of the most dangerous and historically consequential antisemitic conspiracy theories that contributed to the Holocaust. The framing does not adequately contextualize or critique this dangerous narrative.

5. **Equating communist party with capitalist oligarchy**: The claim that "it doesn't really matter" whether you have a party or an oligarchy elides fundamental differences in how these systems operate, their accountability structures, and their effects on populations.

6. **"Seamless" transition in China**: This dismisses the immense human cost of China's economic reforms, including mass unemployment from state-owned enterprise closures, rural-urban inequality, and the suppression of political dissent (Tiananmen 1989).

7. **Allied intervention as debt collection that "attacked the whites"**: This inverts the conventional historical understanding. The Allies generally supported the White movement, not attacked it. While their support was inadequate and self-interested, characterizing it as anti-White is misleading.

8. **Framing the Romanov execution as motivated by bank deposits**: While an interesting speculative hypothesis, this ignores the more immediate political motivations documented by historians — preventing the Romanovs from becoming a rallying point for White forces, particularly as Czech Legion forces were approaching Yekaterinburg.

## Original vs. Cited Ideas

### Jiang's Original Framing / Synthesis
- The overarching thesis that communism was a weapon created by capitalism to destroy its real enemies (monarchy, theocracy, nationalism, democracy/socialism) — this is Jiang's core original synthesis, drawing on but going beyond the Sutton/Spence school.
- The "false dialectic" framework positioning capitalism and communism as allies rather than opposites.
- The systematic comparison of the Communist Manifesto's planks with capitalist practices (centralized banking = Bank of England, etc.).
- The specific game-theory framing of the post-1848 elite strategy: divide the bourgeoisie-worker coalition by injecting class conflict through communism.
- The application to China: communism as capitalism's advance guard, with the Cultural Revolution clearing traditional barriers to hyper-capitalism.
- The speculative connection between Romanov foreign bank deposits and the motivation for their execution.

### Ideas Drawn from Other Thinkers
- **Antony Sutton / Richard Spence**: The thesis that Wall Street financed the Bolsheviks. Jiang explicitly cites Spence's *Wall Street and the Russian Revolution* and draws heavily on this school of thought. Sutton's earlier *Wall Street and the Bolshevik Revolution* (1974) is the foundational text of this argument.
- **Karl Marx**: Jiang accurately summarizes several Marxist concepts — false consciousness, the vanguard party, the dictatorship of the proletariat, class struggle — though he reinterprets them as features designed to undermine rather than advance worker interests.
- **Lenin**: The concept of the vanguard party accelerating revolution in pre-industrial societies.
- **Mao Zedong**: The adaptation of revolution to peasant societies.
- **Standard historiography**: The 1848 revolutions, the Concert of Europe, the Russian Civil War, and the Constituent Assembly election results are drawn from conventional historical sources, though interpreted through Jiang's conspiratorial lens.

## Summary

In this second installment of his Game Theory lecture series, Prof. Jiang Xueqin uses the failures of modern schooling as a case study to teach students how to apply game-theoretic reasoning to real-world systems. He begins by identifying three core purposes of education — literacy, core competencies (critical thinking, collaboration, communication), and lifelong learning — and argues that most schools not only fail to deliver on these goals but actively produce the opposite outcomes: declining literacy, competitive rather than collaborative environments, and a hatred of learning.

Jiang then narrates his own autobiographical case study. In 2008, he was hired at Shenzhen Middle School in southern China to build an international study-abroad program. He introduced seminar-style teaching, mandatory book reading (replacing SAT word-list memorization), a student-run coffee house, and a daily student newspaper — all designed to cultivate real skills rather than test performance. The program achieved strong college admissions results (Yale, Wharton, Cornell), yet Jiang was fired, rejected by all stakeholders, and never allowed to run a school program again. He characterizes the reaction as one of hostility: he was called a "dictator" and an "asshole" rather than an innovator.

The core analytical move of the lecture is to identify the six stakeholders (players) in the education game — students, parents, teachers, administrators, government, and colleges — and to contrast an idealistic view of their motivations with what Jiang argues are their actual motivations. The central game-theoretic principle he introduces is that all players seek maximum results with minimum effort. Parents want "face" (prestige) and control, not genuine learning; teachers want to do the minimum to keep their jobs; administrators want to protect relationships with powerful parents; the government wants compliance and no problems; colleges (especially American ones) primarily want tuition revenue. The convergence of these self-interested motivations produces the dysfunctional equilibrium that characterizes modern schooling: white faces for marketing, grade inflation, cheating, high turnover, and a few scholarship students for bragging rights.

Jiang introduces the concept of "superstructure" — the macro-level societal conditions (demographics, economy, politics, culture) that shape players' motivations — measured along three axes: energy, openness, and cohesion. He argues that young, poor societies (like 1980s China) score high on all three and produce good schools, while wealthy, unequal, corrupt societies see all three decline, resulting in individualized competition, fear of admitting mistakes, and loss of motivation. He concludes that reform is possible, but only within the "convergence point" of stakeholder interests — not by imposing an entirely new game from outside, as he tried to do.

The lecture also introduces the concept of nested games: each player's behavior in the school game is shaped by other games they are simultaneously playing (e.g., parents competing with siblings, maintaining relationships with colleagues). Players fear ostracism — being "exiled" from the group — more than poor educational outcomes, which explains why conformity prevails over innovation. The lecture ends by establishing that game theory is descriptive, not normative: it examines "the way things are," not "the way things should be."

## Key Arguments

1. **Schools fail at their stated mission.** Most schools do not effectively teach literacy, core competencies, or lifelong learning; in many cases they produce the opposite effect (hatred of learning, declining attention spans, competitive rather than collaborative behavior).

2. **All players in the education game are rationally self-interested.** The fundamental game-theoretic principle is that every stakeholder seeks the best possible outcome with the least possible effort. This applies to students, parents, teachers, administrators, government, and colleges alike.

3. **The "game" of education is an equilibrium produced by stakeholder convergence.** The rules and culture of a school are not designed by any single actor but emerge from the point where the interests of all players overlap. Reform is only possible within this convergence zone.

4. **Jiang's personal failure as a reformer illustrates the limits of top-down change.** Despite producing excellent outcomes, his program was rejected because it was imposed from outside the existing game — threatening stakeholders' sense of control and violating social norms.

5. **Superstructure determines the quality of education.** Societal conditions — energy (motivation/work ethic), openness (willingness to learn from mistakes), and cohesion (communal investment in shared futures) — shape the motivations of all players. Wealthy, unequal societies see all three decline.

6. **Players are engaged in nested, simultaneous games.** A parent's behavior in the school game is shaped by their family competition game and their workplace colleague game. Students prioritize pleasing parents and maintaining friendships over actual learning.

7. **Fear of ostracism enforces conformity.** Players avoid deviation not because the current game works well but because deviation risks social exile — being kicked out of the group entirely.

8. **Power is unevenly distributed among players.** Parents and teachers are the most powerful stakeholders; students, government, and colleges matter far less in determining how the game is actually played.

9. **Game theory is descriptive, not prescriptive.** It examines how systems actually function based on player incentives, not how they ideally should function.

## Theoretical Framework

Jiang introduces and applies several game theory concepts, though his usage is informal and drawn more from applied social theory than formal mathematical game theory:

- **Players/Stakeholders**: The foundational move — identifying all actors in a system and their relative power. He identifies six: students, parents, teachers, administrators, government, colleges.

- **Motivations and Incentives**: Each player's behavior is driven by rational self-interest — maximum output for minimum input. This is a loose application of the rational actor model from classical game theory.

- **Convergence Point / Equilibrium**: Where the interests of multiple players overlap, a stable "game" (set of rules, norms, incentives) forms. This concept resembles the Nash equilibrium — a stable state where no single player benefits from unilaterally changing their strategy.

- **Zero-Sum Framing**: Jiang notes that school ranking systems create a zero-sum perception among students and parents, where one student's gain is perceived as another's loss.

- **Nested Games**: Players simultaneously participate in multiple games (family competition, workplace relationships, school system), and their behavior in any one game is constrained by the others. This draws on George Tsebelis's "nested games" concept from political science.

- **Superstructure**: A macro-level framework (borrowed loosely from Marxist theory but adapted) describing how societal conditions — demographics, economy, politics, culture — shape the motivations of individual players. Jiang operationalizes this with three metrics: energy, openness, and cohesion.

- **Cheating and Deviation**: From the perspective of the existing game, any behavior outside the accepted norms — even beneficial innovation — is perceived as "cheating." This connects to the concept of norm enforcement in evolutionary game theory.

- **Ostracism as Enforcement Mechanism**: The threat of social exile keeps players within the existing equilibrium, preventing experimentation or deviation.

- **Incremental vs. Revolutionary Reform**: Jiang argues that successful reform must work within the convergence zone (incremental), not attempt to impose an entirely new game (revolutionary). This parallels the distinction between evolutionary stable strategies and disruptive innovations in game theory.

## Fact-Check & Verification

1. **Jiang attended Yale College as an English major.**
   **VERIFIED.** Multiple sources confirm Jiang Xueqin graduated with distinction from Yale College in 1999 with a degree in English literature. (Wikipedia, WISE biography, Harvard GEII page)

2. **In 2008, Jiang was hired at Shenzhen Middle School to build an international program.**
   **VERIFIED.** Sources confirm he served as Deputy Principal of Shenzhen Middle School from 2008–2010, building an international study-abroad department. (Sixth Tone, Wikipedia, WISE)

3. **About 10% of students (approximately 80 per year) were planning to go abroad.**
   **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** The 10% figure and 80-student estimate are plausible for a large Chinese high school in 2008 but are not independently confirmed. Sixth Tone mentions he had "fifty students" in his department who did not take the gaokao, which is lower than the 80 he claims.

4. **Chinese classrooms: 50 students, note-taking only, no discussion.**
   **VERIFIED (broadly).** This is a well-documented characterization of traditional Chinese pedagogical methods, particularly in public schools. Class sizes of 40–60 are standard.

5. **Students were memorizing SAT word lists instead of reading.**
   **VERIFIED.** Jiang himself has written about this in multiple publications. Sixth Tone confirms he "despised exam-oriented rote learning — whether SAT prep, TOEFL vocabulary lists."

6. **Jiang set up the first coffee house in a Chinese high school.**
   **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** This is Jiang's personal claim. Sixth Tone and NYRB interviews confirm the coffee house existed as part of his program, but the "first in China" claim has no independent verification. Given the scale of China's education system, this is difficult to confirm or deny.

7. **Jiang set up the first daily newspaper in a Chinese high school, possibly worldwide.**
   **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** Again Jiang's personal claim. The newspaper is confirmed to have existed, but the "first in China" and "possibly worldwide" claims are unverified. The "worldwide" qualifier seems unlikely — student newspapers have a long history in American and European high schools.

8. **Students worked until midnight or 2 AM on the newspaper.**
   **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** Plausible given the demands of daily publication, but this is Jiang's personal account without independent corroboration.

9. **Program produced admissions to Yale, Wharton, Cornell; best record in South China.**
   **PARTIALLY VERIFIED.** Sources confirm strong admissions results and that the program gained fame. However, "best record in South China" is Jiang's claim and is not independently verified with specific rankings or data.

10. **Jiang was fired.**
    **PARTIALLY VERIFIED.** Sources describe his departure more diplomatically. Sixth Tone reports that "by late 2009, the school's admission scores waned, parents got restless, and Wang, his benefactor, had quit." His tenure lasted "a little under two years." His own characterization of being "fired" may simplify a more complex departure involving loss of institutional support.

11. **After four years, never allowed to run a program again.**
    **QUESTIONABLE.** Wikipedia and WISE confirm he moved to Peking University High School as Program Director (2010–2012), which is itself another management role. He later held roles at Tsinghua University High School and the Harvard Global Education Innovation Initiative. So the claim that he was "never allowed to set up another program again" appears to be an exaggeration or refers specifically to the type of autonomous control he had at Shenzhen, not to management roles in general.

12. **University professors shocked students don't read; assign paragraphs or videos instead.**
    **NEEDS VERIFICATION.** This is a common claim in education discourse and aligns with widely reported trends, but it is presented here as a blanket generalization without citation to specific studies.

13. **People lose focus after about 5 minutes in a lecture.**
    **QUESTIONABLE.** Academic research does not support a 5-minute attention span. A comprehensive review in *Advances in Physiology Education* (2017) found "no evidence to support a decline in student attention span during the first 10–15 minutes of a lecture." The commonly cited figure is 10–15 minutes, and even that is poorly supported. Jiang's 5-minute figure is more extreme than the already weakly supported conventional wisdom.

14. **Chinese students rip up books after the gaokao as a ceremony of liberation.**
    **VERIFIED.** This is a well-documented phenomenon. ChinaFile published photos from 2012 showing students at Yibin No. 1 High School tearing apart textbooks and throwing pages in the air days before the gaokao. It is a recognized cultural practice, though "ceremony" slightly overstates the formality — it is more of an informal, spontaneous tradition.

15. **Finland has about 5 million people.**
    **VERIFIED.** Finland's population is approximately 5.6 million (2024), making the "about 5 million" characterization reasonably accurate.

16. **Finland's best and brightest become teachers; well-paid, high status, high autonomy.**
    **MOSTLY VERIFIED.** This is a well-supported characterization. Finnish teacher education programs accept roughly the top 10% of applicants, all teachers hold master's degrees, and teachers enjoy status comparable to doctors and lawyers. However, "well-paid" is slightly misleading: Finnish teacher salaries are moderate by OECD standards, though the combination of status, autonomy, and working conditions compensates. The World Economic Forum and Smithsonian have documented this extensively.

17. **In 1980s China, schools were "amazing."**
    **QUESTIONABLE.** This is a significant oversimplification. While there is scholarship noting that 1980s Chinese education benefited from post-Cultural Revolution reform energy and egalitarian ideals, the system also faced severe resource constraints, limited access (especially in rural areas), lingering Soviet-model rigidity, and low educational attainment overall. University graduates of the era were sometimes described as "not even capable of reading a book in their own fields." Jiang's claim reflects a nostalgic framing that overemphasizes motivation and morale while ignoring systemic deficiencies.

18. **American college tuition is $50,000–$100,000 per year.**
    **PARTIALLY VERIFIED.** For international students at top private universities, total cost of attendance (tuition + room/board + fees) can approach or exceed $80,000–$90,000 per year as of 2025. The $100,000 figure is at the upper extreme and applies to a small number of schools. For many American colleges, costs are significantly lower. The range is roughly correct for the schools his students would be targeting.

## Controversial or Potentially Misleading Claims

1. **"All players want maximum results with minimum effort."** While this is a useful heuristic and draws loosely on rational choice theory, it is a significant oversimplification of human motivation. Many teachers are genuinely passionate, many parents do prioritize their children's development over status, and many students are intrinsically motivated. Jiang acknowledges this ("not all schools, not all teachers") but his analytical framework treats the reductive case as the baseline, which risks mischaracterizing the many exceptions.

2. **US colleges "just want the money" and "don't care."** This is a provocative generalization. While there are legitimate critiques of the commercialization of American higher education and its recruitment of full-paying international students, top US universities also provide substantial financial aid and have rigorous admissions processes. The claim that Ivy League schools primarily admit students from "powerful families" rather than "the best students" conflates legacy admissions (a real issue) with the entire admissions process.

3. **International schools in China are characterized by "white faces" as marketing tools.** While this is a recognized critique in Chinese education discourse (sometimes called the "white face" phenomenon), Jiang presents it in a reductive and somewhat racially charged manner. The reality is more complex: parents also value English-medium instruction, Western curricula (IB, AP, A-Levels), and overseas university pathways, not merely the physical presence of white teachers.

4. **1980s China had "amazing" schools.** As noted in the fact-check, this is a nostalgic and selective reading. The 1980s Chinese education system had real strengths (post-Cultural Revolution idealism, strong teacher commitment) but also serious deficiencies in resources, access, and curricular breadth.

5. **"After four years... never allowed to set up another program again."** This appears to contradict his own career timeline. He ran programs at Peking University High School (2010–2012) and subsequently held positions at Tsinghua University High School. The claim seems designed to dramatize his narrative of martyrdom.

6. **"First coffee house / first daily newspaper in China" claims.** These are unverifiable superlatives that add narrative drama but should be treated with skepticism given the scale and diversity of China's education system.

7. **"People lose focus after about 5 minutes."** As noted, this is unsupported by the empirical literature on attention spans in educational settings.

**Ideas Drawn from Other Thinkers:**
- **Game theory fundamentals** (rational actors, incentives, equilibria): From classical game theory (von Neumann, Nash, etc.), though Jiang does not cite any specific theorists
- **Superstructure**: Borrowed from Marxist theory (the concept of societal base and superstructure), though Jiang adapts it into his own three-axis framework and does not credit Marx explicitly
- **Nested games**: The concept of players simultaneously participating in multiple interconnected games comes from political scientist George Tsebelis's *Nested Games* (1990), though Jiang does not cite him

- **Zero-sum game**: Standard game theory terminology applied informally

Jiang's contribution is not original game theory but rather the applied synthesis — using informal game-theoretic reasoning to analyze a specific institutional failure he experienced personally.

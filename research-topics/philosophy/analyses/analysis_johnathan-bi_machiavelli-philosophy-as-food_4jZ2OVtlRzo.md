# Analysis: Machiavelli's Letter to Vettori and Philosophy as Nourishment

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4jZ2OVtlRzo
- **Channel**: Johnathan Bi
- **Analyzed**: 2026-02-25

---

## Central Claim

Bi's core thesis is that Machiavelli's famous 1513 letter to Francesco Vettori -- written from exile in Sant'Andrea in Percussina -- reveals a distinctive and philosophically rich understanding of what it means to engage with great books: philosophy is not information retrieval or the passive collection of truth claims, but a form of nourishment that must be digested, integrated, and reproduced in order to become knowledge. Bi frames this through Machiavelli's own gastronomical metaphor ("the food that alone is mine, and that I was born for") and argues that this metaphor captures something essential about the relationship between reader and text. The argument is distinctive in several respects. First, Bi treats the letter not merely as a biographical curiosity but as a statement of philosophical method, elevating what is often read as charming personal correspondence into a programmatic claim about the nature of learning. Second, Bi draws out four discrete lessons from the letter -- the significance of aesthetic self-presentation, the treatment of great thinkers as living friends, the analogy of philosophy as food, and the practice of reconstruction as digestion -- weaving them into a unified vision that connects Machiavelli to Plato, opposes Socrates' critique of writing, and ultimately justifies Bi's own "Great Books" project as a form of philosophical self-nourishment. The scope is ambitious: Bi is not merely interpreting Machiavelli but using him to articulate a general theory of humanistic education as existential sustenance.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **The unity of seriousness and levity as a rare virtue.** Bi opens by establishing Machiavelli's character as someone who combined deep political seriousness -- exile, the composition of *The Prince* with aspirations to eternal glory -- with childlike playfulness, exemplified by his vulgar card games and self-deprecating comedies like *La Mandragola*. Bi supports this with the observation (attributed to an unnamed Italian Machiavelli scholar) that no other great modern political theorist -- not Locke, Hobbes, or Hegel -- could have written both a world-historic treatise and a genuinely funny comedy. The argument functions rhetorically to establish Machiavelli as a uniquely whole human being, worthy of the kind of intimate friendship Bi will later claim one can have with dead thinkers. The structure is analogical: Machiavelli is compared against other theorists and found uniquely capable of balancing gravity and humor. This sets the emotional and characterological stage for the central claim.

2. **Aesthetic self-presentation as a form of honoring the subject.** Bi highlights the detail that Machiavelli, though completely alone, changed out of his muddy work clothes and put on his finest garments before entering his study. Bi reads this as more than eccentricity: it is a deliberate act of honoring the gravity of the intellectual encounter. Bi connects this to his own practice of wearing formal attire in his videos and investing in high production quality. The underlying logic draws on a broadly Platonic principle that the beautiful and the good are not opposed but unified -- that aesthetics can serve as a "ladder" to higher goods. The argument is analogical and rhetorical, moving from Machiavelli's personal ritual to a general claim about the relationship between external form and internal substance. It connects to the central claim by suggesting that the bodily, material dimension of intellectual life (clothing, setting, presentation) is not superficial but part of what it means to take philosophy seriously as nourishment.

3. **Great books as living friendships, not information retrieval.** Bi argues that Machiavelli's description of entering "the ancient courts of ancient men, where received by them lovingly" should be taken seriously as a model of reading. The relationship with great thinkers is not extractive (pulling out data) but relational (building a friendship over time). Bi supports this with a personal anecdote about reading Peter Brown's *Augustine of Hippo* during a difficult period in his entrepreneurial career, finding in Augustine a friend who understood the tension between worldly ambition and contemplative withdrawal. The argument is primarily inductive, generalizing from Machiavelli's experience and Bi's own to a broader claim about what deep reading involves. It connects to the central claim by establishing that philosophy-as-food requires a personal, intimate, reciprocal relationship with the text.

4. **A counter-Socratic defense of writing.** Bi explicitly frames his position as opposed to Socrates' critique of writing in Plato's *Phaedrus*. Socrates argues that writing is inferior to speech because it cannot adapt itself to different interlocutors -- it always says the same thing. Bi counters that in his own experience, great books do speak differently to the reader at different stages of life, effectively adapting their message as the reader matures. Furthermore, Bi claims that a sufficiently deep reader can "make" the dead thinkers answer new questions -- asking Nietzsche about incels or Plato about technology -- in a kind of anachronistic but philosophically legitimate dialogue. The argument is experiential and inductive, relying on Bi's testimony about his own changing relationship with texts. It connects to the central claim by extending the food metaphor: just as the same food nourishes differently depending on the body's condition, the same text nourishes differently depending on the reader's development.

5. **Philosophy as food: the gastronomical metaphor unpacked.** This is the argumentative core. Bi takes Machiavelli's metaphor of philosophy as "the food that alone is mine" and argues it is not decorative but structurally precise. Food must be digested to be useful; philosophy must be integrated into life to be knowledge. Bi reinforces this with a second Machiavelli passage from the *Discourses*, where Machiavelli criticizes moderns who collect ancient vases but fail to "taste the true flavor" of antiquity -- who hear about ancient deeds but fail to imitate them. The structural analogy is: collecting is to tasting as hearing is to imitating; and tasting/imitating is the philosophical analogue of digestion. The argument is analogical but tightly constructed: Bi draws a clear parallel between the bodily process of digestion and the intellectual process of integration. It directly articulates the central claim.

6. **Imitation and reconstruction as the method of philosophical digestion.** Bi argues that the practical method by which one "digests" philosophy is through active reconstruction: summarizing, reproducing, and repeating back what one has learned. He connects this to his own background in mathematics, where understanding a proof requires being able to reconstruct it, and to his "Great Books" project, which he describes not as altruistic teaching but as selfish learning. The process of preparing lectures, interviewing scholars, and delivering condensed summaries forces the kind of active engagement that constitutes genuine philosophical nourishment. The argument is analogical (math proofs to philosophical understanding) and autobiographical. It connects to the central claim by providing the practical mechanism through which philosophy-as-food is actually consumed.

7. **Machiavelli's ambition as expressed even in the food metaphor.** A brief but pointed observation: Bi notes that Machiavelli's phrasing -- "the food that alone is mine" (*il mio solo*) -- reveals his characteristic ambition and possessiveness even in the context of intellectual nourishment. This is not communal feasting but a solitary, proprietary claim on greatness. The argument is textual and interpretive, functioning to keep the portrait of Machiavelli as a complex figure (serious yet playful, generous yet ambitious) in view. It connects to the central claim by suggesting that the food metaphor is not generic but specifically Machiavellian -- tinged with the competitive, glory-seeking spirit that also produced *The Prince*.

8. **The experiential power of place.** Though not formally argued, Bi's entire video is filmed at Machiavelli's house and the inn in Sant'Andrea in Percussina, and he repeatedly emphasizes the significance of being physically present where Machiavelli wrote. The implicit argument is that intellectual understanding is enhanced by embodied, situated experience -- another dimension of the anti-abstractionist, pro-integration thesis. This reinforces the central claim by suggesting that even the physical setting of intellectual work is part of how philosophy becomes nourishing.

## Evidence Quality Assessment

**Strengths**

- **Direct engagement with primary text.** Bi reads Machiavelli's letter to Vettori at length and in close detail, and the passage he quotes is recognizably accurate in its substance (though delivered from a translation, not the Italian original). The letter is indeed one of the most famous documents in Renaissance intellectual history, and Bi's selection of it as philosophically significant -- rather than merely anecdotal -- is well-founded.
- **Cross-referencing within Machiavelli's corpus.** Bi does not rely solely on the letter but brings in the *Discourses on Livy* to reinforce the gastronomical metaphor, showing that this is a recurring motif in Machiavelli's thought rather than a one-off figure of speech. He also references *La Mandragola* and Machiavelli's comedies to support the seriousness/levity duality.
- **Effective use of the Platonic tradition.** The connection between Machiavelli's aesthetic self-presentation and the Platonic unity of the beautiful and the good is a genuine philosophical insight, and Bi's invocation of the *Phaedrus* to frame his counter-Socratic defense of writing shows real engagement with the philosophical tradition.
- **Honest autobiographical grounding.** Bi is transparent that his "Great Books" project is a form of self-education, not pedagogy, and his personal anecdotes (the Augustine reading, the math background) give the analysis a lived quality that is appropriate to the subject matter.

**Weaknesses**

- **Missing citation specificity.** The letter to Vettori is dated December 10, 1513, and addressed to Francesco Vettori, the Florentine ambassador to Rome. Bi never names Vettori or provides the date, which weakens the scholarly framing. The passage from the *Discourses* is referenced without specifying where in the text it appears (it is from the Preface to Book I).
- **Unnamed scholar.** Bi attributes a striking claim -- that no other modern political theorist could have written both a great treatise and a great comedy -- to "one of the top Italian Machiavelli scholars" without naming them. This is anecdotal and unverifiable as presented.
- **Oversimplification of the Phaedrus critique.** Socrates' critique of writing in the *Phaedrus* (274b-278b) is considerably more nuanced than Bi presents it. Socrates does not simply say writing is "bad" -- the argument is embedded in a complex discussion of memory, the soul, and the relationship between writing and philosophical truth. Bi's summary flattens this into a simple claim about adaptability.
- **No engagement with scholarly literature on the letter.** The Vettori letter has been extensively analyzed by scholars (e.g., John Najemy, Robert Black, Maurizio Viroli). Bi treats it as if he is discovering its significance for the first time, missing an opportunity to engage with established interpretive traditions.
- **The Platonic connection is asserted rather than argued.** The claim that "the beautiful is the good" is presented as a Platonic position without engaging with the complexity of Plato's actual arguments about beauty in the *Symposium* (210a-212a) or the *Phaedrus*, where beauty is specifically described as the most visible of the Forms but is not simply identified with the Good.

## Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots

1. **Socrates' critique of writing is more sophisticated than Bi acknowledges, and may actually support Bi's own position.** Bi presents his defense of writing as "counter-Socratic," but Socrates' argument in the *Phaedrus* is widely read by scholars (e.g., Derrida in *Dissemination*, Griswold in *Self-Knowledge in Plato's Phaedrus*) as deliberately self-undermining: Plato, after all, wrote the critique of writing down. Moreover, Socrates specifically says that writing can serve as a "reminder" (*hypomnesis*) for those who already know -- which is arguably exactly what Bi is describing when he talks about great books speaking differently at different stages of life. Bi may be closer to Socrates than he thinks. **Severity: moderate** -- this does not undermine the central claim but reveals a significant misreading that weakens the counter-Socratic framing.

2. **The food metaphor obscures the social and political dimensions of philosophy.** By emphasizing philosophy as personal nourishment -- food "that alone is mine" -- Bi (following Machiavelli) risks privatizing what is, in most philosophical traditions, an essentially communal activity. Socratic dialogue is inherently public; Aristotle held that the good life requires the polis; even Machiavelli's own *Discourses* are structured as a conversation with friends (the Orti Oricellari circle). The food metaphor, taken seriously, implies a solitary, even possessive relationship with wisdom that is in tension with the dialogical and political traditions Bi elsewhere invokes. **Severity: moderate** -- this is a genuine tension in the argument, though Bi could respond that communal and private dimensions are complementary.

3. **"Philosophy is not a science" is a contentious claim presented as self-evident.** Bi asserts that "you're not after truth claims in philosophy" but rather "truth claims that nourish life." This is a substantive philosophical position (aligned broadly with existentialist or therapeutic readings of philosophy) that many philosophers would reject. Analytic philosophers from Frege to Williamson would insist that philosophy is precisely in the business of establishing truth claims, and that "nourishment" is at best a side effect. Even within the continental tradition, Husserl and Heidegger would resist reducing philosophy to personal nourishment. Bi presents a partisan view as if it were uncontroversial. **Severity: significant** -- this is a foundational assumption of the entire argument and deserves explicit defense rather than casual assertion.

4. **The claim that great books "speak differently" at different life stages does not actually refute Socrates.** Socrates' point in the *Phaedrus* is that a text cannot assess its audience and adjust its argument accordingly -- it cannot ask clarifying questions, respond to objections, or tailor its examples. The fact that a reader brings different life experiences to a text and therefore takes different things from it is perfectly compatible with Socrates' claim: the text has not changed or adapted; the reader has. Bi conflates the reader's changing reception with the text's responsiveness. **Severity: moderate to significant** -- this is a logical gap in what Bi presents as one of his key philosophical interventions.

5. **Anachronistic dialogue with dead thinkers ("asking Nietzsche about incels") risks projection rather than understanding.** Bi claims that a sufficiently deep reader can make dead thinkers "answer" contemporary questions. But this risks what Quentin Skinner and the Cambridge School of intellectual history have argued against: imposing our questions on thinkers whose conceptual frameworks were fundamentally different. "What would Nietzsche think about incels?" may produce an entertaining exercise, but it may tell us more about the questioner's assumptions than about Nietzsche's philosophy. Genuine historical understanding may require precisely the opposite: letting thinkers ask their own questions. **Severity: moderate** -- this does not invalidate the food metaphor but qualifies the claim about how philosophical digestion should work.

6. **Machiavelli's letter may be more strategic than sincere.** Scholars like John Najemy have argued that the letter to Vettori is not simply a transparent description of Machiavelli's daily life but a carefully crafted rhetorical performance designed to persuade Vettori to help him regain political favor. The dramatic contrast between the vulgar daytime activities and the noble evening reading may be a deliberate literary construction rather than a faithful account. If so, reading it as a straightforward statement of Machiavelli's philosophical method -- as Bi does -- may miss the performative and political dimensions of the text. **Severity: moderate** -- this does not destroy Bi's reading but complicates it considerably.

7. **The Platonic unity of beauty and goodness does not straightforwardly support dressing well for videos.** Bi invokes Plato's identification of beauty with the good to justify his practice of wearing fine clothes and investing in production quality. But Plato's arguments about beauty in the *Symposium* and *Phaedrus* are specifically about how beauty leads the soul *away* from material particulars and *toward* the Form of Beauty itself. Physical beauty is the lowest rung on Diotima's ladder. Using Plato to justify attention to material aesthetics inverts the direction of the argument: Plato would say you should be drawn *through* beautiful clothes toward something that has nothing to do with clothes. **Severity: minor to moderate** -- Bi's broader point about aesthetics mattering is defensible, but the Platonic grounding is strained.

## Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing

1. **"I feed on the food that alone is mine, and that I was born for" (Machiavelli, letter to Vettori, December 10, 1513).** This is the central metaphor of the entire video. Worth developing into an essay on competing metaphors for philosophical activity -- philosophy as food (Machiavelli), philosophy as medicine (Epicurus, Wittgenstein), philosophy as midwifery (Socrates), philosophy as therapy (Nussbaum). Each metaphor implies a different relationship between philosophy and life.

2. **"Philosophy is not a science. You're not after truth claims in philosophy. You're after truth claims that nourish life" (Bi).** A provocative formulation that could anchor an essay on the therapeutic vs. theoretical conceptions of philosophy, drawing on Pierre Hadot's *Philosophy as a Way of Life* and Martha Nussbaum's *The Therapy of Desire*. The claim deserves more defense than Bi gives it.

3. **"To have understood without retaining does not make knowledge" (Dante, via Machiavelli).** Machiavelli quotes Dante's *Paradiso* (V.41-42) to justify his practice of writing down what he has learned from the ancients. This could seed an essay on the relationship between understanding, memory, and writing as technologies of philosophical retention.

4. **"He's dressing up as if he's about to meet his good friends, because he treats the great books and the great thinkers who wrote these books as his good friends" (Bi).** Worth developing into an essay on the phenomenology of reading -- what it means to experience a book as a relationship rather than an information source. Connections to Hans-Georg Gadamer's hermeneutics (the "fusion of horizons") and Wayne Booth's *The Company We Keep: An Ethics of Fiction*.

5. **"What merely collecting vases is to tasting antiquity, hearing is to imitating" (Bi, paraphrasing Machiavelli's Discourses).** A compact formulation of the distinction between passive and active engagement with tradition. Could anchor an essay on the Renaissance concept of *imitatio* and its relationship to originality -- connecting Machiavelli to Petrarch, Erasmus, and the broader humanist tradition.

6. **"Every time in my life that I've matured and have picked up one of these great works, it has a very, very much different language and very different message that it wants to send me" (Bi).** This directly contradicts Socrates' critique of writing and could develop into an essay on the hermeneutic circle -- how the reader's horizon shapes what the text reveals. Connections to Gadamer, Ricoeur, and the entire tradition of philosophical hermeneutics.

7. **"Can you imagine any other great political theorist of the modernity who's capable of writing a world-historic treatise, writing a self-deprecating comedy that was actually really good?" (unnamed scholar, via Bi).** This observation about Machiavelli's uniquely wide register could develop into an essay on the relationship between philosophical seriousness and literary play -- connecting Machiavelli to Kierkegaard (who also wrote under pseudonyms and in wildly different registers), Nietzsche (who composed both *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* and *The Gay Science*), and Plato (whose dialogues include both the sublime *Symposium* and the comic elements of the *Euthydemus*).

8. **"This project right here, the great books project, is not an altruistic teaching project. It's a selfish learning project" (Bi).** An honest and philosophically interesting claim. Could develop into an essay on the relationship between teaching and learning -- whether genuine teaching is always also self-education. Connections to Plato's *Meno* (the slave-boy demonstration), Augustine's *De Magistro*, and Paolo Freire's *Pedagogy of the Oppressed*.

9. **"Unlike the other goods, beauty is striking in a way that let's say justice is not. When you see a beautiful man, a beautiful woman... you are stricken in a way that you aren't if you see a just city" (Bi, paraphrasing Plato).** This is a recognizable paraphrase of *Phaedrus* 250d, where Plato argues that beauty alone among the Forms has the property of being immediately perceptible through the senses. Could develop into an essay on why beauty has this privileged phenomenological status and what it means for the other virtues.

10. **"I was reading Augustine at my age. I was very tracked in his career, and like me, I had this constant pull towards contemplation" (Bi).** The parallel between Bi's entrepreneurial career and Augustine's career in rhetoric and imperial service is suggestive. Could develop into an essay on the perennial tension between *vita activa* and *vita contemplativa*, from Augustine through Hannah Arendt.

11. **"Death does not frighten me" (Machiavelli, letter to Vettori).** Embedded in the longer passage, this line deserves separate attention. Machiavelli claims that philosophical reading overcomes the fear of death -- a claim with deep roots in Epicurean and Stoic philosophy. Could develop into an essay on whether and how reading can genuinely address mortality anxiety.

## Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas

**Ideas from Machiavelli (via the letter to Vettori and the Discourses)**

- The daily ritual of changing into fine clothes before entering the study to commune with ancient thinkers, as described in the December 10, 1513 letter to Francesco Vettori.
- The characterization of philosophical reading as "the food that alone is mine, and that I was born for."
- The description of four hours of reading during which "I feel no boredom, I forget every pain, I do not fear poverty, death does not frighten me."
- The metaphor of being "received lovingly" into "the ancient courts of ancient men."
- The criticism (from the *Discourses*, Preface to Book I) of moderns who collect ancient vases without "tasting the true flavor" of antiquity -- who hear of ancient deeds without imitating them.
- The combination of serious political ambition with playful comedy (*The Prince* alongside *La Mandragola*).

**Ideas from Other Thinkers (cited by Bi)**

- **Dante Alighieri** (*Paradiso* V.41-42): "To have understood without retaining does not make knowledge." Machiavelli quotes this in the letter to justify writing down his conversations with the ancients. Bi uses it to support the reconstruction/digestion model of learning. The attribution is accurate.
- **Plato** (*Symposium*, *Phaedrus*): The unity of beauty and the good, and beauty's unique capacity to strike the senses and serve as a ladder to higher goods. Bi invokes this broadly without specifying the dialogue, though the content is most recognizable from *Phaedrus* 250d and *Symposium* 210a-212a. The attribution is broadly correct but imprecise.
- **Socrates/Plato** (*Phaedrus* 274b-278b): The critique of writing as inferior to speech because it cannot adapt to its audience. Bi presents this as a position he opposes. The attribution is correct in substance, though Bi's summary is somewhat reductive.
- **Peter Brown** (*Augustine of Hippo*): Referenced as a biography Bi read during a difficult period. No philosophical claim is attributed to Brown; the reference is autobiographical.
- **Unnamed Italian Machiavelli scholar**: The observation that no other modern political theorist combined world-historic treatise-writing with genuinely good comedy. Unverifiable as presented.

**Bi's Own Interpretive Contributions**

- **The systematic unpacking of the food metaphor as a theory of philosophical method.** While Machiavelli uses the gastronomical language, Bi is the one who draws out the full analogy: collecting is to tasting as hearing is to imitating, and both map onto the distinction between passive absorption and active digestion. This structural analysis is Bi's own.
- **The counter-Socratic defense of writing based on the reader's changing relationship with texts.** While the general idea that rereading yields new insights is common, Bi frames it specifically as a refutation of the *Phaedrus* and argues that great books are functionally "alive" in a way Socrates denied. This framing is original to Bi.
- **The connection between mathematical proof reconstruction and philosophical learning.** Bi's claim that his math background taught him to reconstruct proofs, and that he applies the same method to philosophy, is a distinctive personal contribution that gives the digestion metaphor a specific pedagogical method.
- **The four-part reading of the Vettori letter.** Bi's organization of the letter's significance into four lessons (aesthetic self-presentation, books as friendships, philosophy as food, reconstruction as digestion) is his own interpretive framework, not derived from any cited source.
- **The explicit framing of the "Great Books" video project as selfish learning rather than altruistic teaching.** This is Bi's own honest self-characterization, and it mirrors Machiavelli's possessive language ("the food that alone is mine") in an interesting way.

## Factual Notes Requiring Verification

1. **The letter to Vettori is dated December 10, 1513.** Bi does not provide the date or name the recipient. The standard scholarly identification is the letter to Francesco Vettori, dated December 10, 1513. This should be verified against standard editions (e.g., Atkinson and Sices, *Machiavelli and His Friends: Their Personal Correspondence*).

2. **Machiavelli was "almost sentenced to death."** Bi says Machiavelli was "almost sentenced to die" and "almost died." After the fall of the Florentine Republic in 1512 and the return of the Medici, Machiavelli was arrested, imprisoned, and subjected to the *strappado* (a form of torture). He was released in March 1513, likely as part of an amnesty following Giovanni de' Medici's election as Pope Leo X. Whether he was formally "sentenced to die" is debatable -- he was suspected of involvement in an anti-Medici conspiracy but was not convicted. The phrasing "almost sentenced to death" may overstate the case.

3. **The inn in Sant'Andrea in Percussina.** Bi claims the outer walls of the inn where Machiavelli drank and played cards are "preserved from the time of Machiavelli." The Albergaccio (as it is traditionally known) in Sant'Andrea in Percussina does exist and is associated with Machiavelli by longstanding local tradition, but the extent to which the current structure preserves original 16th-century walls should be verified with local historical sources.

4. **La Mandragola was "performed for the Medicis."** *La Mandragola* is generally dated to around 1518, and there are accounts of it being performed in Florence and possibly in Rome. Whether it was specifically performed *for* the Medici is a claim that should be verified -- some scholars associate a 1520 performance in Rome with Pope Leo X (a Medici), but the evidence is not entirely settled.

5. **The Discourses passage about vases and tasting.** Bi paraphrases a passage from the *Discourses on Livy*. The relevant passage is in the Preface to Book I, where Machiavelli criticizes those who admire ancient statues and artifacts for their artistry but fail to imitate the political and military virtues of the ancients. The specific language about "vases" and "flavor" should be checked against a standard translation (e.g., Mansfield and Tarcov).

6. **The Dante quotation.** Bi attributes to Dante (via Machiavelli) the line "to have understood without retaining does not make knowledge." This is from *Paradiso* V.41-42: "perch'elli incontra che piu volte piega / non fa scienza, sanza lo ritenere, / l'avere inteso" -- roughly, "because hearing many times without retaining does not make knowledge." The attribution is correct, though the exact wording of the translation Bi uses should be checked.

7. **Rousseau as the closest analogue to Machiavelli's combination of seriousness and comedy.** Bi suggests "Rousseau" (likely the intended reference, though the transcript renders it ambiguously as "who's so") as the closest modern parallel. Whether Rousseau wrote genuinely comic works comparable to *La Mandragola* is debatable -- *Confessions* has comic elements, and Rousseau composed the opera *Le Devin du Village*, but neither is quite the same register as Machiavelli's comedies. This comparison deserves scrutiny.

8. **Plato's identification of "the beautiful" with "the good."** Bi states this as a Platonic position. While Plato connects beauty and goodness in multiple dialogues, the precise nature of this connection is debated among scholars. In the *Symposium*, Diotima's ladder moves from physical beauty to the Form of Beauty, which is connected to but not simply identical with the Good. In the *Republic*, the Good is "beyond being" and the Form of Beauty is not explicitly identified with it. Bi's formulation is a common but imprecise rendering of a complex Platonic position.

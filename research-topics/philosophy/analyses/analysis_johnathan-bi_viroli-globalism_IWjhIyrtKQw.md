# Analysis: Viroli's Case for Patriotism as the Alternative to Both Globalism and Nationalism

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IWjhIyrtKQw
- **Channel**: johnathan-bi
- **Analyzed**: 2026-02-25

---

## Central Claim

Maurizio Viroli argues that patriotism -- understood as love for the republican liberty and democratic institutions of one's country -- is the only viable political alternative to the twin dangers of cosmopolitan globalism and ethno-cultural nationalism. His thesis is not merely that patriotism is preferable, but that it is historically prior, rhetorically more powerful, and philosophically more coherent than either rival. Viroli traces a genealogy in which patriotism, rooted in Roman republicanism, the Italian Renaissance, and the Risorgimento, has always meant devotion to the liberty and dignity of one's political community, while nationalism, emerging later with Herder in the eighteenth century, redefined political love around cultural, linguistic, ethnic, and religious homogeneity. The distinctiveness of Viroli's position lies in his claim that cosmopolitanism, while morally and intellectually admirable, is functionally an elite ideology -- accessible only to polyglot, mobile academics and professionals -- and therefore politically impotent. Because ordinary citizens cannot inhabit cosmopolitanism, they default to nationalism when no patriotic alternative is available. This creates a dangerous vacuum: when progressive elites abandon national rhetoric for internationalism, they cede the emotional and rhetorical terrain of political belonging to nationalists. Viroli's ambition is therefore not only theoretical but practical: he claims to have tested and validated this thesis as a consultant to Italian President Carlo Azeglio Ciampi (1999-2006), demonstrating that a patriotism of liberty can generate mass political enthusiasm in the twenty-first century.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Historical priority of patriotism over nationalism.** Viroli argues that patriotism is the older tradition, tracing it back to Roman antiquity, the Italian Renaissance, and the Enlightenment. Nationalism, by contrast, is a modern invention that emerged around 1766 with Herder. This genealogical argument serves a legitimizing function: by establishing historical priority, Viroli claims that patriotism is the authentic form of political love, while nationalism is a later distortion. The argument is primarily historical and inductive -- drawing on a long tradition of republican thought to establish a pattern -- though it risks the genetic fallacy (that older necessarily means better). It connects to the central claim by providing the foundational distinction on which everything else rests.

2. **Distinct objects of love: republic vs. ethnos.** Patriotism's object is the republic -- the constitutional form, democratic institutions, and the liberty of the state. Nationalism's object is the ethno-cultural identity of a people: their language, religion, customs, and ethnic composition. This distinction is crucial because it means patriotism is agnostic to the form of cultural content (a republic can contain many cultures), while nationalism is agnostic to political form (a monarchy, communist state, or democracy can all be nationalist as long as cultural homogeneity is preserved). Bi summarizes this well in the interview. The argument is conceptual and definitional, structuring the entire analysis that follows.

3. **Cosmopolitanism as an elite ideology.** Viroli contends that cosmopolitanism requires material and cultural privileges -- multilingualism, the means to travel, a cosmopolitan education -- that are available only to a small intellectual and professional elite. Ordinary citizens who are monolingual, geographically rooted, and economically constrained cannot inhabit cosmopolitanism. This is an empirical and sociological argument: it identifies a structural limitation in cosmopolitanism's political appeal. It connects to the central thesis by explaining why the left's embrace of internationalism and cosmopolitanism creates a political vacuum that nationalists exploit.

4. **The reactive dynamic: globalism produces nationalism.** When elites promote cosmopolitanism, those excluded from its privileges feel condescended to and alienated. They turn to nationalist leaders who validate their local identities and resentments. Viroli frames this as a predictable political mechanism, not merely a historical accident. This is a causal argument with both sociological and rhetorical dimensions. It gives the central claim urgency: patriotism is not just preferable but necessary to prevent the nationalist reaction.

5. **Cultural values enhanced, not replaced, by republican liberty.** Responding to Bi's thought experiment about Italy losing its ethno-cultural character, Viroli argues that he does not oppose national cultures -- food, customs, traditions, religion. Rather, he claims these are *enhanced* by republican liberty: cultural traditions become richer and more vital in a free republic than under authoritarian homogeneity. Under authoritarianism, culture becomes an expression of "docility" and "cultural obedience." The argument is that liberty is the condition for authentic cultural flourishing, not its enemy. This is an important qualification that prevents the thesis from being read as cultural nihilism.

6. **The Ciampi case study: patriotism works in practice.** Viroli offers his biographical experience as a consultant to Italian President Carlo Azeglio Ciampi (1999-2006) as empirical evidence for the political effectiveness of patriotic rhetoric. Ciampi restored the June 2nd Republic Day parade, placing anti-fascist martyrs and concentration camp survivors at the head of the military procession. Viroli claims this was an "enormous political success." This is an argument from personal experience and a single case study -- anecdotal but vivid. It connects to the central claim by demonstrating that patriotism of liberty can generate genuine mass enthusiasm.

7. **Teaching patriotism through history and culture, not abstraction.** Viroli argues that patriotic education must use the "most powerful instrument available" -- history. He describes teaching Italian students about Risorgimento heroes and having them listen to Verdi's "Va, pensiero" chorus, claiming that students are moved to discover "a dignified, a noble, a generous way of being a patriot." This is an argument about rhetorical and pedagogical method: abstract principles must be couched in national narratives and cultural forms to be motivating. The argument implicitly concedes the nationalist point that emotional attachment requires cultural particularity.

8. **"Make America Free Again" vs. "Make America Great Again."** Viroli distinguishes patriotic and nationalist slogans: the nationalist pursues *greatness* (power, dominance, military strength), while the patriot pursues *freedom*. He proposes "Make America Free Again" as the patriotic counterpart to Trump's MAGA. This is a rhetorical and conceptual argument that crystallizes the distinction between the two ideologies into a concrete political formula. The argument works by analogy and contrast.

9. **Dual patriotism is possible; dual nationalism is not.** Viroli, an Italian-American dual citizen, argues that "if you have a generous heart, you can love two countries" -- but in different ways and to different degrees. This is possible for patriotism because its object is liberty, which is universal and non-competitive. Nationalism, which demands superiority and exclusivity, makes dual loyalty contradictory. This argument extends the theoretical distinction into lived experience and addresses a practical question about immigration and belonging.

10. **Mazzinian internationalism vs. communist internationalism.** Italian patriots inspired by Giuseppe Mazzini fought not only for Italian liberty but for Greek and Polish liberty as well -- not because they were internationalists but because they were patriots who believed in the liberty of all peoples. Viroli distinguishes this from communist internationalism, which subordinates national liberation to a universal class struggle led by communist parties. This is a historical and comparative argument that positions patriotic solidarity as qualitatively different from ideological internationalism.

## Evidence Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**

- Viroli demonstrates deep familiarity with Italian republican thought from Machiavelli through Mazzini to the anti-fascist Resistance, drawing on a scholarly career of decades. His citations of Garibaldi, Mazzini, the Rosselli brothers, and the Risorgimento are historically grounded and appropriate.
- The Ciampi anecdote provides rare first-hand evidence of political philosophy being applied in practice. The detail about anti-fascist representatives leading the military parade is a powerful and specific illustration.
- The distinction between patriotism and nationalism is well-articulated and draws on Viroli's published scholarly work (*For Love of Country: An Essay on Patriotism and Nationalism*, Oxford University Press, 1995), giving it academic credibility beyond the interview format.
- Viroli's engagement with Martha Nussbaum's cosmopolitanism is fair and respectful while identifying a genuine structural weakness in her position. He characterizes it as "intellectually and morally perfectly defensible" but "weak from a political and rhetorical point of view."
- The interview format, with Bi pressing challenging thought experiments (the Italy-as-mosque scenario, the Richmond demographic case), forces Viroli to articulate positions with unusual specificity.

**Weaknesses:**

- The date attribution for Herder and the origin of nationalism -- "around 1766" -- is imprecise. Herder's *Fragments on Recent German Literature* was published in 1767-68 and was primarily about language and literature, not nationalism as such. His more systematic engagement with cultural nationalism came in *Yet Another Philosophy of the History of Mankind* (1774). Viroli, as a scholar, likely means Herder's early work on Volk and language, but the interview presentation oversimplifies and slightly misdates the intellectual history.
- The Ciampi case study, while vivid, is a single data point from Viroli's own career. It is not subjected to any external evaluation or counterfactual analysis. We do not hear what critics of Ciampi's patriotic turn said, or whether the effect was durable.
- Viroli's response to Bi's thought experiment about Italy losing its ethno-cultural character is evasive. He shifts from "cultural values are enhanced by liberty" to "I don't see why I have to give up my Chineseness to live as a free person," which does not directly answer whether the loss of Italy's cultural character -- even under conditions of full liberty -- would constitute a loss.
- The discussion lacks engagement with the substantial philosophical literature on liberal nationalism (Yael Tamir, David Miller) that occupies the middle ground between Viroli's positions.
- Over-reliance on Italian examples may limit generalizability. Nearly all historical evidence comes from Italy's specific republican tradition, which may not translate to countries without a comparable heritage (e.g., much of post-colonial Asia and Africa).

## Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots

1. **The patriotism/nationalism distinction may be historically unstable.** Viroli presents patriotism and nationalism as clearly separable traditions, but many historians argue they are deeply entangled. In the Risorgimento itself, Mazzini's "patriotism" relied heavily on Italian linguistic and cultural unity -- precisely the values Viroli assigns to nationalism. As scholars like Anthony D. Smith (*National Identity*, 1991) have argued, republican patriotism has historically drawn on ethno-cultural sentiment for its motivating force. Bi gestures at this when he notes that "even the language of patriotism needs to be couched within the ethno-cultural rhetoric of people," and Viroli agrees. This concession significantly blurs the clean distinction that structures the entire argument. **Severity: significant -- it does not destroy the thesis but suggests the distinction is a matter of emphasis rather than kind.**

2. **The "elite ideology" critique of cosmopolitanism applies equally to republican patriotism.** Viroli's republican patriotism requires citizens to understand constitutional principles, historical traditions of liberty, and the distinction between love of freedom and love of cultural homogeneity. This is itself an intellectually demanding position. The same monolingual, geographically rooted citizen whom Viroli says cannot inhabit cosmopolitanism may also find "love of republican institutions" abstract and unmotivating compared to the visceral attachments of nationalism. Viroli implicitly acknowledges this when he describes needing to teach patriotism through history and music -- but the question is whether patriotism is genuinely more accessible than cosmopolitanism or merely a different form of elite pedagogy. **Severity: moderate -- it challenges the claimed political advantage over cosmopolitanism.**

3. **Liberal nationalism offers a more honest middle ground.** Yael Tamir (*Liberal Nationalism*, 1993) and David Miller (*On Nationality*, 1995) argue that national identity -- including its ethno-cultural dimensions -- can be compatible with liberal principles. This tradition acknowledges what Viroli's framework struggles to accommodate: that cultural identity is not merely instrumental to liberty but is intrinsically valuable, and that people's attachment to their nation is partly ethno-cultural. By refusing to grant independent value to ethno-cultural bonds, Viroli may be leaving the most powerful human motivations to the nationalists. **Severity: significant -- this is the strongest alternative framework, and the interview does not engage it.**

4. **The thought experiment remains unanswered.** Bi asks whether a fully liberal, fully republican Italy in which St. Peter's is a mosque, spaghetti is replaced by dandan noodles, and ethnic Italians are a small minority would represent a genuine loss. Viroli's response -- that cultural values are enhanced by liberty, and that one need not give up cultural identity to live freely -- does not address the scenario. The question is not whether individuals must sacrifice culture for liberty, but whether the collective transformation of a culture, even under conditions of perfect liberty, constitutes something worth mourning. Viroli's evasion here suggests that his framework may not have the resources to address this question. **Severity: moderate to significant -- it reveals a gap in the theory's capacity to account for collective cultural loss.**

5. **Patriotism of liberty can become its own form of exclusion.** If the patriotic test is adherence to republican and democratic values, then citizens or immigrants who hold illiberal views (religious conservatives, communitarians, traditional monarchists) fail the patriotic standard. This creates a paradox: a patriotism of liberty that excludes the illiberal is itself imposing a cultural test -- not of ethnicity, but of ideology. Critics like Chantal Mouffe (*The Democratic Paradox*, 2000) have argued that liberal democratic theory always smuggles in substantive values while claiming to be purely procedural. Viroli's patriotism may be more culturally specific than he admits. **Severity: moderate -- it complicates the universalist aspirations of the theory without refuting it.**

6. **The Machiavelli inheritance is more ambiguous than presented.** Viroli claims Machiavelli as a patriotic hero, citing his famous line "I love my country more than my soul." But Machiavelli's patriotism, as Viroli himself acknowledges, was not interested in the liberty of other nations and was focused on Florentine and Italian power. Machiavelli's advice in *The Prince* includes the instrumental use of cruelty, deception, and military force. Presenting him as the ancestor of a benign patriotism of liberty requires significant interpretive work that glosses over the darker elements of Machiavellian republicanism. **Severity: minor -- this is more a complication than a refutation, since Viroli has written extensively on Machiavelli elsewhere.**

7. **The American example cuts both ways.** Viroli praises the United States as a "nation of nations" with remarkable cultural and religious pluralism. But American history also shows that republican patriotism (devotion to the Constitution, liberty, democratic institutions) has coexisted with slavery, Jim Crow, Japanese internment, and indigenous dispossession -- all carried out by self-described patriots. The American case demonstrates that patriotism of liberty is compatible with severe injustice, undermining the claim that patriotism reliably protects against the worst outcomes. **Severity: moderate -- it does not refute the ideal but challenges the empirical record.**

## Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing

1. **"Cosmopolitanism is a noble idea that is only for the elites" (Viroli).** This encapsulates Viroli's central political critique and could generate an essay on the class dimensions of cosmopolitan ideology. It connects to debates about "Brahmin left" politics (Thomas Piketty), the disconnect between progressive elites and working-class voters, and the sociology of globalization losers. It also resonates with Christopher Lasch's *The Revolt of the Elites* (1995).

2. **"If you use the language of patriotism correctly, you are going to win in politics" (Viroli).** A bold empirical claim about political rhetoric that could be tested across multiple democracies. Worth developing into an essay on political language and mobilization: when has patriotic rhetoric succeeded, and under what conditions has it failed or been co-opted by nationalists? The Ciampi case could be compared to Obama's patriotic rhetoric or De Gaulle's Fifth Republic.

3. **"Make America Free Again" vs. "Make America Great Again" (Viroli).** A crystallized rhetorical contrast between patriotism (liberty) and nationalism (greatness/power). Could anchor an essay on the semiotics of political slogans and the distinction between freedom-oriented and power-oriented political movements. Connects to Isaiah Berlin's distinction between positive and negative liberty.

4. **"If you have a generous heart, you can love two countries" (Viroli).** A philosophically rich claim about the nature of political love. Worth developing into an essay on dual citizenship, diaspora identity, and whether political attachment is zero-sum or expandable. Connects to Hannah Arendt's reflections on statelessness and belonging.

5. **"Those who cannot travel, those who cannot speak different languages... they're going to hate us. And they become nationalists" (Viroli).** A causal mechanism linking elite cosmopolitanism to populist nationalism. This could generate an essay on the political psychology of resentment and the sociology of globalization. Connects to Arlie Hochschild's *Strangers in Their Own Land* and J.D. Vance's *Hillbilly Elegy* as sociological accounts of the same dynamic.

6. **The Ciampi parade: anti-fascist martyrs leading the military procession (Viroli).** A powerful image of how patriotic symbolism can be restructured. Could generate an essay on national ceremonies, collective memory, and the politics of commemoration. Connects to Pierre Nora's *Les Lieux de Memoire* and the broader literature on invented traditions (Hobsbawm and Ranger).

7. **"My country right or wrong -- no. If my country is wrong, I am saying that is wrong, but I don't leave" (Viroli).** Articulates the concept of conditional patriotic love: committed but critical. Worth developing into an essay on loyalty, dissent, and the ethics of exit vs. voice (Albert Hirschman's *Exit, Voice, and Loyalty*). Also connects to debates about whether whistle-blowers and conscientious objectors are patriots or traitors.

8. **"The same song that you would listen to as a kid can make you move to tears when you're an adult" (attributed to Rousseau by Viroli).** An argument about affective attachment and its role in political motivation. Could generate an essay on the role of nostalgia and sentiment in political philosophy, and whether rational political theory can account for emotional bonds. Connects to Rousseau's broader thought on the social contract and civic religion.

9. **"Which songs, you as a cosmopolitan, can you offer to people that would move them?" (Viroli).** A devastating rhetorical question about cosmopolitanism's motivational deficit. Worth developing into an essay on the aesthetic and emotional dimensions of political identity: can supra-national entities (the EU, the UN) generate the affective bonds necessary for political solidarity? The Ninth Symphony example (the EU anthem) is telling.

10. **"A country should deserve your love" (Viroli).** The concept of conditional patriotism -- that the state must earn the loyalty of its citizens through just governance. This inverts the usual framing where citizens owe loyalty to the state. Could generate an essay on the social contract tradition from Locke through Rawls, and on when legitimate dissent becomes legitimate departure.

11. **The Bi thought experiment: Italy fully liberal but culturally unrecognizable.** Though Viroli does not answer it satisfactorily, this thought experiment is itself philosophically rich. It isolates the question of whether cultural continuity has intrinsic value independent of liberty. Could generate an essay engaging Derek Parfit's work on personal identity (does "Italy" persist through total cultural transformation?) and the ship-of-Theseus problem applied to nations.

## Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas

**Ideas from Maurizio Viroli (via the interview)**

- The historical distinction between patriotism (rooted in Roman republicanism, the Italian Renaissance, and the Enlightenment) and nationalism (emerging with Herder in the eighteenth century) as two separate intellectual traditions with different objects of love.
- The claim that cosmopolitanism is structurally an elite ideology because it requires material and cultural privileges (multilingualism, mobility, cosmopolitan education).
- The argument that progressive/left abandonment of patriotic language creates a political vacuum that nationalists fill.
- The formula: patriotism = love of republican liberty; nationalism = love of ethno-cultural homogeneity.
- The claim that cultural traditions are *enhanced* by republican liberty rather than being in tension with it.
- The concept of conditional patriotic love: the country must deserve your love, but if it does not, you stay and fight rather than leave.
- The biographical account of advising President Ciampi on patriotic rhetoric and the restructuring of the June 2nd parade.
- The distinction between Mazzinian patriotic internationalism (supporting other nations' liberty) and communist internationalism (subordinating national liberation to class struggle).
- The rhetorical contrast: "Make America Free Again" (patriotic) vs. "Make America Great Again" (nationalist).
- The claim that dual patriotism is possible because love of liberty is non-competitive.

**Ideas from Other Thinkers (cited by Viroli or Bi)**

- **Johann Gottfried Herder** -- credited as the originator of the language of nationalism (circa 1766-67). Viroli characterizes Herder's position as the elevation of cultural, linguistic, and religious homogeneity as the supreme political value. This is a somewhat reductive reading; Herder is better understood as a cultural pluralist who valued the distinctiveness of each Volk, not a proponent of aggressive homogeneity. Scholars debate whether Herder is properly classified as a "nationalist" at all (see the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Herder).
- **Giuseppe Mazzini** -- cited as Viroli's "mentor when it comes to patriotism." Presented as the proponent of "patriotism of compassion," who combined love of Italian liberty with international solidarity (supporting Greek and Polish liberation). This is consistent with the scholarly literature on Mazzini, though Mazzini's own nationalism relied heavily on cultural and linguistic arguments that blur Viroli's clean distinction.
- **Niccolò Machiavelli** -- cited as a patriotic hero via his line "I love my country more than my soul." Viroli acknowledges that Machiavelli's patriotism was not interested in other nations' liberty, making him an imperfect ancestor for the modern patriotism of international solidarity. Bi aptly compares Viroli to Machiavelli as "an advisor to princes."
- **Martha Nussbaum** -- cited as the foremost proponent of cosmopolitanism. Viroli characterizes her position as "intellectually and morally perfectly defensible" but politically and rhetorically weak. Notably, Nussbaum herself evolved toward what she calls "globally sensitive patriotism" later in her career, partially conceding Viroli's critique.
- **Rousseau** -- cited (by Bi) for the observation that a song heard in childhood can move one to tears in adulthood. Used to illustrate the emotional power of particular cultural bonds.
- **Abraham Lincoln and Franklin D. Roosevelt** -- cited as examples of American elites who used the language of patriotism powerfully and for progressive ends, demonstrating that elite status and patriotism are compatible.
- **Martin Luther King Jr.** -- cited as an example of an American patriot who was "no less American" than the House Un-American Activities Committee, demonstrating that critical dissent is a legitimate form of patriotism.
- **Giuseppe Garibaldi** -- cited in passing as part of Italy's patriotic tradition.
- **Carlo and Nello Rosselli** -- cited as "anti-fascist martyrs," representing the Italy of liberty against the Italy of fascism.
- **Richard Rorty** -- briefly cited (as "Richard Roth," likely a transcription error) for the characterization of the United States as "a nation of nations."

**Bi's Own Interpretive Contributions (as interviewer)**

- The Richmond, Vancouver thought experiment: using his own immigration experience to challenge the sufficiency of republican liberty in the face of demographic and cultural transformation.
- The Italy thought experiment (St. Peter's as a mosque, dandan noodles replacing spaghetti, Islamic dress): a vivid scenario designed to isolate whether ethno-cultural continuity has independent value from political liberty.
- The framing of Viroli's project as using "the best parts of nationalism in the service of patriotism" -- a synthesis that Viroli agrees with but did not formulate in those terms.
- The distinction between Mazzinian international solidarity and communist internationalism (the Norman Bethune / Baituan example from Canadian-Chinese history).
- The observation that patriotism to two countries is possible precisely because Viroli defines patriotism around liberty (which is non-competitive), unlike nationalism (which demands superiority).

## Factual Notes Requiring Verification

1. **Herder and the date "around 1766."** Viroli attributes the emergence of the language of nationalism to Herder circa 1766. Herder's *Fragments on Recent German Literature* was published in 1767-68, and it was primarily about language and literature, not nationalism per se. His more explicit engagement with cultural nationalism came in *Yet Another Philosophy of the History of Mankind* (1774). The date is approximate but slightly early, and the characterization of the work as a founding text of nationalism is a standard but contested scholarly claim.

2. **Ciampi as president from 1999 to 2006.** Viroli says "between 1999 and 2006, I've been consulted for the president of the Republic of Italy, Carlo Azeglio Ciampi." This is confirmed: Ciampi served as President of the Italian Republic from May 18, 1999 to May 15, 2006.

3. **Restoration of the June 2nd military parade.** Viroli claims the Republic Day parade tradition "had been interrupted in the 70s" and that Ciampi restored it. Historical records confirm that Ciampi restored the full June 2nd celebrations and military parade, and used the occasion to promote patriotic national symbols. The exact date of the interruption should be verified -- some sources suggest the parade was not so much interrupted as downgraded over several decades.

4. **The Rosselli brothers as "anti-fascist martyrs."** This is accurate. Carlo Rosselli (1899-1937) and Nello Rosselli (1900-1937) were Italian anti-fascist activists assassinated in France in 1937 on orders linked to Mussolini's regime.

5. **Machiavelli: "I love my country more than my soul."** This is a well-known quotation from a letter by Machiavelli (letter to Francesco Vettori, April 16, 1527), and Viroli has written extensively about it in his scholarship. The attribution is accurate.

6. **Martin Luther King Jr. vs. the House Un-American Activities Committee.** Viroli's reference to "the committee on American activities that persecuted radicals and socialist dissidents" refers to the House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC). The comparison is historically sound, though King's relationship to HUAC specifically was less direct than his broader persecution by the FBI under COINTELPRO.

7. **Richard Rorty and "nation of nations."** The transcript renders the name as "Richard Roth," which is almost certainly a transcription error for Richard Rorty, the American philosopher. Rorty did write about American patriotism in *Achieving Our Country* (1998), though the phrase "nation of nations" has a longer history (Walt Whitman used it). The attribution should be verified as to whether Rorty used this specific phrase.

8. **Norman Bethune ("Baituan") as a Canadian communist hero in China.** Bi's reference is accurate. Henry Norman Bethune (1890-1939) was a Canadian physician who served with the Chinese Communist forces and became a national hero in China after Mao Zedong wrote a eulogy for him. He is known as Bai Qiu'en (白求恩) in Chinese.

9. **Viroli's claim that Italian patriots fought in Greece "against the Turks."** This refers to Italian volunteers who participated in the Greek War of Independence (1821-1829). This is historically documented, though the extent of Italian patriot involvement varied.

10. **The book title "Per Amor di Patria" (For Love of Country).** Viroli mentions that his book was shown to President Ciampi. The Italian title is *Per amore della patria*; the English edition is *For Love of Country: An Essay on Patriotism and Nationalism* (Oxford University Press, 1995). The attribution is correct.

# Research: Machiavelli Through Johnathan Bi

- **Videos analyzed**: 6 (7th pending)
- **Channel**: Johnathan Bi (@bi.johnathan)
- **Scholars featured**: Harvey Mansfield (Harvard), Maurizio Viroli (Princeton)
- **Date**: 2026-02-25

## Executive Summary

Across six videos -- two original lectures, two scholarly interviews, a Q&A session, and a personal reflection -- Johnathan Bi constructs a multifaceted portrait of Machiavelli that refuses to settle into any single interpretive camp. The strongest convergent finding is that Machiavelli's thought revolves around the concept of *necessita* (necessity) as both a political mechanism and a moral force: necessity replaces goodness as the basis of political order, compels virtuous action where moral exhortation fails, and provides the justification for the "entry into evil" that political founding demands. This thesis appears in every video, from Mansfield's philosophical treatment of necessity as the engine of unlimited acquisition, through Bi's own lectures on necessity as the antidote to civilizational decay and as the justification for Moses's founding violence, to the Q&A's discussion of artificial necessity as a leadership tool. The second major convergent finding is that Machiavelli is not a nihilist or a cynic but an idealist about ends (stability, liberty, rule of law, glory) who is ruthlessly realistic about means -- and that the tension between these two dimensions is what makes his thought permanently provocative rather than merely historically interesting.

The most illuminating feature of this research project is the structured disagreement between the two major scholars Bi interviews. Harvey Mansfield reads Machiavelli as the self-conscious founder of modernity itself -- the philosopher whose concept of "effectual truth" (*verita effettuale*) inaugurated the entire modern worldview, from empirical science to representative government to the logic of unlimited acquisition. Maurizio Viroli reads Machiavelli as a passionate patriot whose central concern was republican liberty, and whose legacy should be understood not as a philosophical revolution but as a practical political tradition that can still mobilize citizens against both nationalist authoritarianism and cosmopolitan detachment. These readings are not strictly contradictory -- Mansfield focuses on Machiavelli's philosophical ambition, Viroli on his political motivation -- but they produce fundamentally different assessments of what Machiavelli means for us today. Mansfield sees Machiavelli as the hidden architect of a world we inhabit without understanding; Viroli sees him as an ally in a political fight that is still being waged.

Bi's own contribution, developed across his lectures and the Q&A, is a synthetic position that borrows from both scholars while adding original elements: the "Machiavelli Paradox" (those who confront political darkness wield power more benevolently), the psychology of glory as a bridge between self-interest and public good, the domain-shift thesis (that technological and commercial expansion can substitute for geopolitical conquest in satisfying Machiavelli's imperative for growth), and a reading of Machiavelli's method as a deliberate oscillation between extremes that functions like scientific experimentation. The personal reflection on the Vettori letter adds a humanistic dimension that the more analytical videos lack, presenting philosophy itself as a form of nourishment that must be actively digested -- a metaphor that aptly describes Bi's entire project of reading, reconstructing, and redeploying Machiavelli's ideas for a contemporary audience.

## Convergent Claims

These ideas appear across multiple videos, making them the most robust findings of the research.

### 1. Necessity as the master concept (all 6 videos)

Every video in the series treats *necessita* as the gravitational center of Machiavelli's thought. Mansfield presents it as the replacement for goodness in the modern moral framework -- people are good only when compelled, and acquisition driven by necessity extends indefinitely beyond survival to encompass glory. Bi's ethics lecture frames it as the justification for entering into evil: Moses massacred because the situation demanded it, and the quality of the cruelty (concentrated, brief, turned to the subjects' utility) is measured by its necessity. The foreign policy lecture elevates necessity to the engine of civilizational health -- without the existential pressure of conflict, states decay into division and effeminacy. The Q&A discusses manufacturing artificial necessity as a leadership tool. Even the Vettori letter reflection touches on it: Machiavelli's exile and near-death imposed the necessity that drove him to write The Prince. And Viroli's patriotism is presented as the political vehicle through which necessity (the threat to republican liberty) becomes motivating.

**Assessment**: This convergence is genuine and well-supported by the primary texts. Necessity is indeed central to Machiavelli's thought across The Prince, the Discourses, and the Florentine Histories. The remaining question -- whether the concept is precise enough to be analytically useful or so broad as to be unfalsifiable -- is raised in the foreign policy analysis and deserves attention in any essay.

### 2. Machiavelli as idealist about ends, realist about means (Mansfield, Ethics, Foreign Policy, Q&A)

Both Bi and his scholarly interlocutors insist that Machiavelli is not a nihilist. Mansfield identifies Machiavelli's ultimate goods as glory and the perpetual republic. Bi's ethics lecture structures its entire argument around three qualifications that make Machiavelli "more digestible": every evil is justified only in service of a greater good (stability, rule of law, freedom, equality); the invitation to evil applies only to extraordinary circumstances; and Machiavelli is an idealist who believes in great men. The foreign policy lecture frames conquest itself as a moral imperative aimed at preventing decay. The Q&A identifies Machiavelli's commitment to rule of law, freedom, and egalitarianism as underappreciated.

**Assessment**: This convergence is somewhat idealized. It reflects Bi's interpretive framework (and to some degree Viroli's republican reading) more than Mansfield's, who is more willing to take Machiavelli's "teaching of evil" at face value. Strauss's reading -- that Machiavelli undermines the good/evil distinction itself -- is acknowledged but not fully engaged. The convergence is real but reveals a tilt in the research toward the rehabilitative reading.

### 3. The armed prophet doctrine and the instrumentalization of religion (Mansfield, Ethics, Foreign Policy, Viroli)

Machiavelli's thesis that "all the armed prophets conquered and the unarmed ones were ruined" appears directly in the ethics lecture (via Moses), is contextualized in the Mansfield interview (Machiavelli as anti-Christ who imitates Christianity's prophetic structures), recurs in the foreign policy lecture (fraud and force as complementary instruments), and is inverted in the Viroli interview (patriotism as a secular form of the prophetic tradition that mobilizes citizens through songs, ceremonies, and historical narratives rather than arms). The instrumentalization of religion -- Moses lying about divine authorization, the Roman generals manipulating augury, Machiavelli's critique of Christianity as "militant but not military" -- is a thread that runs through four of the six videos.

**Assessment**: This is one of the research project's strongest findings because it emerges from multiple independent sources (Bi's own lectures, Mansfield's Straussian reading, and Viroli's textual scholarship, including his argument about Moses's lie). The convergence across different interpretive traditions strengthens the claim that the armed prophet thesis is genuinely central to Machiavelli.

### 4. Glory as the irreducible motivational force (Mansfield, Ethics, Q&A, Philosophy as Food)

Mansfield argues that glory exceeds material acquisition and that Machiavelli's own philosophical glory surpasses princely glory. Bi's ethics lecture develops the psychology of glory as a "dependable mechanism" that bridges self-interest and public benefit -- more robust than altruism, more publicly beneficial than mere self-interest. The Q&A refines this into a distinction between fame (what Caesar had, destructive) and genuine glory (founding or refounding, constructive). The Vettori letter reflection frames Machiavelli's intellectual activity itself as a form of glory-seeking: "the food that alone is mine" is a possessive claim on philosophical greatness.

**Assessment**: The convergence is genuine, but the analyses collectively reveal that the concept of glory does significant load-bearing work without being precisely defined. The distinction between glory and fame is helpful but thin -- historically, the line between founding and conquering has been drawn by the victors. The best essay candidate here would test whether glory functions as a reliable mechanism or merely as a retrospective justification.

### 5. "I love my fatherland more than my soul" (Ethics, Q&A, Philosophy as Food, Viroli)

This quotation from Machiavelli's letter of April 16, 1527 appears in four of the six videos, serving different argumentative functions each time. In the ethics lecture, it is the culmination of Machiavelli's response to the Platonic challenge -- willingness to risk spiritual corruption for one's country as the ultimate form of commitment. In the Q&A, it is traced to a Florentine collective response to papal excommunication, giving it a republican rather than merely personal meaning. In the Viroli interview, it anchors the concept of patriotic love as devotion to republican liberty. And in the Vettori reflection, it implicitly frames Machiavelli's intellectual exile as a form of the same sacrifice -- giving up political life for the sake of philosophical nourishment that will ultimately serve the fatherland.

**Assessment**: The recurrence of this quotation across four videos marks it as the single most important passage for any essay emerging from this research. Its multiple valences -- personal, republican, philosophical, theological -- make it an ideal organizing principle.

### 6. The rejection of the middle way (Ethics, Foreign Policy, Q&A)

Machiavelli's dictum that "men should either be caressed or eliminated" and that "middle ways are very harmful" appears in the ethics lecture (the Samnite/Caudine Forks cautionary tale), the foreign policy lecture (Rome's binary approach to conquered peoples), and the Q&A (the distinction between tumults and divisions). The convergent claim is that half-measures in matters of existential political conflict produce the worst outcomes -- worse than either full generosity or full severity.

**Assessment**: This is a genuinely Machiavellian theme well-supported across the primary texts. The research reveals it as both a strategic principle and a psychological insight about the dynamics of resentment and revenge.

## Divergent Claims

### 1. Mansfield vs. Viroli: The nature of Machiavelli's project

This is the deepest divergence in the research. **Mansfield** reads Machiavelli as a world-historical philosopher who deliberately set out to replace the classical-Christian framework with a new civilization based on effectual truth, necessity, and acquisition. Machiavelli is the "anti-Christ" who imitates Christianity's structures to redirect civilization toward this-worldly ends. His ambition is philosophical and civilizational, not merely political. **Viroli** reads Machiavelli as a passionate republican patriot whose central concern was the liberty and dignity of his political community. While Viroli acknowledges Machiavelli's intellectual power, his Machiavelli is not a founder of civilizations but an exemplary citizen and political thinker whose legacy is a tradition of republican patriotism.

These readings are not strictly contradictory -- one can be both a civilizational founder and a patriot -- but they produce fundamentally different orientations toward the texts. Mansfield's Machiavelli is esoteric, coded, and playing a game across centuries; Viroli's Machiavelli is passionate, direct, and engaged with the immediate crisis of Italian liberty.

**Bi's position**: Bi explicitly locates himself between the two, arguing in the Q&A that Machiavelli was "simultaneously seeking employment, advancing a genuine intellectual innovation, and expressing authentic patriotism." This synthetic reading is intellectually attractive but risks flattening the genuine tension between the two scholarly traditions.

### 2. Mansfield vs. Viroli: Machiavelli and Christianity

**Mansfield** argues that Machiavelli's quarrel with Christianity is civilizational -- he attacks not the corrupt Church but Christianity's metaphysical core: the claim that honor and salvation reside in the next world. Christianity is Machiavelli's chief enemy because it personalizes Plato's "idea of the good" into God, making the transcendent framework accessible to ordinary people and thus harder to uproot. **Viroli**, by contrast, presents Machiavelli as someone whose relationship with Christianity is more complex -- in his published work (Machiavelli's God), Viroli has argued that Machiavelli may have been a "genuine Christian" of an unconventional kind, someone who rejected the institutional Church while retaining a form of Christian piety.

This divergence matters because it determines whether Machiavelli is fundamentally a secularizer (Mansfield) or fundamentally a reformer (Viroli). The pending 7th video (Viroli on Machiavelli's God) should illuminate this disagreement further.

### 3. Bi's lectures vs. Mansfield: The scope of Machiavelli's influence

Bi's ethics and foreign policy lectures treat Machiavelli as a political thinker whose insights can be applied analogically to contemporary domains (startups, technology, cultural criticism). **Mansfield** insists that Machiavelli is not merely a political thinker but the founder of modern philosophy, science, and epistemology -- the inventor of "fact" itself. The difference in scope is significant: Bi's Machiavelli is a brilliant analyst of power whose insights transfer to non-political contexts; Mansfield's Machiavelli is the architect of the entire intellectual framework within which those contexts exist. Bi never directly challenges Mansfield's grander claim, but his lectures implicitly operate at the more modest level.

### 4. Bi vs. Mansfield: The possibility of withdrawal

**Mansfield** argues that Machiavelli forecloses political withdrawal entirely: "Society is radically politicized, leaving no refuge." Even Machiavelli's own contemplative exile was a more powerful form of political action. **Bi** confesses in the ethics lecture that studying Machiavelli "has thoroughly radicalized me -- but in the opposite direction that he would have hoped for," pushing him toward withdrawal. In the Vettori letter video, Bi celebrates Machiavelli's contemplative life as genuinely nourishing and valuable on its own terms, not merely as politics by other means. This tension between Mansfield's reading (no escape from the political) and Bi's personal response (philosophy as genuine refuge) is never fully resolved in the series.

### 5. The role of ethno-cultural identity

**Viroli** insists that patriotism is about republican liberty, not ethno-cultural identity, and that cultural traditions are enhanced rather than threatened by republican freedom. **Bi** presses back in the interview with thought experiments about demographic and cultural transformation, suggesting that the loss of a culture's character -- even under conditions of perfect liberty -- might represent a genuine loss. This disagreement is never resolved: Viroli evades the most pointed version of the question, and Bi does not press to a conclusion. The foreign policy lecture's discussion of "wars of habitation" and population displacement touches similar ground from Machiavelli's own perspective but without connecting it to the Viroli conversation.

## Evidence Gaps

### 1. The Straussian reading is acknowledged but never fully confronted

Leo Strauss's *Thoughts on Machiavelli* (1958) is cited in three of the six videos as the foundational work on Machiavelli, but its specific arguments -- the numerological analysis, the sustained case for esoteric writing, the claim that Machiavelli is a "teacher of evil" without qualification -- are never directly engaged. Mansfield operates within the Straussian tradition but presents his own extensions rather than Strauss's original arguments. Bi acknowledges the "teacher of evil" label but spends more time softening it than reckoning with it. A thorough research project on Machiavelli should include direct engagement with Strauss's text.

### 2. The Cambridge School contextualist alternative is absent

Quentin Skinner's reading of Machiavelli as a neo-Roman republican thinker, embedded in the humanist rhetorical tradition, is mentioned in passing in the counter-arguments of some analyses but never engaged as a primary source. Skinner's *Machiavelli: A Very Short Introduction* and his broader work on republican liberty offer a significant alternative to both Mansfield's Straussian reading and Viroli's patriotic reading. Philip Pettit's development of neo-Roman republicanism from Machiavellian premises is similarly absent.

### 3. Feminist scholarship on Machiavelli is barely touched

Hanna Pitkin's *Fortune Is a Woman* (1984), which argues that Machiavelli's gendered imagery is structural rather than incidental, is mentioned in one counter-argument section but never engaged substantively. Bi's lectures reproduce Machiavelli's gendered vocabulary ("effeminacy" as the opposite of virtue, fortune as a woman who must be beaten) without sustained critical analysis. This is a significant gap given the centrality of the fortune/virtue dynamic to Machiavelli's thought.

### 4. Non-Western parallels are unexplored

Ibn Khaldun's *Muqaddimah* -- with its cyclical theory of civilizational rise and decline driven by *asabiyyah* (group solidarity) and its analysis of how luxury corrupts martial virtue -- offers striking parallels to Machiavelli's arguments about necessity, decay, and the dynamics of conquest. The Mansfield analysis mentions this gap; the foreign policy lecture's discussion of the "virtue-quiet-leisure-disorder-ruin" cycle practically demands the comparison. Kautilya's *Arthashastra* offers another non-Western parallel for political realism.

### 5. Machiavelli's actual political career and its relationship to his theory

While the Vettori letter video and the Q&A touch on Machiavelli's biography, the series lacks a sustained treatment of how Machiavelli's political experience (his diplomatic missions to Cesare Borgia, his militia project, his service during the Soderini republic) shaped his theoretical conclusions. The relationship between theory and practice is one of the series' recurring themes, but the biographical evidence is used selectively rather than systematically.

### 6. The economic dimension of Machiavelli's thought

Mansfield mentions that Machiavelli's logic of necessity extends to unlimited acquisition and connects it to Adam Smith and modern capitalism, but none of the videos develops this connection. The relationship between Machiavellian political economy (keep the public rich and the private poor, distribute the spoils of war to the republic) and modern capitalism is a significant unstudied thread.

### 7. Machiavelli's relationship to Xenophon

Mansfield identifies Xenophon as "Machiavelli's favorite classical thinker" but does not elaborate. This is a major thread in Straussian scholarship (Strauss himself wrote extensively on Xenophon) and deserves development, particularly the connection between Xenophon's *Cyropaedia* (the education of Cyrus as an ideal ruler) and Machiavelli's prince.

## Essay Candidates

### 1. "The Effectual Truth and Its Discontents: Can Modernity Ground Itself?"

**Proposed thesis**: Mansfield's argument that the effectual truth "still has to be measured by an ineffectual truth" exposes the deepest contradiction in the modern project Machiavelli inaugurated -- a civilization built on judging everything by its effects has no non-circular way to determine which effects are good.

**Supporting sources**: Mansfield interview (the effectual truth as philosophical invention, the critique that it requires a normative standard), Bi's ethics lecture (the tension between Machiavelli's good ends and evil means), the Q&A (the oscillation between positions as evidence that Machiavelli himself could not resolve this tension).

**Challenging sources**: Viroli's practical patriotism suggests that the normative question can be answered politically (liberty is the good) without needing a metaphysical foundation. Bi's foreign policy lecture treats the question as settled (necessity determines what is good) rather than open.

**Additional research needed**: Direct engagement with Alasdair MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (diagnosis of modern moral incoherence), Hans Blumenberg's *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age* (defense of modernity's self-grounding), and Charles Taylor's *Sources of the Self* (the hidden moral sources of modern identity). Comparison with pragmatist responses (William James, Richard Rorty) that deny the need for a non-effectual standard.

### 2. "Moses's Massacre and the Violence of Founding: From Machiavelli to Benjamin"

**Proposed thesis**: Machiavelli's reading of the Exodus narrative -- that the ethical foundations of the Judeo-Christian worldview rest on a founding murder concealed by a lie -- anticipates Walter Benjamin's distinction between law-making and law-preserving violence and Rene Girard's scapegoat theory, revealing a persistent insight about the violent origins of political order that liberal thought has difficulty accommodating.

**Supporting sources**: Bi's ethics lecture (detailed Moses analysis, Viroli's textual argument about the lie, the armed prophet doctrine), Mansfield interview (Christianity as Machiavelli's chief enemy, the anti-Christ thesis), foreign policy lecture (wars of habitation and the logic of elimination).

**Challenging sources**: The Q&A's insistence that Machiavelli "really values rule of law" complicates the thesis -- if founding violence gives way to legal order, then the violence may be genuinely temporary rather than structurally persistent. Viroli's patriotism implies that the republican tradition has transcended its violent origins.

**Additional research needed**: Benjamin's "Critique of Violence" (1921), Girard's *Violence and the Sacred* (1972), Derrida's "Force of Law" (1990), and Hannah Arendt's *On Revolution* (1963) on the paradox of founding. Close reading of Exodus 32 against Machiavelli's Discourses I.9 (on one-man rule in founding) and II.8 (on wars of habitation).

### 3. "Patriotism Against Nationalism: Can Viroli's Distinction Hold?"

**Proposed thesis**: Viroli's distinction between patriotism (love of republican liberty) and nationalism (love of ethno-cultural homogeneity) is analytically useful but historically unstable, because patriotic movements have always drawn motivational force from ethno-cultural sentiment -- as Bi's unanswered thought experiment about a culturally transformed Italy reveals.

**Supporting sources**: Viroli interview (the full argument, the Ciampi case study, the evasion of the Italy thought experiment), Q&A (Bi's observation that "even the language of patriotism needs to be couched within ethno-cultural rhetoric"), Mansfield interview (Machiavelli's patriotism as particularist and power-oriented, not universalist).

**Challenging sources**: Viroli's response that cultural traditions are "enhanced" by liberty, and his argument that dual patriotism is possible precisely because liberty is non-competitive.

**Additional research needed**: Yael Tamir's *Liberal Nationalism*, David Miller's *On Nationality*, Anthony D. Smith's *National Identity*, and Benedict Anderson's *Imagined Communities*. Empirical comparison of the Ciampi case with other attempts at civic patriotism (post-reunification Germany, post-apartheid South Africa, post-Meiji Japan).

### 4. "Necessity and Decay: Machiavelli, Ibn Khaldun, and the Cyclical Theory of Civilization"

**Proposed thesis**: Machiavelli's thesis that peaceful prosperity produces spiritual and political decay -- "virtue gives birth to quiet, quiet to leisure, leisure to disorder, disorder to ruin" -- finds its closest parallel not in the Western tradition but in Ibn Khaldun's *Muqaddimah*, and comparing the two reveals both the strengths and the limits of cyclical theories of civilizational health.

**Supporting sources**: Bi's foreign policy lecture (the full decay thesis, the Sparta example, the "diseases of peace"), Mansfield interview (necessity as the replacement for goodness, the perpetual republic as Machiavelli's version of progress vs. the classical cycle), Q&A (Machiavelli as pivotal in the shift from valuing stability to valuing innovation).

**Challenging sources**: Mansfield argues that Machiavelli breaks the cycle through the perpetual republic and genuine progress via the effectual truth -- if so, the parallel with Ibn Khaldun may be superficial. Bi's 21st-century adaptation (technology as the new frontier) implies that the cycle can be escaped.

**Additional research needed**: Ibn Khaldun's *Muqaddimah* (especially on *asabiyyah*, luxury, and dynastic cycles), Polybius's *anacyclosis* theory, Oswald Spengler's *The Decline of the West*, and Arnold Toynbee's *A Study of History*. William James's "The Moral Equivalent of War" as a parallel attempt to find non-violent substitutes for the discipline of conflict.

### 5. "Glory, Fame, and the Psychology of Political Ambition"

**Proposed thesis**: Machiavelli's distinction between genuine glory (founding that benefits the republic) and mere fame (Caesar's self-aggrandizing conquest) offers a more psychologically realistic framework for understanding political ambition than either the classical model (the reluctant philosopher-king) or the modern model (the rational self-interested actor), but the boundary between the two is far more porous than Bi's lectures acknowledge.

**Supporting sources**: Mansfield interview (glory as superior to material acquisition, the philosopher's glory exceeding the prince's), ethics lecture (glory as the "dependable psychological mechanism" bridging self-interest and public benefit), Q&A (the fame/glory distinction, glory's irreducibly dual nature -- selfish and altruistic), Vettori letter reflection (Machiavelli's possessive claim on philosophical nourishment as a form of glory-seeking).

**Challenging sources**: The ethics lecture's own counter-arguments (glory-seeking has historically produced Napoleon as well as Washington), the foreign policy lecture (Rome's glory eventually produced the imperial corruption that destroyed the republic).

**Additional research needed**: Hegel's master-slave dialectic and the struggle for recognition, Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments* on the desire for praiseworthiness, Plutarch's *Lives* as the classical tradition of evaluating political glory, and contemporary empirical work on the psychology of power and ambition. Alexander Hamilton's Federalist No. 72 on "the love of fame, the ruling passion of the noblest minds" as an American application of the Machiavellian insight.

# Analysis: Kierkegaard's Christian Existentialism

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SMJc9UMzFSE
- **Channel**: Michael Sugrue
- **Views**: ~686,000
- **Analyzed**: 2026-02-25

---

## Central Claim

Kierkegaard's philosophy exposes the impossibility of rationally grounding the most fundamental choice in human life -- between the aesthetic life (pleasure, whether physical or intellectual) and the ethical/religious life (obedience to God) -- and insists that this "criterionless choice" must nevertheless be made, with absolute commitment and no compromise. The result is a vision of human existence that is grimly modern: we are adrift without rational moorings, forced into decisions of ultimate consequence on the basis of a "leap of faith" that no algorithm or proof can validate.

---

## Supporting Arguments

### 1. Kierkegaard as anti-Hegelian
Sugrue frames Kierkegaard's entire philosophy as a reaction against the Hegelian synthesis. Hegel claimed to reconcile reason and faith, Athens and Jerusalem, into a comprehensive system that effectively "closed up shop" on philosophy. Kierkegaard rejected this as smug, megalomaniacal, and inconsistent with the lived experience of both reason and religion. Where Hegel offers thesis-antithesis-synthesis, Kierkegaard insists on either/or -- no dialectical middle ground, no pragmatic compromise.

### 2. The Athens vs. Jerusalem framework
Sugrue organizes the lecture around the ancient tension between Greek rationalism (Athens) and biblical faith (Jerusalem). This maps onto a series of binaries: naturalistic vs. metaphysical ontologies, reason vs. faith, the profane vs. the sacred, a human-centered vs. God-centered conception of the world. Kierkegaard holds that every person must choose between these orientations, and that any justification offered for the choice inevitably presupposes what it tries to prove -- making the decision fundamentally circular and therefore criterionless.

### 3. The three stages of existence
Sugrue presents Kierkegaard's hierarchy of human existence:
- **The aesthetic life**: the pursuit of pleasure, whether sensual or intellectual. This is the natural, default human orientation -- the analog of original sin. It culminates in boredom, which Kierkegaard calls "the root of all evil," and, if fully confronted, in despair or suicide.
- **The ethical life**: commitment to moral righteousness independent of pleasure. This resembles Kant's ethics but rejects Kant's rationalistic grounding -- doing the right thing because reason demands it is just another form of aesthetic self-satisfaction.
- **The religious life**: total, unconditional submission to the will of God, even when God's commands violate ethical norms. This is the highest stage, accessible only through the "leap of faith."

### 4. The criterionless choice
Sugrue identifies this as Kierkegaard's decisive contribution to philosophy and the link to 20th-century existentialism. There is no rational decision procedure for choosing between the aesthetic and the ethical/religious life. Any criterion you invoke already presupposes the orientation it is supposed to justify. You must decide, you cannot avoid deciding (refusing to decide is itself an aesthetic choice), and you have "no star to steer by."

### 5. The Abraham story as test case: Kant vs. Kierkegaard
This is the lecture's rhetorical centerpiece. Sugrue contrasts Kant's and Kierkegaard's readings of the Binding of Isaac (Genesis 22):
- **Kant** (in *Religion Within the Limits of Reason Alone*): Any putative divine command must be tested against the categorical imperative. If God orders you to kill your son, the command cannot be universalized, so this being is not truly God. Reason trumps apparent revelation.
- **Kierkegaard** (in *Fear and Trembling*): Abraham's greatness lies precisely in his willingness to obey God without rational justification. The act is unethical by any moral standard -- Kierkegaard does not deny this. But the "teleological suspension of the ethical" in favor of absolute faith is what makes Abraham the supreme man of faith. Faith begins exactly where reason ends.

### 6. The clown in the burning theater
Sugrue highlights Kierkegaard's parable from the *Diapsalmata*: a clown rushes onstage to warn the audience that the theater is on fire, but they laugh, assuming it is part of the act. Sugrue identifies this as Kierkegaard's self-conception -- a deeply serious thinker whose mode of expression (irony, humor, literary indirection) causes people to miss the mortal urgency of what he is saying.

### 7. Kierkegaard as proto-existentialist
Sugrue traces a direct line from Kierkegaard to Heidegger, Sartre, Camus, and Marcel. The key existentialist themes -- subjectivity as truth, the primacy of individual existence over systematic abstraction, the anguish of free choice without rational grounding, the absurd -- all originate in Kierkegaard, even though later existentialists largely stripped away the Christian content.

## Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots

### 1. The either/or binary is too rigid
Sugrue presents Kierkegaard's framework as if the aesthetic, ethical, and religious are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. But many readers of Kierkegaard -- notably Alasdair MacIntyre and Louis Mackey -- have argued that the stages are more fluid and interpenetrating than the lecture suggests. The ethical is not simply abandoned in the religious stage; it is "suspended" in a specific, limited sense. Sugrue's presentation risks making Kierkegaard sound like a straightforward irrationalist, when the actual texture of the pseudonymous works is far more dialectical.

### 2. The pseudonymous authorship is barely mentioned
This is a significant omission. Kierkegaard did not write *Either/Or* or *Fear and Trembling* in his own voice -- he attributed them to fictional pseudonymous authors (Victor Eremita, Johannes de Silentio). This is not a minor literary conceit; it is central to his philosophical method. The views expressed by the "aesthetic" author of Volume I of *Either/Or* are not straightforwardly Kierkegaard's own. Sugrue mostly collapses the distinction between Kierkegaard and his pseudonyms, which is a common pedagogical simplification but philosophically problematic.

### 3. The "teleological suspension of the ethical" is underexplored
Sugrue conveys the drama of Abraham's situation but does not unpack Kierkegaard's technical argument in *Fear and Trembling* -- the distinction between the tragic hero (who sacrifices within the ethical) and the knight of faith (who suspends the ethical entirely), or the concept of the "absolute relation to the Absolute." The lecture makes it sound as if Kierkegaard simply endorses blind obedience, when the actual argument is more agonized and self-questioning than that.

### 4. No mention of *The Sickness Unto Death* or *The Concept of Anxiety*
These are arguably Kierkegaard's most philosophically rigorous works, where he develops his accounts of despair and anxiety as structural features of selfhood. Their absence means the lecture's portrait of Kierkegaard leans heavily on the literary-dramatic side (*Either/Or*, *Fear and Trembling*) at the expense of the more systematic psychological-philosophical analysis.

### 5. The biographical dimension is gestured at but not developed
Sugrue calls Kierkegaard "morbid and emotionally disturbed" and says the work is "produced by a tortured individual," but never specifies the biographical context -- the broken engagement with Regine Olsen, the attack on the Danish state church, the Corsair affair, the death of his father. These are not merely colorful details; they shaped the content and form of his philosophy in ways that a serious analysis should at least note.

### 6. The Kant comparison, while vivid, oversimplifies both thinkers
Sugrue's Kant is a caricature -- "a Prussian god" who "likes rules and obeys them perfectly." This is entertaining but elides Kant's own struggles with the limits of reason (the antinomies, the postulates of practical reason, the "primacy of practical reason"). Kant was not as naively confident in rationalism as the lecture implies. Conversely, Kierkegaard was not as purely anti-rational as Sugrue suggests; *Philosophical Fragments* and *Concluding Unscientific Postscript* engage in sustained, rigorous argumentation.

### 7. The political and social dimension is entirely absent
Kierkegaard's philosophy was deeply shaped by and directed against the established Lutheran church of Denmark and the bourgeois Christianity of the Copenhagen intelligentsia. The lecture treats the ideas in a purely abstract philosophical register without any social or political context, which makes the passion behind the critique harder to understand.

### 8. The danger of the position is noted but not seriously pressed
Sugrue acknowledges that Kierkegaard's framework has a "fierceness of fanaticism" but does not push the obvious objection: if criterionless leaps of faith trump ethical norms, how do you distinguish Abraham from a delusional fanatic who murders in God's name? This is precisely the question that thinkers like Derrida (*The Gift of Death*) and Levinas have pressed against Kierkegaard, and it is conspicuously unexamined in the lecture.

## Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing

- **"Boredom is the root of all evil"** -- Kierkegaard's inversion of the biblical maxim (1 Timothy 6:10, "the love of money is the root of all evil"). A rich concept for an essay on modern ennui, consumerism, and the entertainment industry as responses to existential emptiness.

- **The clown in the burning theater** -- One of the most powerful images in the lecture. The idea that existential urgency is structurally incommunicable because the messenger's form (irony, humor, indirection) undermines the message. Applicable to any thinker whose mode of expression is at odds with their content: Nietzsche, Wittgenstein, even certain stand-up comedians.

- **"Criterionless choice"** -- The concept that the most consequential decision in human life has no rational decision procedure. This is the philosophical kernel that connects Kierkegaard to Sartre's radical freedom, Heidegger's Entschlossenheit (resoluteness), and contemporary debates about moral foundations. Worth developing: is this an insight or a reductio?

- **"Truth is subjectivity"** -- Sugrue mentions this but does not unpack it. Kierkegaard's claim is not that truth is whatever you feel like believing, but that the most important truths -- about how to live, what to commit to -- are appropriated only through passionate individual engagement, not detached objective analysis. A concept begging for careful development.

- **"Distracted from distraction by distraction"** -- Sugrue attributes this to T.S. Eliot (it is from *Burnt Norton*, the first of the *Four Quartets*) and links it to Kierkegaard's aesthetic man. The lineage from Kierkegaard through Heidegger's analysis of "das Man" to Eliot's modernist diagnosis is a genuine and underexplored intellectual thread.

- **The Kant/Kierkegaard divergence on Abraham** -- The most essay-ready material in the lecture. Two brilliant thinkers look at the same biblical text and reach diametrically opposed conclusions, revealing the fault line between Enlightenment and Romanticism, reason and faith, ethics and religion. A natural structure for a comparative essay.

- **"I would be very worried for you if you made all the same decisions as Kierkegaard"** -- Sugrue's closing equivocation. After presenting Kierkegaard's position with great rhetorical force, he steps back and says he is not recommending it -- only insisting that the problem is real. This raises the question of whether you can take the existentialist diagnosis (no rational grounding for ultimate choices) without accepting the Kierkegaardian prescription (leap of faith toward God).

- **Kierkegaard as "modern Job"** -- A suggestive comparison that could be developed: both refuse to blaspheme despite suffering, both insist on fidelity to a God whose purposes are inscrutable, both reject the "rational" counsel of their friends. The difference is that Job's fidelity is tested by external catastrophe, while Kierkegaard's is tested by internal philosophical honesty.

## Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas

### Kierkegaard's own ideas (as presented by Sugrue)
- The three stages of existence: aesthetic, ethical, religious
- The either/or structure: no compromise, no synthesis
- Boredom as the root of all evil and the defining malady of the aesthetic life
- The criterionless choice and the leap of faith
- Truth as subjectivity
- The teleological suspension of the ethical (Abraham as knight of faith)
- The clown/theater fire parable (from the *Diapsalmata* in *Either/Or*)
- The "rotation method" and the ironic history of boredom

### Ideas Kierkegaard is responding to or citing (as presented by Sugrue)
- **Hegel**: The encyclopedic philosophical system that reconciles all contradictions through dialectical synthesis -- Kierkegaard's primary antagonist
- **Kant**: The categorical imperative as the supreme moral law; the rationalist reading of Abraham; *Religion Within the Limits of Reason Alone*
- **Tertullian**: "I believe because it is absurd" (or more precisely, "it is certain because it is impossible") -- the precedent for faith's triumph over reason
- **Luther**: "Faith alone" (*sola fide*); "the believing Christian must pluck out the eyes of his reason" -- Kierkegaard's Protestant inheritance
- **Augustine**: The original formulation of the faith-reason problem that Kierkegaard reformulates
- **The Book of Job**: The archetype of faithful suffering without rational explanation
- **The Binding of Isaac (Genesis 22)**: The biblical text that both Kant and Kierkegaard use as their test case

### Sugrue's own interpretive contributions
- The characterization of Kierkegaard as "morbid and emotionally disturbed" but also "one of the funniest" philosophers -- a biographical-literary framing that shapes the entire lecture
- The comparison of Kierkegaard to Nietzsche as intellectual equals in morbid introspection, differing only on the question of God
- The reading of the aesthetic life as inevitably culminating in suicide if fully self-conscious
- The characterization of Kant's God as "a Prussian god" -- "remarkably like Kant with all his vices taken away"
- The closing equivocation: recommending Kierkegaard as diagnostician of the human condition while not endorsing his prescriptions
- The framing of Kierkegaard as "a contemporary of ours" who understands moral confusion "probably better than any of us"
- The sustained emphasis on Kierkegaard's literary quality as a reason to read him even if you reject his philosophy
- The implicit comparison of Kierkegaard to Job as a structural key to the entire lecture

## Overall Assessment

This is a charismatic, rhetorically powerful introductory lecture that succeeds in conveying the existential urgency of Kierkegaard's thought. Sugrue's greatest strength is his ability to make abstract philosophical positions feel like matters of life and death -- which is, of course, exactly how Kierkegaard wanted them to feel. The Kant/Kierkegaard comparison on Abraham is the lecture's best section: concrete, dramatic, and philosophically illuminating.

The lecture's weaknesses are those of a good popularization. The pseudonymous authorship -- arguably Kierkegaard's most radical philosophical innovation -- is almost entirely elided. The three stages are presented too schematically. The "criterionless choice" is stated but not interrogated: Sugrue does not ask whether the concept is coherent, or whether Kierkegaard's own elaborate argumentation for it constitutes a kind of rational justification despite itself. The most dangerous implication of the position -- that faith-based suspension of ethics could license atrocity -- is noted in passing ("fierceness of fanaticism") but never seriously confronted.

The most essay-worthy threads are: (1) the Kant vs. Kierkegaard reading of Abraham as a microcosm of the Enlightenment/Romanticism divide; (2) the concept of criterionless choice and whether it is genuinely criterionless or smuggles in unacknowledged criteria; and (3) the clown-in-the-burning-theater as a parable about the structural incommunicability of existential truth.

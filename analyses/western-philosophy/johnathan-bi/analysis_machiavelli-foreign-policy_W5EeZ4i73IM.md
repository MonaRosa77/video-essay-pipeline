# Analysis: Machiavelli's Case for Conquest — Necessity, Moral Decay, and the Spiritual Dangers of Peace

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=W5EeZ4i73IM
- **Channel**: Johnathan Bi
- **Analyzed**: 2026-02-25

---

## Central Claim

Johnathan Bi argues that Machiavelli's advocacy of conquest and expansion is not merely a strategic recommendation but a deeply moral one: peaceful, stable societies inevitably fall into spiritual and political decay, and the only reliable antidote is exposure to *necessity* — the existential pressures generated by conflict, danger, and expansion. The core thesis is that the opposite of conquering is not stability but rather being conquered, because human affairs are always in motion and a state that stands still relative to expanding neighbors is effectively declining. What makes this framing distinctive is Bi's insistence that Machiavelli is not a warmonger who celebrates bloodshed for its own sake, but a thinker who views danger and conflict as *pedagogical* — as forces that discipline a people against moral complacency, factional division, and the delusion that goodness alone, without power, can sustain a civilization. Bi structures the lecture in three parts — why Machiavelli urges conquest, how he advises states to conquer (with Rome as the ideal model combining fraud and selective force), and a personal critique adapting these insights to a 21st-century context where nuclear weapons make geopolitical conquest untenable. The argument's scope is ambitious: it attempts to derive from Machiavelli's *Discourses on Livy* and *The Prince* a unified theory of political health that applies equally to nation-states, startup cultures, and the moral texture of Western civilization after decades of peace.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Necessity as the engine of virtue** — Bi argues that Machiavelli's concept of *necessita* — circumstances that compel virtuous (in the Machiavellian sense: decisive, manly, ruthless) action — is the central mechanism of political health. When people face existential pressure, they are compelled toward meritocracy, resourcefulness, and clear-eyed realism. When necessity is absent, moral decadence fills the vacuum. This is the foundational premise from which the entire lecture proceeds, functioning as both a causal claim and a normative one: necessity is not just instrumentally useful but spiritually purifying. The argument is primarily analogical and inductive, drawing on three extended examples (UChicago, Gandalf, startups) before connecting to Machiavelli's textual claims.

2. **The UChicago anecdote: violence as a reality check** — Bi recounts a conversation with a UChicago friend who attributed the school's robust free speech culture to the proximity of violence in Chicago's South Side. The claim is that physical danger operates as a check on impractical moral posturing — students who live near real violence cannot indulge in ideas like "defund the police" without confronting immediate consequences. This argument functions rhetorically as an accessible entry point, grounding Machiavelli's abstract claims in a contemporary setting. Its logical structure is analogical: if proximity to violence produces sobriety at the micro level (a university), it should do so at the macro level (a state). Bi himself acknowledges he cannot verify whether this analysis is correct, which is an important qualification.

3. **The Gandalf comparison: professions of good vs. the union of goodness and power** — Bi contrasts Tolkien's Gandalf ("I am a servant of the secret fire, wielder of the flame of Anor") with the 2020s TV series version ("I am good") to illustrate what Machiavelli would call the decadence of *professions of good*. Tolkien, a World War I veteran, understood that goodness without power is impotent; the modern writers, having known only peace, mistake moral self-identification for actual capability. This argument is rhetorical and illustrative rather than deductive — it relies on the audience sharing the intuition that the TV version is laughable. It connects to the central claim by showing how cultural products register the spiritual decay Machiavelli predicts.

4. **The startup analogy: economic necessity as artificial virtue-forcing** — Bi argues that early-stage startups exhibit the same dynamic as states under military pressure: scarce resources force meritocratic hiring, ruthless efficiency, and clear-eyed prioritization. As companies grow fat and stable, they lose these virtues. The best founders, like Machiavelli's wise rulers in fertile lands, inject artificial necessity through aggressive growth targets and radical accountability. This is an analogical argument that extends Machiavelli's framework from the political to the economic domain. It is effective at making the abstract concrete, but it also flattens the distinction between military survival and market competition.

5. **Two diseases of peace: division and effeminacy** — Bi systematizes Machiavelli's warnings about stable states into two pathologies. *Division*: when the pie is not growing, factions fight over shares of it, turning internal groups into enemies in place of external ones. *Effeminacy*: a soft incompetency that moralizes its own weakness, calling cowardice "humanity" and inability "humility." This is a textually grounded exposition of Machiavelli's vocabulary from the *Discourses*, and Bi accurately captures the way Machiavelli uses *effeminato* as the opposite of *virtu*. The argument is deductive within Machiavelli's framework: if peace removes necessity, and necessity produces virtue, then peace produces the opposite of virtue — division and effeminacy.

6. **The false dichotomy of stability vs. conquest** — Bi presents Machiavelli's claim that the choice between stability and expansion is illusory: "since all things of men are in motion and cannot stay steady, they must either rise or fall." States that aim for stability are not actually stable; they are declining relative to expanding neighbors. This is the pivot point of the lecture's first section and is presented as Machiavelli's strongest reason for favoring expansion. The logic is that of a dynamic system: in a competitive environment, standing still is equivalent to falling behind.

7. **Sparta's self-destruction through victory** — Bi uses the example of Sparta's victory over Athens and its aftermath to argue that stable powers destroy themselves even when they win conflicts. Sparta's institutions — closed citizenship, austere egalitarianism, permanent garrisons — were incompatible with managing an empire. Victory forced Sparta into an expansionary posture for which it was constitutionally unsuited, and it collapsed. This is a historical-deductive argument: the premise that institutions optimized for stability cannot absorb expansion leads to the conclusion that military success itself becomes fatal for stable powers.

8. **The three modes of conquest: Tuscan, Spartan, and Roman** — Bi expounds Machiavelli's taxonomy from *Discourses* II.4. The Tuscan (league) mode is easy but caps expansion because committee incentives kill boldness. The Spartan (direct subjugation) mode is bloody and resource-draining. The Roman mode synthesizes both: it begins with fraud (junior partnerships that disguise Roman dominance) and transitions to force once enough firepower accumulates. This is a textual exposition of Machiavelli's argument, and Bi faithfully represents the structure. The rhetorical function is to show that Machiavelli is not merely advocating conquest but has a sophisticated strategic theory of *how* to conquer sustainably.

9. **Fraud over force as the humane strategy** — Bi highlights Machiavelli's counterintuitive claim that deception is more humane than force because it achieves the same ends with far less bloodshed. Rome's use of "junior partnerships" that were actually instruments of domination allowed it to expand with minimal violence in the early stages. This connects to the central claim by addressing the moral objection: conquest need not be maximally brutal if executed through strategic fraud. The argument is consequentialist — fraud is justified by its outcomes relative to the alternative.

10. **The 21st-century adaptation: technology and commerce as new frontiers** — Bi concludes by arguing that nuclear weapons have rendered geopolitical conquest impossible, but the underlying Machiavellian insight — that states need expansionary frontiers to remain healthy — can be satisfied through technological and commercial expansion. "We no longer push up against nation states. We can push up against the limits of nature with technology." This is Bi's own contribution, and he acknowledges that Machiavelli himself would likely resist it (given his dismissal of artillery and his view of commerce as corrupting). The rhetorical structure is a deliberate inversion of the Fabius story: we must disobey Machiavelli's specific orders to fulfill his deeper goal.

## Evidence Quality Assessment

**Strengths**

- *Textual familiarity with Machiavelli*: Bi demonstrates genuine engagement with both *The Prince* and the *Discourses on Livy*, accurately citing structural features of the texts (the three modes of conquest in Discourses II.4, the discussion of Sparta and Rome, the concept of necessity). His paraphrases of Machiavelli's language — "professions of good," "effeminacy," "necessity" — are faithful to the originals.
- *Accurate Rome-Sparta comparison*: The contrast between Rome's open, absorptive, expansionary model and Sparta's closed, austere, stable model is a legitimate and well-established reading of the *Discourses*. Bi captures the key institutional differences (open vs. closed citizenship, evolving vs. frozen institutions) correctly.
- *Honest epistemic framing*: Bi repeatedly qualifies his claims — he admits the UChicago anecdote may be "completely off base," he acknowledges the problems with conquest even as he expounds its benefits, and he explicitly reserves his critique for the end. This intellectual honesty strengthens rather than weakens the analysis.
- *The Fabius story*: The account of Fabius prosecuting the war in Tuscany without Senate approval is drawn from the *Discourses* and deployed effectively both as historical illustration and as the lecture's closing literary device.
- *Original and creative analogies*: The comparison between America and Rome, Switzerland and Sparta, is pedagogically effective and grounded in real structural parallels (geography, immigration policy, military posture).

**Weaknesses**

- *Missing specific citations*: Bi rarely gives book and chapter numbers for his Machiavelli quotations. "Virtue gives birth to quiet, quiet to leisure, leisure to disorder to ruin" is from *Florentine Histories* V.1, but this is not identified. The Virgil quotation from the *Aeneid* (VI.851-853) is similarly unattributed by book/line.
- *Historical oversimplifications*: The claim that Sparta "collapsed almost immediately" after defeating Athens oversimplifies a decades-long decline (Sparta won the Peloponnesian War in 404 BCE and was decisively weakened at Leuctra in 371 BCE — a span of 33 years). Similarly, the claim about Lycurgus "freezing" laws is a debated historical tradition, not an established fact.
- *Selective engagement with Machiavelli's qualifications*: While Bi acknowledges that Machiavelli praises stable states like Germany and Switzerland, he does not dwell on the extensive passages in the *Discourses* where Machiavelli wrestles with the costs of the Roman model (corruption of the military, social inequality from conquest, the eventual fall of the Republic precisely because of imperial overextension).
- *UChicago anecdote is unverifiable*: An anonymous friend's theory about why a university has good free speech norms is thin evidence for a sweeping claim about the relationship between violence and intellectual sobriety. Bi acknowledges this but still uses it as a foundational example.
- *Over-reliance on rhetoric vs. close argumentation*: The lecture's most compelling moments are its vivid analogies (Gandalf, startups, Switzerland's bunkers) rather than careful textual analysis. This is appropriate for a public lecture but means the philosophical claims are often asserted rather than argued.
- *"Effeminacy" framing is presented uncritically*: Bi reproduces Machiavelli's gendered vocabulary without sustained critical engagement. While he later critiques Machiavelli on other grounds, the specific equation of weakness with femininity — which is a substantive philosophical claim, not merely a historical idiom — passes without examination.

## Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots

1. **The survivorship bias in the Rome example** — Bi and Machiavelli both treat Rome as proof that the expansionary model is superior, but this commits a survivorship bias. Rome is the one expansionary republic that lasted long enough to become the paradigmatic case; countless other expansionary states (Assyria, Macedon under the Diadochi, the Mongol Empire) collapsed spectacularly from overextension. Machiavelli himself concedes that Rome eventually fell to corruption fed by imperial success — the same mechanism he attributes to Sparta. If Rome's expansion ultimately produced the decadence that destroyed the Republic, the argument that expansion prevents decay is seriously weakened. **Severity: significant** — this does not refute the thesis but forces a much more nuanced formulation of when and how expansion helps rather than harms.

2. **Machiavelli's concept of "necessity" may be unfalsifiable** — Bi defines necessity as "circumstances which compel virtuous action" and then expands it to include physical, economic, psychological, rhetorical, and artificial forms. When a concept is stretched this broadly, it risks becoming unfalsifiable: any situation that produces good outcomes can be retroactively described as containing "necessity," and any situation that produces bad outcomes can be described as lacking it. A more rigorous analysis would need to specify in advance what counts as necessity and test it against cases where necessity was present but virtue did not follow (e.g., the Weimar Republic, which experienced extreme economic necessity and produced fascism, not Machiavellian virtue). **Severity: moderate** — this is a methodological concern about the explanatory framework rather than a direct refutation.

3. **The peace-causes-decadence thesis ignores counterexamples** — The Pax Romana itself, the period of Rome's greatest stability and cultural flourishing, lasted roughly two centuries (27 BCE to 180 CE) without the kind of existential military threats Machiavelli claims are necessary for virtue. Similarly, the Dutch Golden Age, Meiji Japan's modernization, and post-war Japan's economic miracle all represent periods where relative peace coincided with extraordinary dynamism and cultural achievement, not decadence. Bi's framing of post-war Western culture as uniquely decadent is itself a contestable claim that selectively highlights culture-war triviality while ignoring the extraordinary technological, scientific, and economic achievements of the same period. **Severity: significant** — if extended peace can coexist with cultural health, the necessity thesis is substantially weakened.

4. **The Tolkien comparison misrepresents the original text** — Bi contrasts Tolkien's Gandalf (who understood that good must be backed by power) with the TV series Gandalf (who naively thinks being good is enough). But Tolkien's own philosophy, deeply informed by his Catholicism, would reject the Machiavellian framing entirely. Tolkien's Gandalf draws power from a transcendent moral order — the "Secret Fire" is the creative power of Iluvatar (God). This is precisely the kind of "profession of good" Machiavelli ridicules: the claim that moral goodness is metaphysically real and ultimately more powerful than political force. The comparison works only if one ignores Tolkien's actual philosophical commitments and reduces his text to a lesson about realpolitik. **Severity: moderate** — this does not undermine Machiavelli's thesis, but it undermines the way Bi uses the example to illustrate it.

5. **Commerce and technology as "new frontiers" may not satisfy Machiavelli's requirements** — Bi's 21st-century adaptation proposes that technological and commercial expansion can replace geopolitical conquest as a source of necessity. But Machiavelli's argument is specifically about the kind of necessity that involves physical danger, the risk of death, and the sobering proximity of violence. Economic competition, however fierce, does not produce the same existential dread as watching a city burn. A failed startup founder files for bankruptcy; a defeated general is killed. If the thesis depends on the claim that *mortal danger specifically* is what disciplines a polity, then commercial competition is a categorically different kind of pressure that may not produce the same effects. Bi himself half-acknowledges this when he notes that commerce was, for Machiavelli, the source of corruption rather than virtue. **Severity: significant** — this threatens the viability of the lecture's proposed solution.

6. **The argument elides the distinction between leaders and populations** — Machiavelli's advice in *The Prince* is directed at rulers; his analysis in the *Discourses* addresses republics. Bi moves freely between advice for founders/leaders (inject artificial necessity), states (pursue conquest), and entire civilizations (the West is decadent). But necessity may operate very differently at these scales. A founder who imposes brutal accountability on a team of 20 is operating in a fundamentally different dynamic than a state that wages war to discipline millions of citizens. The aggregation problem — how individual exposure to necessity translates into collective political virtue — is never addressed. **Severity: moderate** — the thesis may still hold at the state level, but the casual analogies between startups and empires obscure real structural differences.

7. **"Artificial necessity" has a dark historical track record** — Bi suggests that wise leaders should inject artificial necessity through harsh laws and difficult conditions. But the historical record of states that deliberately imposed hardship on their populations to cultivate martial virtue is not encouraging: Sparta itself produced a brutally repressive society built on the enslavement of helots; Maoist China's Cultural Revolution and Great Leap Forward were exercises in imposed necessity that produced mass death; North Korea's *juche* ideology of self-reliance imposes constant necessity with catastrophic results. The lecture provides no framework for distinguishing beneficial artificial necessity from totalitarian cruelty. **Severity: significant** — without such a framework, the policy recommendation is dangerously underspecified.

## Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing

1. **"Danger cleanses your soul. Violence is a moral teacher. War brings out the honorable man."** (Bi's summary of Machiavelli) — This encapsulates the lecture's most provocative claim: that danger has a positive moral function. An essay could explore this against the literature on post-traumatic growth vs. post-traumatic stress, and against thinkers like William James (*The Moral Equivalent of War*) who sought non-violent substitutes for the discipline of combat.

2. **"The opposite of conquering is not stability. It's to be conquered."** (Bi, paraphrasing Machiavelli) — This reframing of the stability/expansion dichotomy as a false binary is the lecture's most rhetorically powerful move. An essay could test this claim against game-theoretic models of international relations, particularly defensive realism (Waltz) vs. offensive realism (Mearsheimer).

3. **"A man who wants to make a profession of good in all regards must come to ruin among so many who are not good."** (Machiavelli, *The Prince* ch. 15) — This is one of Machiavelli's most famous sentences and the philosophical foundation for the entire lecture. An essay could trace its reception history from the 16th century to Leo Strauss, Isaiah Berlin, and contemporary debates about liberal idealism vs. realism in foreign policy.

4. **"Virtue gives birth to quiet. Quiet to leisure. Leisure to disorder to ruin."** (Machiavelli, *Florentine Histories* V.1) — The cyclical theory of political health and decay. An essay could compare this to Ibn Khaldun's *asabiyyah* cycle, Polybius's *anacyclosis*, and Spengler's *Decline of the West* to examine whether civilizational cycle theories have empirical validity or are merely rhetorical.

5. **"If you want to kill any creative bold conquering act, just shove it through committee."** (Bi) — This is Bi's vivid summary of why leagues (the Tuscan mode) cannot sustain offensive expansion. An essay on institutional design could connect this to the theory of decisive leadership in crisis (Carl Schmitt's *sovereign is he who decides the exception*) and to contemporary debates about the paralysis of multilateral institutions (UN, EU).

6. **"Rome never went the middle way when it dealt with people. If it benefited you, it would shower you with gifts and honors. If it was forced to injure you, it would completely take you out of action."** (Bi, paraphrasing Machiavelli, *Discourses* II.23) — The binary logic of Roman statecraft. An essay could explore whether this all-or-nothing approach has analogues in modern organizational management, M&A strategy, or foreign policy (the Powell Doctrine of overwhelming force vs. limited engagement).

7. **"We no longer push up against nation states. We can push up against the limits of nature with technology."** (Bi) — The proposed 21st-century adaptation of Machiavelli's expansionism. An essay could test whether technological and scientific ambition (the Space Race, the Human Genome Project, the AI race) actually produces the same civic virtues Machiavelli associates with military expansion, or whether it produces an entirely different set of virtues and vices.

8. **"We must march against Machiavelli as Fabius did the Senate."** (Bi) — The lecture's closing rhetorical flourish, using the Fabius story to justify disobeying Machiavelli's specific policy recommendations in order to fulfill his deeper goals. An essay could examine the hermeneutic principle at work here — when is it legitimate to disobey a thinker's explicit instructions in the name of their deeper commitments? — and connect it to debates about constitutional originalism vs. living constitutionalism.

9. **"The best founders find a way to inject artificial necessity even when economic necessity is no longer there."** (Bi) — This concept of artificial necessity as a leadership tool bridges Machiavelli and contemporary management theory. An essay could connect this to Andy Grove's "Only the Paranoid Survive," to Jeff Bezos's "Day 1" philosophy, and to the broader question of whether organizations can sustain intensity without genuine existential threat.

10. **"They don't have martial vigor. They call it humanity. They can't win any glory. They call it humility."** (Bi, paraphrasing Machiavelli) — The claim that moral vocabulary can be a mask for weakness. An essay could engage this with Nietzsche's *Genealogy of Morals* (the slave morality thesis), with Max Scheler's *Ressentiment*, and with contemporary debates about whether progressive moral frameworks are expressions of genuine moral progress or symptoms of civilizational decline.

## Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas

**Ideas from Machiavelli (via the lecture)**

- The concept of *necessita* as the force that compels virtuous (decisive, competent) action and prevents moral decay
- The claim that stable, peaceful states inevitably decline into division and effeminacy (*Discourses* I.6, I.1)
- The false dichotomy between stability and expansion: "all things of men are in motion and cannot stay steady" (*Discourses* I.6)
- The three modes of conquest: Tuscan (leagues), Spartan (direct subjugation), Roman (fraud transitioning to force) (*Discourses* II.4)
- Fraud as more humane and effective than force in conquest (*Discourses* II.13)
- The importance of never going "the middle way" in dealing with peoples — either benefit fully or eliminate (*Discourses* II.23, *The Prince* ch. 3)
- The cyclical theory of virtue-quiet-leisure-disorder-ruin (*Florentine Histories* V.1)
- The role of "sensational executions" and limited acts of violence in restoring civic sobriety (*Discourses* III.1)
- Wise generals imposing necessity on their own soldiers (burning boats) while removing it from enemies (*Discourses* III.12)
- Sparta's ruin through victory: the stable state destroyed by being forced into expansion after winning wars it was not structured to win (*Discourses* II.4-6)
- Rome's use of junior partnerships as a fraud to conceal domination (*Discourses* II.4)
- Rome's strategies for absorbing populations: open citizenship for culturally compatible peoples, complete elimination or dispersal of hostile groups (*Discourses* II.23)
- The principle of keeping the "public rich and the private poor" from the spoils of war (*Discourses* II.2)
- Short wars to prevent generals from forming private loyalties (*Discourses* II.6, III.24)

**Ideas from Other Thinkers (cited by the speaker)**

- *Virgil* (*Aeneid* VI.851-853): "Roman, these will be your arts: teach the ways of peace to those you conquer, spare the defeated, tame the proud." Used to characterize the Roman self-conception of conquest as a civilizing mission. The attribution is correct; these are Anchises' words to Aeneas in the underworld.
- *Livy*: Necessity as "the last and greatest weapon." Bi references Livy as the source, consistent with the *Ab Urbe Condita* which is the text Machiavelli comments on in the *Discourses*. The precise citation is not given and would require verification.
- *Lycurgus* (legendary Spartan lawgiver): Credited with founding Sparta's institutions and freezing them in time. This is the traditional account from Plutarch's *Life of Lycurgus*; the historicity of Lycurgus is contested by modern scholarship. Bi presents it as straightforward history.
- *Tolkien* (*The Lord of the Rings*): Gandalf's battle cry against the Balrog as evidence that a war veteran understands the necessity of power behind goodness. The attribution is correct; Tolkien served in World War I (Battle of the Somme, 1916) and wrote *The Lord of the Rings* during and after World War II.
- *Cesare Borgia*: Referenced as an example of limited but spectacular violence — killing his minister (Remirro de Orco) to restore order. This is from *The Prince* ch. 7 and is correctly attributed.
- *Brutus* (Lucius Junius Brutus, founder of the Roman Republic): Referenced as executing his own sons to establish the republic. This is a well-known episode from Livy (*Ab Urbe Condita* II.5) and *Discourses* III.3. The attribution is correct.

**Speaker's Own Interpretive Contributions**

- *The UChicago anecdote as a modern example of necessity*: Bi's use of a personal conversation about urban violence disciplining intellectual culture is entirely his own framing. This is a creative application of Machiavelli's concept to a contemporary institutional setting.
- *The Gandalf comparison as cultural diagnosis*: The juxtaposition of Tolkien's Gandalf with the Amazon TV series Gandalf as evidence of civilizational moral decay is Bi's original rhetorical device. It is not drawn from any Machiavelli commentary tradition.
- *The startup-as-wartime-republic analogy*: The sustained comparison between early-stage startups under economic pressure and states under military necessity is Bi's own contribution, extending Machiavelli's framework into the domain of organizational culture and business strategy.
- *America-as-Rome, Switzerland-as-Sparta*: While the Rome/Sparta comparison is Machiavelli's, mapping it onto America and Switzerland is Bi's original pedagogical device. The specific details (Swiss bunkers, demolition points, America's frontier mythology extending to Mars) are his additions.
- *Technology and commerce as the 21st-century frontier*: The proposal that technological innovation and commercial competition can substitute for geopolitical conquest as a source of necessity is Bi's most significant original contribution. He explicitly positions this as going against Machiavelli's own views and frames it through the Fabius metaphor.
- *The Fabius story as meta-hermeneutic device*: Using the Fabius episode (a general who disobeys the Senate's specific orders to fulfill the deeper mission) as a framing device for how we should read Machiavelli today — disobeying his specific recommendations to fulfill his deeper philosophical goals — is a sophisticated original rhetorical move.
- *The "UN Urges Restraint" quip*: The observation that multilateral institutions are structurally incapable of bold action because of committee incentives is Bi's own contemporary gloss on the league/Tuscan mode critique.

## Factual Notes Requiring Verification

1. **UChicago's private police force**: Bi's friend claims UChicago has "one of the largest private police forces in the world." The University of Chicago Police Department is indeed one of the largest private police forces in the United States (approximately 100 officers), but the claim "in the world" may be an exaggeration. Needs verification of comparative size.

2. **Sparta's timeline of collapse**: Bi implies Sparta collapsed "almost immediately" after winning the Peloponnesian War (404 BCE). Sparta's hegemony lasted until its defeat at Leuctra by Thebes in 371 BCE — 33 years later. While this is short by historical standards, "almost immediately" is misleading.

3. **Switzerland's civil war history**: Bi claims Switzerland "hasn't suffered a civil war for 200 years." The Sonderbund War occurred in 1847 — roughly 179 years ago as of 2026. This is close enough to be broadly accurate but not precisely 200 years. Additionally, the Sonderbund War itself was extremely brief (lasting about 25 days with fewer than 100 casualties), so whether it constitutes a full "civil war" is debatable.

4. **Swiss bunker capacity exceeding the population**: Bi claims there are more bunker spaces than Swiss citizens. This is a widely reported fact — Switzerland has approximately 365,000 shelters with capacity for about 9 million people (as of recent estimates), exceeding the population of roughly 8.8 million. This appears to be accurate.

5. **Tolkien's wartime service**: Bi says Tolkien was a "World War veteran" and wrote *Lord of the Rings* during World War II. Tolkien served in World War I (not merely "a World War veteran" — the specificity matters for the argument about proximity to violence). He began writing *The Lord of the Rings* in 1937 and completed it in 1949, spanning both the pre-war and WWII periods. Bi's framing is essentially correct.

6. **"The first genetically engineered human babies already here"**: Bi references this in his discussion of technological change. This likely refers to He Jiankui's CRISPR-edited babies born in 2018 in China. The claim is factually accurate, though the full scope of subsequent developments would need verification.

7. **Fabius prosecuting a war without Senate approval**: Bi tells this story as an example of Roman trust in generals. The reference is likely to Quintus Fabius Maximus Rullianus (consul 322 BCE) and events from the Second Samnite War, recounted in Livy (*Ab Urbe Condita* VIII-IX) and discussed in *Discourses* II.33. The specific detail of him going through the "Ciminian forest" into Tuscany matches Livy's account of Fabius entering the Ciminian Forest (309 BCE). However, in Livy's account, the Senate was actually alarmed and sent envoys — the story is more complex than Bi's telling suggests.

8. **Virgil quotation accuracy**: Bi quotes Virgil: "Roman, these will be your arts: teach the ways of peace to those you conquer, spare the defeated, tame the proud." The standard translation of *Aeneid* VI.851-853 reads: "Roman, remember by your strength to rule / Earth's peoples — for your arts are to be these: / To pacify, to impose the rule of law, / To spare the conquered, battle down the proud" (Fitzgerald translation). Bi's paraphrase captures the sense but is not a precise quotation of any standard translation.

9. **Italy as a "plaything of great powers" for centuries after Machiavelli**: Bi claims that because no Italian city-state could unify Italy, it became dominated by foreign powers. This is broadly accurate — the Italian Wars (1494-1559) saw French, Spanish, and Habsburg intervention, and Italian unification did not occur until 1861. However, the causal claim (that this happened *because* no city-state could conquer the others) oversimplifies a complex geopolitical situation involving the papacy, shifting alliances, and the sheer power differential between Italian city-states and emerging nation-states like France and Spain.

10. **Machiavelli's dismissive comments on artillery**: Bi mentions Machiavelli's dismissal of artillery in passing. This refers to *Discourses* II.17 and *The Art of War*, where Machiavelli argued that infantry remained more decisive than artillery — a view that was already becoming obsolete in his own time and is often cited as one of his significant analytical failures.

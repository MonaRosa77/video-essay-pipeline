# Analysis: Harvey Mansfield on Machiavelli as the Founder of Modernity

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=l21HUb4uunM
- **Channel**: johnathan-bi
- **Analyzed**: 2026-02-25

---

## Central Claim

Harvey Mansfield argues that Machiavelli is the true founder of modernity -- not merely a clever political commentator or immoral schemer, but the philosopher who set in motion the entire modern worldview. The central thesis is that Machiavelli's concept of the "effectual truth" (verita effettuale) constitutes the philosophical revolution from which modern science, modern political institutions (the executive, party government, indirect representation), and modern individualism all descend. Mansfield contends that the standard genealogy of modernity beginning with Descartes or Bacon is wrong: those thinkers are derivative from Machiavelli. What makes this thesis distinctive is its scope -- Mansfield is not claiming Machiavelli merely influenced political theory, but that he inaugurated a total reorientation of Western civilization away from the classical-Christian framework (which grounded truth in an invisible, transcendent good) toward a framework in which truth is defined by its effects, facts replace forms, necessity replaces goodness, and acquisition replaces contemplation. Machiavelli accomplished this as a deliberate, self-conscious "anti-Christ" who modeled his own project on the structure of Christianity -- imitating Christ's prophetic conquest of the world but redirecting it toward this-worldly ends. The ambition of Mansfield's reading is enormous: it positions Machiavelli not alongside but above figures like Hobbes, Locke, Adam Smith, and even Nietzsche, all of whom are cast as commissioners operating under Machiavelli's original dispensation.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Christianity as Machiavelli's chief enemy, not merely the corrupt Church**
   Mansfield argues that Machiavelli's critique of Christianity goes beyond the standard Renaissance complaint about ecclesiastical corruption. Machiavelli attacks the heart of the religion: its teaching that honor and salvation reside in the next world rather than this one. He levels two criticisms -- Christianity is simultaneously too cruel (demanding more of human nature than it can deliver) and too weak (unable to command armies, being "militant but not military"). This dual critique establishes why Machiavelli cannot simply reform Christianity but must replace its entire orientation. The argument is deductive from Machiavelli's premises about human nature: if humans act from necessity, not goodness, then a religion premised on transcendent goodness will always produce either hypocrisy or weakness. This supports the central claim by showing that Machiavelli's quarrel is not parochial but civilizational -- he opposes the entire metaphysical framework that Christianity inherited from classical philosophy.

2. **The classical tradition as the "predecessor" enemy behind Christianity**
   Mansfield traces Machiavelli's opposition further back than Christianity to the classical philosophical tradition itself. Christianity, in Machiavelli's reading, personified Plato's "idea of the good" into God -- making the transcendent accessible to ordinary people through worship rather than contemplation. Thus the classical philosophers are the deeper source of the worldview Machiavelli opposes. Yet Machiavelli selectively rescues certain classical elements -- not the philosophers' ideas (forms, the good, contemplation) but classical political action, the virtuous deeds of Roman consuls and generals. The argument proceeds by analogy: just as Machiavelli critiques Christianity while imitating its structures, he critiques classical philosophy while harvesting its political exemplars. This supports the thesis by showing modernity as a selective inheritance, not a wholesale rejection of antiquity.

3. **Necessity as the replacement for goodness**
   Mansfield presents Machiavelli's replacement of the "profession of good" with the "profession of necessity" as the foundational ethical move of modernity. People are good only when compelled to be; necessity is therefore more reliable than goodness as a basis for political order. But Mansfield emphasizes that Machiavellian necessity is not mere survival -- it extends through the logic of competition and anticipation to encompass unlimited acquisition. Because you can never know how much you will need, and because rivals are always gaining, necessity becomes perpetual and all-encompassing. The argument is inductive, drawing on the modern example of billionaires who never stop acquiring despite having far exceeded any natural limit of need. This is central to the thesis because the replacement of goodness by necessity is what makes the modern world "worldly" -- a world with no reference to another world.

4. **The effectual truth as Machiavelli's philosophical invention**
   Mansfield identifies the phrase "verita effettuale" (effectual truth) from Chapter 15 of The Prince as Machiavelli's singular philosophical invention -- a phrase with no known classical source, used exactly once in all his writings, and therefore a deliberate marker of originality. A thing is what its effect is; truth is defined by outcome, not by reference to a transcendent standard. Mansfield argues this is the seed from which modern empirical science grows: Bacon's experimental method, the reduction of Aristotle's four causes to efficient and material causation alone, and the modern privileging of "facts" over "forms" all follow from this single move. The argument is partly philological (tracing the word "fact" to the same Latin root as "effect") and partly historical (showing Bacon's explicit acknowledgment of Machiavelli). This is the keystone of the central claim -- if the effectual truth is Machiavelli's invention, and modern science and politics both operate on effectual truth, then Machiavelli is indeed the founder of modernity.

5. **Machiavelli as anti-Christ who imitates Christ's structures**
   Mansfield argues that Machiavelli deliberately models his own project on Christianity's. Just as Christ conquered the world unarmed through prophecy, Machiavelli conquers through writing. Just as the Church operates through indirect authority (the priest channels God's will), Machiavelli introduces indirect government (the executive carries out the people's will). The identification of Cesare Borgia with Christ (via the tradition of imitatio Christi) and of Borgia's dependence on Pope Alexander VI with Christ's dependence on God the Father reveals the theological architecture beneath Machiavelli's supposedly secular political science. The argument is analogical and interpretive, reading The Prince as a coded theological text. This supports the central claim by showing that Machiavelli understood his project in world-historical, quasi-religious terms -- he was founding a new dispensation, not merely advising rulers.

6. **The invention of indirect government and the modern executive**
   Mansfield traces the modern concept of executive power to Machiavelli's insight that power is most effective when disguised as the execution of someone else's will. The executive appears merely to carry out the law, the people's mandate, or God's decree, but in fact wields the real power because enforcement -- including the power to execute people -- is where political will becomes actual. This double meaning of "execute" (to carry out and to kill) captures the Machiavellian structure of modern government. Mansfield extends this to representation (Hobbes), management (the modern administrative state), and even confession (the priest who channels divine authority). The argument is conceptual-historical, tracing a single structural insight through its institutional ramifications. It supports the thesis by showing Machiavelli's influence on political forms we take entirely for granted.

7. **Glory as the ultimate good, superior to material acquisition**
   Mansfield reveals a hierarchy within Machiavelli's thought that the surface reading obscures. While Machiavelli teaches the many to pursue acquisition and princely power, the true Machiavellian insight is that philosophical glory exceeds princely glory. Machiavelli himself -- the minister more intelligent than the prince he advises -- has greater and more lasting glory than any ruler. But this truth must be hidden: if people recognized the philosopher's superiority, they could not serve Machiavelli's purposes. So Machiavelli "tricks" the ambitious into pursuing political power while reserving the higher glory of understanding for himself and his true readers. The argument is structured as an esoteric reading: the surface teaching (acquire power) conceals a deeper teaching (understanding is the highest power). This supports the thesis by showing Machiavelli as a self-conscious founder who understood his own project's place in the history of philosophy.

8. **The perpetual republic as Machiavelli's version of progress**
   Mansfield argues that Machiavelli replaces the classical cycle of regimes (the anacyclosis of Polybius) with the idea of a perpetual republic -- not a single enduring state but a succession of states united by Machiavellian political science. Each republic eventually falls to routine and fortune, but the philosophical understanding that fortune must be overcome persists across them. Each subsequent philosopher imitates Machiavelli by innovating -- and to innovate is the most Machiavellian thing one can do. This produces genuine progress rather than cyclical return, because the effectual truth itself is progressive: technologies advance, and the experimental method generates cumulative knowledge. The argument is both structural (explaining the mechanism of progress) and historical (citing the succession of philosophers from Bacon through Hobbes, Locke, Smith, Rousseau, and Nietzsche). It supports the central claim by showing how Machiavelli's founding extends indefinitely into the future.

9. **Machiavelli's refutation of Epicurean withdrawal**
   Mansfield argues that Machiavelli forecloses the possibility of political withdrawal. The talented cannot live unnoticed because their intelligence will attract attention, suspicion, and envy. Even the seemingly private life depends on a prince's protection, which cannot be trusted when interests conflict. Society is "radically politicized, leaving no refuge." Bi pushes back by noting that Machiavelli himself withdrew into writing after political exile, but Mansfield reinterprets this as an even more powerful form of political action: "The less political things are more political if you understand them correctly." The argument is rhetorical and conceptual, drawing on the example of Jane Austen, Cicero, and Churchill to show that intellectual life is politics by other means. This supports the thesis by universalizing Machiavelli's framework -- there is no outside to the world he founded.

10. **Mansfield's own critical distance: Machiavelli as necessary corrective, not final truth**
    In the interview's final movement, Mansfield reveals his own position: he is not a Machiavellian. He reads Machiavelli as a challenge to complacency -- a necessary reminder that goodness needs defense and that classical virtue must reckon with necessity. But the effectual truth, Mansfield suggests, ultimately requires an "ineffectual" normative standard to evaluate whether its effects are good. Machiavelli's own thought contains this contradiction: he cannot justify necessity without appealing to some standard of good (ruin and chaos are bad; stability is good). Mansfield uses Tocqueville's observation that the spiritual proves more enduring than the material as evidence that Machiavelli's materialism undermines itself. The argument is dialectical: Machiavelli's critique of classical virtue ultimately drives one back to classical virtue, now understood more deeply. This is less a supporting argument for Machiavelli's founding than a framing device for Mansfield's own Straussian position.

## Evidence Quality Assessment

**Strengths**

- *Deep primary text familiarity*: Mansfield demonstrates extraordinary command of Machiavelli's texts. He identifies the singular use of "verita effettuale," traces the Borgia-Christ parallel through the structure of The Prince, and connects the longest chapters in both major works (conspiracy) to his thesis about hidden power. His reading of the famous letter about entering the courts of ancient men is deft, noting the "solo" in "the food that is mine alone."

- *Philological precision*: The etymological connection between "effectual," "factual," and the Latin/Italian roots (fare/facere) is a genuine scholarly contribution. The observation that "fact" has no Greek equivalent (the Greeks say "this thing which comes and goes") is a strong historical-linguistic claim that illuminates the conceptual revolution Mansfield describes.

- *Architectural reading of the texts*: Mansfield reads The Prince and the Discourses not as collections of observations but as carefully structured works with esoteric and exoteric layers. The interpretation of Cesare Borgia as a figure for Christ, and of the Borgia-Alexander VI relationship as a figure for the Christ-God the Father relationship, demonstrates the kind of deep structural reading associated with the Straussian school.

- *Breadth of intellectual history*: Mansfield traces Machiavelli's influence through Bacon, Hobbes, Locke, Smith, Rousseau, Tocqueville, and Nietzsche with concision and specificity. He does not merely assert influence but shows the specific mechanisms (effectual truth leads to efficient causation leads to experimental science; indirect government leads to representation leads to constitutional democracy).

- *Honest engagement with contradictions*: Mansfield does not smooth over the tensions in Machiavelli's thought. He acknowledges that Machiavelli's life (the contemplative letter) contradicts his teaching (acquisition and action), and that the case for necessity ultimately requires an appeal to goodness. His own critical position emerges clearly.

**Weaknesses**

- *Lack of textual citations for several major claims*: While Mansfield clearly knows the texts, the interview format means many claims go unsupported by specific chapter-and-verse references. The claim that Machiavelli "accused all Christian priests of being atheists in effect" is attributed to Bacon's paraphrase rather than to a specific passage in Machiavelli. The three chapters in the Discourses where Machiavelli "openly criticizes Christianity" are referenced but not identified by number (they are Discourses I.12, II.2, and II.5, but this is left implicit).

- *Esoteric reading methodology is unfalsifiable*: The Straussian hermeneutic that Mansfield employs -- where surface meaning conceals deeper meaning, and contradictions are deliberate signals rather than flaws -- is powerful but resistant to counterevidence. If Machiavelli says X, it might mean X or it might mean the opposite of X, depending on the interpretive framework. This makes the Borgia-Christ parallel suggestive but impossible to confirm definitively.

- *Oversimplification of the classical tradition*: "The ancients were strong, the moderns were weak" is presented as Machiavelli's most general view, but the relationship between Machiavelli and classical thought is more complex than the interview format allows. Machiavelli's debts to Polybius, Livy, and especially Xenophon (mentioned in passing as his "favorite classical thinker") are not explored.

- *The claim that Machiavelli is "the father of modern science" is asserted more than argued*: The chain from effectual truth to Bacon's experimentalism to modern science is sketched in broad strokes. The specific intellectual-historical links (what exactly did Bacon take from Machiavelli? How does the reduction of Aristotle's four causes to efficient causation actually follow from the effectual truth?) would require more detailed argumentation.

- *Modern examples are sometimes distracting*: References to Trump, Biden, the SEC investigating trillionaires, and McDonald's ketchup packets are colorful but can trivialize the philosophical argument. The billionaire example for unlimited acquisition is effective; the ketchup-packet grandmother is charming but tangential.

## Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots

1. **Against the claim that Machiavelli, not Descartes or Bacon, is the true founder of modernity**
   The standard periodization of modernity beginning with Descartes (epistemological revolution) or Bacon (scientific method) has substantial scholarly support. Mansfield's counter-periodization rests on showing influence, but influence is not the same as founding. One could argue that Machiavelli's innovations were confined to political theory and that the extension to science and epistemology required genuinely independent breakthroughs by Descartes (the cogito, mathematical method) and Bacon (systematic experimentalism). Quentin Skinner's contextualist reading of Machiavelli situates him firmly within the Italian humanist tradition rather than as a radical break. The Cambridge School of intellectual history would resist the Straussian claim that Machiavelli intended to found a new epoch. **Severity: significant** -- if Machiavelli's influence on science is indirect and mediated rather than foundational, the central thesis is substantially weakened, though not refuted.

2. **Against the esoteric reading of Cesare Borgia as a Christ figure**
   The identification of Borgia with Christ via imitatio Christi and of Alexander VI with God the Father is ingenious but speculative. Many scholars (e.g., Sebastian de Grazia, Maurizio Viroli) read Machiavelli's praise of Borgia straightforwardly as admiration for political virtuosity combined with a cautionary tale about dependence on fortune. The "occult" theological reading requires accepting that Machiavelli wrote in a sustained code that only careful readers could detect -- a premise that, even if plausible for some passages, becomes strained when applied to the entire text. John Najemy's work on Machiavelli emphasizes the Republican commitments that sit uneasily with any reading centering princely or prophetic ambition. **Severity: moderate** -- this is one supporting argument among many, and the central thesis does not depend on it.

3. **Against the claim that Machiavelli replaces goodness with necessity tout court**
   Bi himself raises this counter-argument effectively in the interview: Machiavelli opens the Discourses by saying he wants to benefit mankind (a ciascuno), which seems irreducibly altruistic. Mansfield acknowledges a "humanity" in Machiavelli but reabsorbs it into the framework of necessity. However, thinkers like Erica Benner (in *Machiavelli's Ethics*) have argued that Machiavelli preserves a robust, if unconventional, ethical framework that cannot be reduced to necessity or acquisition. If Machiavelli retains genuine ethical commitments -- to republican liberty, to the common good, to human flourishing -- then the stark opposition between goodness and necessity that structures Mansfield's reading may be overdrawn. **Severity: moderate** -- this does not refute the central thesis but suggests a more nuanced Machiavelli than Mansfield presents.

4. **Against the claim that all subsequent modern philosophers are Machiavelli's "commissioners"**
   The idea that Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, and even Nietzsche are all operating under Machiavelli's dispensation is a grand narrative that risks flattening real differences. Hobbes's materialism and social contract theory have independent roots in Thucydides and Epicureanism. Locke's theory of property and natural rights draws on Scholastic natural law (Suarez, Grotius) as much as on any Machiavellian precedent. Rousseau's critique of civilization and celebration of natural sentiment represent a genuine departure, not merely a rebellion within the Machiavellian framework. To call all of modernity "Machiavellian" is to make the category so broad as to lose explanatory power. **Severity: significant** -- the thesis of Machiavelli as founder requires that "founder" not be diluted into mere "precursor" or "early influence."

5. **Against the treatment of Machiavelli's view of women as merely instrumental**
   Mansfield's discussion of women in Machiavelli is cursory: women are "not without power" because they can attract, and their unarmed power is analogous to Christ's prophetic power. This passes over the deeply problematic Chapter 25 of The Prince, where fortune is compared to a woman who must be beaten and conquered. Feminist scholars (Hanna Pitkin's *Fortune Is a Woman*) have shown that Machiavelli's gendered imagery is not incidental but structural to his conception of virtue as masculine domination of a feminized fortune/nature. The interview's light treatment of this dimension -- reducing it to "recreation" -- misses a significant strand of Machiavelli scholarship and a serious ethical problem in the texts. **Severity: moderate** -- this is a blind spot in the interview rather than a refutation of the central thesis.

6. **Against the claim that the effectual truth makes Machiavelli a philosopher rather than a political theorist**
   Mansfield insists that Machiavelli is "greatly underestimated" as a philosopher. But the effectual truth could be read not as a philosophical epistemology but as a practical maxim for political actors: attend to results, not ideals. This is closer to a methodological pragmatism than to a systematic ontology of truth. Isaiah Berlin's famous essay "The Originality of Machiavelli" argues that Machiavelli's significance lies in his recognition of the plurality of values, not in any single philosophical invention. If "effectual truth" is practical wisdom rather than ontological revolution, Machiavelli's claim to be the founder of modernity's philosophical framework is weakened. **Severity: moderate** -- the distinction between practical maxim and philosophical principle matters for the strength of the central claim.

7. **The interview neglects Islamic and non-Western philosophical traditions**
   Mansfield notes that Machiavelli admired Muhammad as a conqueror-prophet and that the Turks were stronger than Christians. But the interview does not engage with the substantial Islamic philosophical tradition (Averroes, Ibn Khaldun) that addressed many of the same questions about the relationship between philosophy, religion, and political power. Ibn Khaldun's cyclical theory of civilizations and his concept of asabiyyah (group solidarity) offer striking parallels to Machiavelli's thought and raise the question of whether Machiavelli's "founding" was as original as Mansfield claims. **Severity: minor** -- this is a gap in the conversation rather than a counter-argument to the thesis, but it reveals a Eurocentric framing.

## Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing

1. **"Modernity starts with Machiavelli, not with Descartes. Those people are derivative from Machiavelli."** (Mansfield)
   A provocative periodization claim that could anchor an essay on the intellectual genealogy of modernity. Worth developing by tracing the specific textual connections between Machiavelli and Descartes (did Descartes read Machiavelli? What evidence exists for or against direct influence?) and comparing with alternative genealogies (e.g., Hans Blumenberg's *The Legitimacy of the Modern Age*, which roots modernity in medieval nominalism).

2. **"Christianity changed the idea of the good into the idea of God. God is a personification of good."** (Mansfield, interpreting Machiavelli)
   A compact thesis about the relationship between philosophy and religion that connects Machiavelli to the Platonic tradition in a surprising way. Could ground an essay comparing Machiavelli's critique of Christianity-as-popularized-Platonism with Nietzsche's claim that "Christianity is Platonism for the people." Connects to Heidegger's reading of onto-theology.

3. **"The church is militant but not military."** (Mansfield, interpreting Machiavelli)
   A lapidary formulation of Machiavelli's critique of Christianity's structural weakness. Worth developing into an essay on the church-state distinction as Christianity's defining political problem -- from the Investiture Controversy through the Reformation to modern secularism. Connects to Carl Schmitt's *Political Theology* and its claim that modern political concepts are secularized theological concepts.

4. **"The prince becomes both conqueror like Caesar and prophet like Christ, the two brought together."** (Mansfield)
   The fusion of political and spiritual authority as Machiavelli's ideal. Could anchor an essay on the concept of the "armed prophet" and its legacy in revolutionary politics (Robespierre, Lenin, Mao). Connects to Eric Voegelin's analysis of modern political religions.

5. **"Uno solo -- one alone -- is the aim of the prince and the human goal itself, to be alone."** (Mansfield)
   A startling claim about the anthropology implied by Machiavelli's political theory. Worth developing as an essay on modern individualism's roots -- connecting Machiavelli's uno solo to Hobbes's state of nature, Tocqueville's "individualism" as a democratic pathology, and Hannah Arendt's analysis of loneliness as totalitarianism's precondition.

6. **"What you touch is more vulgar, more true than what you see. Truth is palpable, not so much visible."** (Mansfield)
   Mansfield's distinction between Machiavelli's appeal to touch and Plato/Aristotle's appeal to sight as rival epistemologies. A rich concept for an essay on the sensorium of political philosophy -- connecting to Martin Jay's *Downcast Eyes* on the denigration of vision in modern thought, and to the phenomenological tradition (Merleau-Ponty on touch and embodiment).

7. **"To anticipate a necessity is not quite to feel it in the same way as being moved by it urgently."** (Mansfield)
   The distinction between immediate and anticipated necessity introduces a temporal dimension to Machiavelli's thought. Could develop into an essay on prudence, time-horizons, and political judgment -- connecting to Reinhart Koselleck's *Futures Past* on how modernity changes the relationship between experience and expectation.

8. **"Acquisition does go beyond survival to glory."** (Mansfield)
   The claim that Machiavelli's concept of necessity transcends biological survival to encompass glory reframes the entire modern acquisitive project. Worth developing as an essay on the relationship between capitalism and glory -- connecting to Max Weber's *Protestant Ethic*, Thorstein Veblen's conspicuous consumption, and contemporary analyses of billionaire philanthropy as glory-seeking.

9. **"The effectual truth of the inventor of effectual truth is a world which believes in the effectual truth."** (Mansfield)
   A self-referential application of Machiavelli's principle to Machiavelli himself -- the philosopher's effect is the world he creates. Could ground an essay on performative philosophy: thinkers whose ideas are validated not by correspondence to reality but by reshaping reality. Connects to Marx's eleventh thesis on Feuerbach and to J.L. Austin's speech act theory.

10. **"Machiavelli creates a kind of oblivion following him. He makes people less concerned with the power of philosophy, because philosophy tells them that power is all they should have."** (Mansfield)
    The paradox that Machiavelli's philosophical triumph requires his own philosophical invisibility. Worth developing into an essay on the self-concealment of modern philosophy -- why the most influential thinker is the least recognized as a philosopher. Connects to Heidegger's concept of the "forgetting of Being" and to Whitehead's claim that the safest general characterization of the European philosophical tradition is that it consists of footnotes to Plato.

11. **"Modernity is the rebellion of reason against rational control."** (Mansfield)
    A paradoxical formulation: modernity is both rational control (Machiavelli's effectual truth applied systematically) and the rebellion against it (Rousseau, Nietzsche, the Romantics). Could anchor an essay on the dialectic of Enlightenment, connecting to Adorno and Horkheimer's thesis and to Charles Taylor's *Sources of the Self*.

12. **"The effectual truth still has to be measured by an ineffectual truth, so you can see what is success."** (Mansfield)
    Mansfield's deepest critical point: the effectual truth cannot ground itself. You need a normative standard that is not itself effectual to judge whether effects are good. This is a powerful argument for the indispensability of classical philosophy and could anchor an essay on the limits of pragmatism, consequentialism, and technocratic governance. Connects to Alasdair MacIntyre's *After Virtue* and its diagnosis of modern moral incoherence.

## Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas

**Ideas from Machiavelli (via the primary texts, as interpreted by Mansfield)**

- Christianity is the chief enemy because it locates honor in the next world, making this world derivative
- Christianity is simultaneously too cruel (demanding superhuman virtue) and too weak (unable to command armies)
- The "effectual truth" (verita effettuale) -- a thing is what its effect is, not what it is imagined to be
- Necessity replaces goodness as the basis of political and moral life
- Acquisition is the fundamental human drive, encompassing both material gain and glory
- The prince must be both conqueror (like Caesar) and prophet (like Christ)
- The unarmed prophet always loses; the armed prophet succeeds
- Muhammad is superior to Christ as a political model because he combined prophecy with conquest
- Rome's strength came from internal conflict between nobles and plebs, not from harmony
- Fortune must be overcome by virtue; the extreme situation defines the ordinary
- Conspiracy is the fundamental structure of power; all government is indirect
- The world is the only world -- modernity eliminates reference to another world
- The distinction between ordinary and extraordinary: the extreme case is the definer, not the outlier

**Ideas from Other Thinkers (cited by Mansfield or Bi)**

- *Plato*: The idea of the good as transcendent and invisible; the philosopher-king who rules reluctantly (Republic); beauty as a distraction from the invisible good. Mansfield uses Plato as the chief point of contrast with Machiavelli's epistemology.
- *Aristotle*: The eleven moral virtues unified by intellectual virtue (Nicomachean Ethics); the requirement of material surplus for virtuous life; four causes (efficient, material, formal, final); happiness as needing good fortune (NE I.8-10). Mansfield presents Aristotle's concession about fortune as the problem Machiavelli radicalizes.
- *Francis Bacon*: Credited with founding modern experimental science, but Mansfield argues he was following Machiavelli's lead. Bacon's reference to "a doctor in Italy named Nicholas Machiavelli" is cited as evidence of conscious debt. (The reference is from *The Advancement of Learning*, though Mansfield does not give the exact citation.)
- *Thomas Hobbes*: Credited with developing representation theory from Machiavelli's indirect government. The "state of nature" as a later formulation of Machiavelli's uno solo.
- *John Locke*: His phrase "industrious and rational" is cited. Locke's liberalism is presented as an attempt to correct Machiavelli by channeling acquisition into economics rather than politics.
- *Adam Smith*: His concept of self-interest is presented as a "degeneration" of Machiavelli's concept of acquisition -- domesticated, bourgeois, limited.
- *Nietzsche*: Compared with Machiavelli as a fellow critic of Christianity. Mansfield distinguishes them: Nietzsche emphasizes the ascetic ideal (strength turned against itself), while Machiavelli is more rational and more impressed by Christianity's actual power. The concept of "slave morality" is noted as present in both thinkers.
- *Rousseau*: Cited as a rebel within the Machiavellian framework who argues that the spiritual is more important than the material, and that the intentional truth is more important than the effectual truth.
- *Tocqueville*: His *Democracy in America* is cited for the observation that America is a consequence of its spirituality, and that the spiritual has more power than the material. Mansfield reads Tocqueville as wanting Machiavelli's spiritedness without his materialism.
- *Hegel*: Referenced by Bi via Axel Honneth's recognition theory and the master-slave dialectic. The point is that Machiavelli inverts Hegel: glory comes from the vulgar many, not from the recognition of worthy equals.
- *Leo Strauss*: Acknowledged as the inspiration for Mansfield's entire project. Strauss's *Thoughts on Machiavelli* is identified as the foundational work, with Mansfield claiming his own contribution is the political dimension that Strauss left for others.
- *Edmund Burke*: Cited for the idea that party could be based on principle, which Mansfield presents as a post-Machiavellian development.
- *Xenophon*: Mentioned as Machiavelli's favorite classical thinker, but not elaborated upon.

**Mansfield's Own Interpretive Contributions**

- The reading of "verita effettuale" as Machiavelli's singular philosophical invention, marked by its unique occurrence and lack of classical source -- this philological-hermeneutic claim is Mansfield's central scholarly contribution.
- The structural parallel between Cesare Borgia / Alexander VI and Christ / God the Father as a coded theological argument within The Prince.
- The argument that Machiavelli is the inventor of the modern concept of "fact" (via the shared etymology of factual/effectual) and therefore the father of modern science, not merely modern political science.
- The concept of the "perpetual republic" as Machiavelli's replacement for the classical cycle -- a succession of states united by shared philosophical principles rather than a single enduring polity.
- The framework of Machiavelli as "anti-Christ" who imitates the structure of Christianity to replace its content -- a systematic interpretive lens applied across both major works.
- The thesis that Machiavelli creates "oblivion" about his own power -- that the effectual truth of the effectual truth is a world that forgets its philosophical origins.
- The critical judgment that the effectual truth requires an "ineffectual" normative standard to evaluate itself -- Mansfield's own neo-classical position, derived from but not identical to Strauss's.
- The reading of Machiavelli's famous letter about entering ancient courts as containing the key word "solo" (alone) that connects even his contemplative life to the logic of uno solo.
- The interpretation of the church-state distinction as arising from Christianity's structural weakness (militant but not military), making it the cause of Italy's -- and by extension the world's -- disunion.

## Factual Notes Requiring Verification

1. **Bacon's reference to "a doctor in Italy named Nicholas Machiavelli"**: Mansfield attributes this to Bacon but does not give the exact source. The reference appears in *The Advancement of Learning* (1605), Book II, but the exact wording should be verified against the text. Some scholars attribute the quote to Bacon's essay "Of the True Greatness of Kingdoms and Estates" or to *De Augmentis Scientiarum*.

2. **"Verita effettuale" appears exactly once in all of Machiavelli's writings**: This is Mansfield's claim and is central to his argument. It should be verified by a comprehensive word search of Machiavelli's complete works, including the letters, *Mandragola*, *The Art of War*, and the *Florentine Histories*. The phrase does appear in *The Prince* Chapter 15, but the uniqueness claim requires checking the entire corpus.

3. **The three chapters in the Discourses where Machiavelli "openly criticizes Christianity"**: Mansfield does not specify these. The standard candidates are Discourses I.12 (on the role of religion in Rome vs. the corruption of the Church), II.2 (on how Christianity made the world weak), and II.5 (on how changes of religion efface the memory of the past). The identification should be verified.

4. **Savonarola was "burned to death in front of Machiavelli in Florence"**: Savonarola was hanged and then burned in the Piazza della Signoria in Florence on May 23, 1498. Machiavelli was in Florence at the time and became Second Chancellor of the Republic shortly after, but whether he personally witnessed the execution is a matter of historical inference rather than documented fact.

5. **The claim that there is no Greek word for "fact"**: This is a significant philosophical-linguistic claim. The Greek language has words like "pragma" (deed, thing done) and "ergon" (work, deed) that have some overlap with the concept of "fact." The claim may be that there is no exact Greek equivalent to the modern empiricist concept of "fact" as a brute particular, which is defensible but requires nuance.

6. **"Aristotle had four causes"**: This is accurate (Physics II.3, Metaphysics V.2), but the claim that modern science uses "just one" (the efficient cause) is a simplification. Modern science also relies heavily on material causation and, in some interpretations (teleological language in biology, for example), retains traces of final causation.

7. **The characterization of Xenophon as "Machiavelli's favorite classical thinker"**: This is a claim within the Straussian tradition (Strauss himself wrote extensively on Xenophon). The evidence typically cited includes Machiavelli's use of Xenophon's *Cyropaedia* and *Hiero*, but some scholars dispute the depth of Machiavelli's engagement with Xenophon relative to Livy, Polybius, or Plutarch.

8. **Churchill "changed his party when it was convenient"**: Churchill crossed the floor of the House of Commons twice -- from Conservative to Liberal in 1904, and from Liberal back to Conservative in 1924. Whether this was "Machiavellian" convenience or principled disagreement (he left the Conservatives over free trade) is a matter of historical interpretation.

9. **Mansfield's claim that Hobbes's concept of representation "comes out of" Machiavelli's indirect government**: This is an intellectual-historical argument that requires tracing Hobbes's sources. Hobbes's representation theory in *Leviathan* (Chapter 16) draws on theatrical metaphor and Roman law concepts of authorization. Direct influence from Machiavelli is plausible but contested.

10. **The dating of the concept of the "state of nature" to after Machiavelli**: Mansfield implies Machiavelli's uno solo anticipates the state of nature. The concept is usually traced to Hobbes (*Leviathan*, 1651), but proto-state-of-nature arguments appear in Lucretius and other ancient sources. Whether Machiavelli is a genuine precursor requires careful argumentation.

# Analysis: Machiavelli's Paradoxes — Glory, Violence, and the Difficulty of Pinning Down a Thinker

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zXUvEN-LI_M
- **Channel**: johnathan-bi
- **Analyzed**: 2026-02-25

## Central Claim

Johnathan Bi's core thesis in this Q&A session is that Machiavelli is a uniquely difficult thinker to pin down because his method of oscillating between extreme positions is itself a deliberate intellectual strategy, and that this strategy reveals truths about politics that more systematic thinkers miss. Bi argues that Machiavelli's apparent contradictions — ranking founders above philosophers and then reversing the ranking, praising brutal princely action while championing rule of law, celebrating disorder while seeking order — are not signs of confusion but of a proto-scientific method that tests ideas at their extremes to expose where power actually originates. This framing allows Bi to position himself between the major scholarly camps: Harvey Mansfield's reading of Machiavelli as a "teacher of evil" and progenitor of modern political science, and Maurizio Viroli's reading of him as a genuine patriot and even a genuine Christian. Bi's own interpretive position is that Machiavelli was simultaneously seeking employment, advancing a genuine intellectual innovation, and expressing authentic patriotism — and that these motivations are not mutually exclusive. The ambition of the argument is considerable: Bi is trying to show that Machiavelli's thought is not merely historically important but constitutively relevant to modernity because his insight that states must embrace dynamic change rather than seek static stability anticipates the modern valorization of innovation and disruption. The Q&A format allows Bi to develop several subsidiary claims about glory vs. fame, the proper role of violence in political life, the distinction between tumults and divisions, and the conditions under which extraordinary political action is justified.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Machiavelli's oscillation between extremes is a deliberate method, not inconsistency.** Bi argues that Machiavelli's habit of asserting a position and then asserting its opposite — ranking founders above philosophers in Book I of the Discourses, then suggesting the philosopher may have the greatest glory in the preface to Book II — reflects a "mathematical" or "scientific" intuition that extreme cases reveal more than ordinary ones. Just as experiments push variables to their limits, Machiavelli pushes political ideas to their extremes to expose the underlying mechanics of power. This is the foundational argument of the session because it provides a hermeneutic key for reading all of Machiavelli's apparent contradictions as productive rather than disqualifying. The argument is analogical in structure: it draws a parallel between scientific experimentation and Machiavelli's method of political analysis.

2. **The scholarship on Machiavelli is uniquely divided, which itself evidences the difficulty of interpretation.** Bi cites the polar opposition between Harvey Mansfield, who reads Machiavelli as "the anti-Christ" and a "teacher of evil period, no qualification," and a scholar (Maurizio Viroli, named later) who reads him as "a genuine patriot and a genuine Christian." Bi uses this scholarly divide not merely as context but as evidence for his claim that Machiavelli's writing style deliberately produces interpretive difficulty. The logic is inductive: from the empirical fact of extreme scholarly disagreement, Bi infers that something in the text itself generates this disagreement, and identifies it as Machiavelli's method of extremes.

3. **The Prince is best read as addressing "strong leaders" generally, not just rulers of principalities.** Bi argues against the reading of The Prince as either a sarcastic critique of princely power (Rousseau's reading) or as incompatible with the republican Discourses. His resolution is that "prince" in Machiavelli's usage means a strong founder or re-founder, a role that is necessary even within republics. He cites Cesare Borgia's role in the Romagna as evidence: establishing civil law required a strong dictator. This argument functions to dissolve the apparent contradiction between The Prince and the Discourses and supports the central claim that Machiavelli's apparent inconsistencies resolve under the right interpretive lens. The argument is deductive: if "prince" means "strong founder-leader" rather than "ruler of a principality," then The Prince and the Discourses address complementary rather than contradictory subjects.

4. **Machiavelli distinguishes genuine glory from mere fame, and this distinction has both selfish and altruistic dimensions.** Bi argues that Machiavelli separates fame — what Caesar had, and which Machiavelli condemns because Caesar destroyed the republic — from genuine glory, which involves both a great deed of founding or refounding and the desire for recognition. Bi insists this duality is irreducible: glory is "extremely altruistic because it's doing a great deed" but "also deeply selfish because it's their reward that they're seeking." He invokes Adam Smith's Theory of Moral Sentiments to support this point: "we not only want to be sympathized with, but sympathize-able." The argument is rhetorical and analogical, drawing on Smith to suggest that the selfish-altruistic duality in glory is a general feature of human motivation.

5. **The "Machiavelli Paradox": those willing to speak about the darkness of power wield it more benevolently.** An audience member names what Bi acknowledges as his own coinage. The paradox is that leaders who openly confront the brutal requirements of power tend to exercise it more humanely than those who refuse to acknowledge those requirements. Bi connects this to Machiavelli's personal character and to the Jungian concept of shadow integration, suggesting Machiavelli had "made peace with whatever shadow that he's had." The argument is primarily rhetorical and draws on psychological intuition rather than textual evidence.

6. **Machiavelli is a pivotal figure in the intellectual history of innovation.** Bi claims that classical political philosophy was suspicious of change, equating it with chaos, while modernity celebrates change under the name "innovation" or "disruption." He identifies Machiavelli as "a key link in that flip," citing Benoit Godin's book Innovation Contested, which traced the intellectual history of the word "innovation" and found that Machiavelli (along with Livy) was one of the only classical-era writers to give innovation a positive connotation. This is an inductive-historical argument that places Machiavelli at a hinge point in Western intellectual history.

7. **Machiavelli's ideal political vision is competitive pluralism, not world empire.** Bi argues that Machiavelli criticizes Rome precisely because it became too large and comfortable, leading to collapse. Instead, Machiavelli's ideal is "a bunch of states who are all manned by these kind of princely figures that kind of push up against each other" — healthy competition between states, including occasional wars, to keep "the spiritual life healthy." Bi then qualifies this by arguing that this vision is no longer viable in modernity due to the nature of modern warfare. The argument is interpretive and draws on the Discourses' treatment of Roman expansion.

8. **Extraordinary political action should always attempt to work through legal means.** Bi argues that despite Machiavelli's emphasis on extraordinary founding and refounding acts, Machiavelli consistently insists on using legal channels where possible. He cites Machiavelli's distinction between tumults (class conflict within legal bounds that kept Rome free) and divisions (factional conflict that destroys states), and the example of Brutus executing his sons through legal process during the founding of the Roman Republic. The logical structure is a qualification of the broader argument: Machiavelli embraces extraordinary action but constrains it within institutional frameworks wherever possible.

9. **The domain of expansion has shifted from military-geopolitical to technological and commercial.** Responding to a question about AGI and expansion, Bi argues that Machiavelli's principle that expansion is the only viable strategy remains valid in modernity, but the domain has changed. He claims that tech founders exercise "similar appetites" to historical conquerors: "if they were born in a different age, they would have been the conquistadors." This is an analogical argument that translates Machiavelli's framework into contemporary terms.

10. **Machiavelli would endorse manufacturing necessity through legal means.** Responding to a question about whether Machiavelli would support leaders staging crises (like a hypothetical foreknowledge of Pearl Harbor), Bi answers affirmatively but qualifies: even extraordinary action should be pursued through legal channels where possible. He translates this into business terms — "not like beating up your employee... it's like making the performance reviews a bit more brutal." The argument is interpretive and relies on Bi's synthesis of multiple passages from the Discourses.

## Evidence Quality Assessment

**Strengths**

- Bi demonstrates genuine familiarity with the primary texts, referencing specific structural features of the Discourses (the ranking of founders vs. philosophers in Book I vs. the preface to Book II, the chapter on tumults vs. divisions in Book I, Cesare Borgia's role in the Romagna).
- He accurately represents the major poles of Machiavelli scholarship, correctly identifying Harvey Mansfield and Maurizio Viroli as representing opposing interpretive traditions, and correctly characterizing Mansfield's reading as seeing Machiavelli as the originator of modern political philosophy.
- The citation of Benoit Godin's Innovation Contested as a history of the word "innovation" is a genuinely useful and less commonly cited scholarly reference that adds intellectual depth.
- Bi is admirably honest about the limits of his own interpretive confidence, repeatedly noting "I'm not totally sure that my reading here is right" and "I don't know if it's the right reading." This epistemic humility is itself a form of scholarly rigor.
- The biographical details about Machiavelli — the Mandragola written during exile and torture, the card-playing anecdote from the famous letter to Vettori, the dream of choosing hell over heaven — are accurate and effectively deployed.

**Weaknesses**

- The transcript is auto-generated (Whisper), resulting in systematic misspellings ("Machuveli," "Maccubally," "Maccivelli," "Chesare and Romero" for Cesare Borgia, "Calakles" for likely Callicles or Thrasymachus, "strouse" for Strauss, "Benwago Din" for Benoit Godin). This makes some references ambiguous.
- Bi's claim that Machiavelli uses a "mathematical" or "scientific" method of testing ideas at extremes is asserted rather than argued. No specific passage is cited where Machiavelli describes his own method in these terms, and the analogy to scientific experimentation is Bi's own overlay.
- The discussion of Machiavelli's shadow in Jungian terms is anachronistic and lacks textual grounding. The concept of shadow integration is imported from 20th-century depth psychology and applied without justification.
- Several historical claims are made in passing without citation or specificity — e.g., "Sparta froze her laws for 800 years," Florence being excommunicated by Rome (no date or specific conflict identified).
- The Q&A format means many arguments are developed only partially before being interrupted by the next question. The argument about glory vs. fame, for instance, never reaches a clear conclusion.
- Bi conflates "Callicles" (from the Gorgias) and "Thrasymachus" (from the Republic) — he says "I think it was Calakles who also has this more realpolitik kind of view" about someone "in the Republic." Thrasymachus is the character in the Republic who argues that justice is the advantage of the stronger; Callicles appears in the Gorgias. This is a notable error for a philosophy lecturer.

## Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots

1. **The "method of extremes" reading may be too charitable.** Bi's central hermeneutic claim — that Machiavelli deliberately oscillates between positions as a kind of proto-scientific method — risks being unfalsifiable. If every contradiction is explained as deliberate methodology, then no textual evidence could ever count against this reading. A more parsimonious explanation, advanced by scholars like John Najemy in his work on Machiavelli's political thought, is that the Discourses were composed over an extended period and reflect genuine changes in Machiavelli's views rather than a unified method. Severity: moderate — this does not undermine the reading entirely but suggests Bi is selecting one interpretive possibility and presenting it as more settled than it is.

2. **The claim that Machiavelli conceives of himself as doing "something completely unique" in theorizing classical political action understates his debts.** Bi argues that Machiavelli saw himself as the first to put the "actual principles of classical action in a theory," but this overlooks the substantial tradition of mirrors-for-princes literature, practical political advice manuals, and humanist historiography that preceded Machiavelli. Writers like Pontano, Patrizi, and even Petrarch engaged with practical political wisdom. What distinguishes Machiavelli is not that he theorized political action for the first time but the specific way he broke with the moralized framing of that tradition. Severity: minor — Bi likely knows this but oversimplifies for the Q&A format.

3. **The analogy between tech founders and historical conquerors is superficial.** Bi's claim that "most founders wouldn't agree to this, but I think if they were born in a different age, they would have been the conquistadors" conflates very different forms of ambition, risk, and moral consequence. Building a company in a regulated market economy — even a ruthlessly competitive one — involves fundamentally different moral stakes than military conquest involving killing, enslavement, and territorial dispossession. The analogy flatters tech founders by associating them with historical grandeur while obscuring the violence of actual conquest. Thinkers like Hannah Arendt, in The Human Condition, carefully distinguish between different modes of human activity (labor, work, action) precisely to prevent this kind of conflation. Severity: moderate — this is a recurring blind spot in "Machiavelli for founders" discourse.

4. **The treatment of violence as differing merely quantitatively is philosophically thin.** Bi's suggestion that the difference between beneficial tumults and destructive divisions may be "just a quantitative question" — how much violence rather than what kind — ignores a major strand of political philosophy that insists on qualitative distinctions between types of political conflict. Arendt's On Violence, for example, argues that violence and power are categorically different phenomena, not points on a spectrum. Even within the Machiavelli scholarship, scholars like John McCormick (Machiavellian Democracy) argue that Machiavelli's distinction between tumults and divisions is precisely qualitative: tumults involve institutional contestation between classes, while divisions involve factional warfare that bypasses institutions. Severity: moderate — this simplification undermines one of the more interesting analytical threads in the Q&A.

5. **The Jungian "shadow" framing is anachronistic and distracting.** The audience member's question about Machiavelli's "shadow" and Bi's engagement with it imports a 20th-century psychological framework that has no obvious connection to Machiavelli's own conceptual vocabulary or to the Renaissance intellectual context. Machiavelli does not think in terms of unconscious psychological dynamics; his analysis of self-deception and moral compromise operates in entirely different registers (fortuna, virtu, necessita). Applying Jungian categories to a 16th-century thinker without justification risks projecting modern assumptions onto a fundamentally different intellectual world. Severity: minor as a scholarly matter, but it signals a tendency in Bi's presentation to blend self-help psychology with political philosophy in ways that can flatten both.

6. **The claim about Thucydides as Machiavelli's intellectual predecessor, while plausible, skips major intermediary figures.** Bi names Thucydides and a figure "in the Republic" (likely Thrasymachus or Callicles) as intellectual predecessors, but omits Polybius, whose Histories and theory of anacyclosis (the cyclical degeneration of regimes) are a direct and acknowledged source for Machiavelli's Discourses. This is a significant omission given that Bi's Q&A repeatedly touches on cyclical theories of political decay. Severity: moderate — the omission of Polybius in a discussion of Machiavelli's intellectual lineage is a notable gap.

7. **The claim that Machiavelli "really" values rule of law, freedom, and egalitarianism may overstate the republican dimension.** Bi repeatedly insists that "people don't appreciate about Machiavelli" his commitment to rule of law. While this is a defensible reading (supported by scholars like Quentin Skinner and Philip Pettit), it risks overcorrecting against the "teacher of evil" reading to the point of domesticating Machiavelli. Machiavelli's concept of freedom (liberta) is not liberal freedom; it is closer to non-domination in a republican sense, and his egalitarianism is instrumental (inequality breeds faction) rather than principled. Severity: minor — Bi likely recognizes these distinctions but does not articulate them in the Q&A.

## Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing

1. **"I love my fatherland more than my soul."** Attributed by Bi to the Florentine response to papal excommunication, later adopted by Machiavelli. This quote encapsulates the Machiavellian willingness to sacrifice eternal salvation for political commitment. It could ground an essay on the secularization of political virtue — the moment when civic duty supersedes religious obligation, and what that means for the foundations of modern political morality. Connects to Weber's "Politics as a Vocation" and its concept of the ethic of responsibility vs. the ethic of conviction.

2. **"The extreme form of an idea is able to tell us more than its ordinary form."** Bi's formulation of Machiavelli's method. This is a powerful epistemological claim that could be developed into an essay on political philosophy's relationship to limit cases — how thinkers from Machiavelli to Carl Schmitt (the exception reveals the norm) to Rawls (the original position as a thought experiment) use extreme or artificial scenarios to illuminate political truths. The connection to scientific methodology is also worth pursuing.

3. **"We call stability stagnation. And instead, we want innovation."** Bi's claim about the inversion of classical political values in modernity. This could generate an essay on the genealogy of innovation as a political value, drawing on Godin's Innovation Contested and tracing the line from Machiavelli's valorization of change through Bacon, the Enlightenment, Schumpeter's "creative destruction," and Silicon Valley's "disruption." The question of whether this inversion is a genuine philosophical advance or a loss of classical wisdom is rich material.

4. **"Fame is what Caesar had... genuine glory is both a desire that is extremely altruistic... but also deeply selfish."** The fame/glory distinction, as Bi reads it, could anchor an essay on the moral psychology of political ambition. The claim that glory is irreducibly dual — that founders want both the deed and the recognition, and that neither alone is satisfying — connects to Hegel's master-slave dialectic, Smith's Theory of Moral Sentiments, and contemporary debates about effective altruism and moral motivation.

5. **"If they were born in a different age, they would have been the conquistadors."** Bi's claim about tech founders. This provocative analogy deserves scrutiny as an essay topic: is the expansionary drive of modern technology companies genuinely analogous to military conquest, or does the analogy romanticize one and sanitize the other? This connects to Peter Thiel's explicitly Machiavellian/Straussian framing of competition and monopoly.

6. **"Tumults were one of the reasons that Rome kept free... division is the death of states."** The tumult/division distinction is one of Machiavelli's most original contributions to political theory and could ground an essay on productive vs. destructive political conflict. This connects directly to contemporary debates about polarization, the role of protest, and whether democratic conflict has crossed from tumult into division. McCormick's Machiavellian Democracy is the key secondary source.

7. **"Always, always try going the legal route. Because even going extra-legal once destroys the institution that requires so much violence to establish."** This underappreciated dimension of Machiavelli's thought could generate an essay on Machiavelli as a theorist of institutional fragility — the idea that legal institutions are extraordinarily costly to create and extraordinarily easy to destroy, which is why even someone who endorses extraordinary founding violence insists on ordinary legal process thereafter. Connects to Douglass North's institutional economics and to contemporary concerns about democratic backsliding.

8. **"He wrote the Mandragola, which is considered by some to be the greatest Italian comedy, when he was exiled, humiliated, and he was just tortured."** The biographical fact of Machiavelli writing comedy during personal catastrophe could anchor an essay on the relationship between suffering and creativity, or more specifically on humor as a philosophical stance. Machiavelli's letter to Vettori (December 10, 1513) is one of the great documents of Western literature, and its mixture of bawdy humor, political despair, and intellectual ambition deserves extended treatment.

9. **"The Machiavelli Paradox: those willing to speak about the darkness of power wield it more benevolently."** Bi's own coinage. Whether or not it holds up as a general principle, it could generate an essay on the relationship between moral realism and moral outcomes in political leadership. Does unflinching acknowledgment of political evil lead to better governance? This connects to Reinhold Niebuhr's Christian realism, Isaiah Berlin's essay on Machiavelli, and the realist tradition in international relations theory.

10. **"We not only want to be sympathized with, but sympathize-able."** Bi's paraphrase of Adam Smith's Theory of Moral Sentiments. The connection between Smith and Machiavelli on the dual nature of moral motivation — wanting both to be good and to be seen as good — is underexplored in the scholarship and could ground a comparative essay on the moral psychology of recognition in early modern thought.

## Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas

**Ideas from Machiavelli (via the lecture/Q&A)**

- The ranking of founders above philosophers (Discourses, Book I), with a possible reversal in the preface to Book II suggesting the philosopher's glory may be greatest
- The distinction between fame (Caesar's destructive legacy) and genuine glory (founding or refounding that creates lasting political order)
- The claim "I love my fatherland more than my soul" — originating from the Florentine response to papal excommunication, adopted by Machiavelli in his correspondence
- The dream anecdote: preferring hell (with Achilles, Cyrus, Plato, Aristotle) to heaven (with "kind peasants")
- The Prince as a handbook for strong leaders/founders, not only rulers of principalities; Cesare Borgia as the example of a strong dictator needed to establish civil law in the Romagna
- The distinction between tumults (healthy political conflict that preserved Roman freedom) and divisions (destructive factional warfare)
- The preference for impetuousness over caution; the metaphor of "surfing the waves" of political fortune
- The insistence on using legal means even for extraordinary political action; the example of Brutus executing his sons legally during the founding of the Republic
- The criticism of Rome for becoming too large and comfortable, leading to collapse
- The ideal of competitive pluralism: multiple states led by virtuous-vicious leaders pushing against each other

**Ideas from Other Thinkers (cited by Bi)**

- **Harvey Mansfield** — Reads Machiavelli as "the anti-Christ," a "teacher of evil," and the progenitor of all modern political science and theory. Mansfield also reads the Discourses Book II preface as Machiavelli "defrauding the political founders, making them think they have the greatest glory in order to execute his will." Source: Mansfield's Machiavelli's Virtue and his translation/commentary on The Prince. Bi's attribution appears accurate.
- **Maurizio Viroli** — Reads Machiavelli as "a genuine patriot and a genuine Christian." - **Leo Strauss** — Referenced as having "went to town on Machiavelli" and as the source of the esoteric/numerological reading tradition. Source: Strauss's Thoughts on Machiavelli (1958). Bi correctly identifies this as "the canonical work" on Machiavelli from the Straussian tradition. Note: the transcript renders "Strauss" as "strouse" due to auto-transcription.
- **Rousseau** — Cited as reading The Prince as "a sarcastic, ironic critique of princely power." This is a well-known attribution from Rousseau's Social Contract (Book III, Chapter 6). Bi rejects this reading.
- **Adam Smith** — Cited for the claim from the Theory of Moral Sentiments that "we not only want to be sympathized with, but sympathize-able" (i.e., worthy of sympathy). This is a reasonable paraphrase of Smith's argument about the desire for praiseworthiness, not just praise (TMS, Part III).
- **Benoit Godin** — Cited for Innovation Contested, a book tracing the intellectual history of the word "innovation." Bi claims Godin found that Machiavelli and Livy were among the only classical-period writers to use "innovation" with a positive connotation. Note: the transcript renders the name as "Benwago Din."
- **Plato** — Referenced for the idea that the soul is the greatest thing to sacrifice, and for the general classical suspicion of political change. Also referenced via the Republic character (likely Thrasymachus or Callicles from the Gorgias) who holds a realpolitik view.
- **Nietzsche** — Cited as "extremely similar to Machiavelli in all these different ways" and as someone who "really elevated Thucydides." This is accurate: Nietzsche praises Thucydides in Twilight of the Idols ("What I Owe to the Ancients," section 2).
- **Michael Dell** (via David Senra's interview) — Cited by an audience member for the practice of "injecting necessity" — creating crises when none exist to maintain organizational vigor.

**Bi's Own Interpretive Contributions**

- **The "Machiavelli Paradox"** — Bi's own coinage: those who openly confront the darkness of power exercise it more benevolently than those who deny it.
- **The "method of extremes" reading** — The interpretation that Machiavelli's oscillation between contradictory positions is a deliberate epistemological method analogous to scientific experimentation, not mere inconsistency.
- **The positioning between Mansfield and Viroli** — Bi's own synthetic reading places Machiavelli somewhere between the "teacher of evil" and "genuine patriot" poles, combining genuine intellectual innovation with job-seeking and patriotism.
- **The domain-shift thesis** — The argument that Machiavelli's expansionary imperative remains valid but has shifted domains from military-geopolitical to technological-commercial, and that modern founders are psychologically equivalent to historical conquerors.
- **The modernity qualification** — Bi's argument that Machiavelli's ideal of competitive state pluralism is "not possible today" due to the nature of modern warfare, which means we must translate his insights rather than apply them directly.
- **The Machiavelli-as-joyful-figure emphasis** — Drawing on Viroli's work but developing it into a personal interpretive stance: Machiavelli as someone who uniquely combined ambition for immortal glory with genuine enjoyment of worldly pleasures, making him distinctively appealing as a human being.

## Factual Notes Requiring Verification

1. **"Sparta froze her laws for 800 years."** This is a common but contested claim. The Spartan system attributed to Lycurgus endured for several centuries, but the claim of 800 years of unchanged laws is an exaggeration. The Spartan constitution underwent significant changes, particularly after the Peloponnesian War. Plutarch's Life of Lycurgus is the main source, and even Plutarch notes uncertainty about the dating.

2. **"Florence as a whole was excommunicated by Rome."** Florence was placed under interdict by Pope Sixtus IV in 1478 during the aftermath of the Pazzi conspiracy, and there were other instances of papal-Florentine conflict. The specific incident Bi references and its connection to the phrase "we love our fatherland more than our souls" needs verification. The phrase appears in Machiavelli's letter of April 16, 1527 to Vettori, though Bi himself notes Machiavelli "actually didn't exactly write that in his letter."

3. **"The Mandragola is considered by some to be the greatest Italian comedy."** This is a widely held view in literary scholarship, though "greatest" is obviously subjective. It is routinely described as one of the masterpieces of Renaissance Italian theatre.

4. **"He wrote it to entertain a Medici wedding."** Bi says the Mandragola was written "to entertain, I think, a Medici wedding." The dating and occasion of the Mandragola's first performance are debated. Some scholars date it to 1518, others to 1520. The claim that it was written for a Medici wedding is one hypothesis but not established fact.

5. **"Calakles" in the Republic with a realpolitik view.** This appears to conflate two characters. Thrasymachus in the Republic (Book I) argues that justice is the advantage of the stronger. Callicles in the Gorgias argues that the strong should rule and natural justice favors the powerful. Neither character's name matches "Calakles" exactly, but the philosophical position described fits both. The error is placing Callicles in the Republic rather than the Gorgias.

6. **Nietzsche "elevated Thucydides."** Accurate. Nietzsche praises Thucydides in Twilight of the Idols: "My recreation, my preference, my cure from all Platonism has always been Thucydides" (Section 2, "What I Owe to the Ancients"). The connection between Nietzsche and Machiavelli is well-established in scholarship (e.g., Don Dombowsky, Nietzsche and Napoleon).

7. **Benoit Godin's Innovation Contested and the claim about Machiavelli and positive connotations of innovation.** Godin's book (2015) does trace the history of the concept of innovation. The specific claim that Machiavelli and Livy were the only classical-period writers to give "innovation" a positive connotation should be verified against Godin's text, as Bi appears to be paraphrasing from memory.

8. **Harvey Mansfield's reading of the Discourses Book II preface as Machiavelli "defrauding the political founders."** This is consistent with Mansfield's interpretation in Machiavelli's Virtue (1996) and his introduction to his translation of the Discourses, but the specific formulation should be verified.

9. **The Pearl Harbor hypothetical.** Bi presents a hypothetical in which the president knew about Pearl Harbor in advance. This echoes actual conspiracy theories about FDR's foreknowledge, which mainstream historians reject. Bi uses it purely as a hypothetical, but the framing could be misleading.

10. **"Brutus killed his sons in the founding of the Republic... he did it legally."** This refers to Lucius Junius Brutus, who according to Livy (Ab Urbe Condita, Book II) presided over the execution of his own sons for conspiring to restore the Tarquin monarchy. Machiavelli discusses this in Discourses III.3. The characterization of this as "legal" reflects Machiavelli's framing; whether it was genuinely legal in a modern sense is another question, as Brutus was both judge and father.

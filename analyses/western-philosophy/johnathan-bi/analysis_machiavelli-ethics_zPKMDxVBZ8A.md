# Analysis: Machiavelli's Invitation Into Evil — Why Good People Make Terrible Leaders

- **Video**: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zPKMDxVBZ8A
- **Channel**: Johnathan Bi
- **Analyzed**: 2026-02-25

---

## Central Claim

Johnathan Bi argues that Machiavelli's political philosophy is not a cynical endorsement of evil for its own sake but a radical thesis about what political founding actually requires: that the achievement of genuinely good political ends — stability, rule of law, freedom, equality — demands a willingness to commit acts that are, by conventional moral standards, evil. The lecture's core thesis is that Machiavelli writes not for "good" people (the altruistic, compassionate, and public-spirited) but for "great" people — those driven by a lust for glory robust enough to survive the psychological toll of necessary evil. Bi frames this through the figure of Moses as Machiavelli's chief exemplar: a leader who ordered an indiscriminate massacre and lied about divine authorization, yet is praised precisely because these acts were necessary to found Israel and establish the Ten Commandments. The lecture is distinctive in its four-part rhetorical architecture — easing the audience into Machiavelli's position through Moses (Part 1), qualifying the invitation to evil with three caveats (Part 2), contextualizing Machiavelli against classical and Christian traditions (Part 3), and then offering a Platonic counter-argument before showing how Machiavelli would respond (Part 4). The scope is ambitious: Bi attempts to rehabilitate Machiavelli not merely as a realist but as an idealist whose willingness to prescribe evil measures the depth of his commitment to the good.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Moses as the paradigm of "entering into evil"**
Bi opens with the Exodus 32 episode — the golden calf, the Levite massacre of 3,000 Israelites — to argue that even the most saintly political figure in the Judeo-Christian tradition resorted to indiscriminate violence to establish political order. The argument functions rhetorically as a disarming move: if Moses did it, ordinary moral squeamishness about political violence is self-indulgent. Machiavelli's point, as Bi reads it, is not to expose Moses but to empower the reader — "who the hell are you to be so damn precious about your morality?" The logic is analogical: if the holiest founder lied and killed, then lying and killing are constitutive features of founding itself, not aberrations. This is the lecture's central anchor, and every subsequent argument radiates from it.

2. **Moses lied about divine authorization for the massacre**
Drawing on Machiavelli scholar Maurizio Viroli (rendered in the transcript as "Maritzo Veroli"), Bi argues that God never actually commanded the Levite massacre. The evidence is threefold: (a) the text says "Moses said unto them, thus sayeth the Lord" rather than the narrator stating "thus spoke the Lord"; (b) there was no time for God to issue the command between relenting and Moses descending; (c) God subsequently plagues the people for the calf, suggesting the massacre was not already divine punishment. This is a close-reading argument that gives the lecture scholarly depth. It connects to the central claim by showing that political founding requires not only violence but deception — specifically, the instrumentalization of religion.

3. **The armed prophet thesis — violence as the necessary supplement to moral authority**
Bi presents Machiavelli's doctrine that "all the armed prophets conquered and the unarmed ones were ruined." Religious or moral authority alone is insufficient for political founding; it must be backed by force. The argument is deductive: people's belief is unstable; when belief fails, force must compel obedience; therefore prophets who lack arms will fail. Moses's religious authority made him a prophet; the massacre made him a founder. This is one of Machiavelli's most famous claims and Bi presents it with fidelity to the source text.

4. **Indiscriminate violence as pedagogy — cruelty as the only effective teacher for a corrupt people**
Bi argues that the massacre was indiscriminate rather than targeted because Moses's goal was not judicial punishment of the guilty but a "public and uncompromising display of violence" that would teach the entire people a "memorable lesson." The logic is consequentialist: the Israelites were so corrupt (idolatry 40 days after God's direct command) that proportional justice would have been inadequate. Only excessive, spectacular violence could recalibrate their behavior. Bi connects this to the Remirro de Orco episode — "the ferocity of this spectacle left the people at once satisfied and stupefied" — showing that Machiavelli applies the same logic across different historical cases.

5. **The Roman augury analogy — the prudent instrumentalization of religion**
Bi tells the story of three Roman generals and their chickens: the pious general who obeys the chickens, the impious general who openly defies them and loses, and the clever general who lies about the augury and kills the dissenting priest. The argument is analogical: Moses, like the clever general, preserved the "outward appearance of piety while doing what was politically necessary." This detour serves a structural function — it generalizes the Moses case from a singular biblical event to a recurring pattern in Machiavelli's political thought: religion is the strongest force for justifying "otherwise difficult to establish deeds," and the wise ruler must know how to manipulate it.

6. **Wars of command versus wars of habitation — the logic of elimination**
Bi presents Machiavelli's taxonomy from Discourses 2.8: wars of command (seeking obedience from others) can and should be waged humanely, but wars of habitation (seeking to displace and settle) require the elimination of native inhabitants. The logic follows from Machiavelli's principle that "men should either be caressed or eliminated, because they avenge themselves for slight injuries but cannot do so for grave ones." In wars of habitation, you inevitably injure the inhabitants gravely (taking their land, killing their combatants), but not gravely enough to prevent revenge — unless you go all the way. Bi is careful to note that "elimination" need not always mean killing (Roman dispersion, forced assimilation), but means removing the capacity for coordinated vengeance. The argument connects back to Moses via Joshua's conquest of the Promised Land.

7. **The Samnite cautionary tale — the catastrophe of the middle way**
Bi recounts the Battle of the Caudine Forks: the Samnites trap the entire Roman army, and the elder Herennius Pontius advises either releasing them with full honors (to make allies) or killing them all (to eliminate the threat). The Samnite commander takes a middle way — humiliation and a forced treaty — and the Romans eventually destroy the Samnites entirely. The argument is inductive, drawn from historical example, and functions as a negative proof of Machiavelli's thesis: half-measures in matters of existential political conflict are the worst possible strategy. Bi humorously invokes Mobb Deep's "there is no such thing as halfway crooks" to underscore the point.

8. **Three qualifications: evil for the good, extraordinary circumstances, and idealism**
Bi structures Part 2 around three caveats that make Machiavelli "more digestible." First, every evil is justified only in service of a greater good — and Machiavelli's conception of the good (stability, rule of law, freedom, equality) largely aligns with modern liberal values. Second, the invitation to evil applies only to extraordinary moments (founding and refounding), extraordinary people, and extraordinary positions of power — not to ordinary political life. Third, Machiavelli is not merely a realist but an idealist: he believes in the "great man theory of history" and writes for those rare individuals who can reshape the course of events. These qualifications are rhetorically effective but also substantive: they distinguish Machiavelli from mere cynicism or nihilism.

9. **Machiavellian virtue as ordering force guided by prudence**
Bi explains Machiavelli's radical redefinition of virtue: no longer moral excellence (as in the classical or Christian traditions) but an "ordering force guided by prudence" — the combination of the lion (raw power) and the fox (cunning). The centaur Chiron — half man, half beast — is Machiavelli's image: man represents law, beast represents force, and the prince must know how to use both natures. Prudence is the classical virtue that survives most intact in Machiavelli, because it determines when to deploy conventional virtues and when to deploy conventional vices. The opposite of virtue is not vice but effeminacy — being "helplessly pushed around by fortune." This section effectively places Machiavelli's moral vocabulary in historical context, showing how far he departs from both classical and Christian frameworks.

10. **The Machiavelli paradox and the psychology of glory**
Bi observes that Machiavelli himself was an exemplary public servant — loyal, incorruptible, patriotic — despite writing books that seem to endorse ruthlessness. This "paradox" is resolved by the second qualification: Machiavelli was an extraordinary man but not in an extraordinary position of power, so he engaged in "ordinary mode" politics. More deeply, Bi argues that Machiavelli writes not for "good" people but for "great" people driven by glory. Altruism and compassion are too fragile to survive the psychological toll of necessary evil; a lust for glory is "brutal and forceful enough" to persist. Glory bridges self-interest and public benefit — the glory-seeker's reputation depends on actually benefiting the republic. This is the lecture's most original and philosophically interesting argument.

11. **Bi's Platonic counter-argument and Machiavelli's response**
In Part 4, Bi channels Plato's Gorgias: isn't it better to suffer injustice than to do it, since doing injustice corrupts the soul? Bi then constructs Machiavelli's multi-layered response: (a) political life should focus on basics (stability, freedom) rather than importing higher philosophical ideals; (b) necessary evil need not scar the soul if one genuinely believes it was necessary (citing Truman on Hiroshima); (c) glory, not altruism, is the proper motivational foundation for political action; and (d) ultimately, Machiavelli would say "I love my fatherland more than my soul" — willingness to risk corruption for one's country is itself a form of selflessness. The final rhetorical move is powerful: Bi concludes that the difference between himself and Machiavelli is "not my aversion towards evil, but his dedication to the good."

## Evidence Quality Assessment

**Strengths**

- **Primary text engagement**: Bi quotes Machiavelli directly and at length from both The Prince and the Discourses on Livy, demonstrating genuine familiarity with the source material. Key passages — the armed prophets doctrine, the cruelty well-used/badly-used distinction, the Chiron metaphor, the fortune-as-woman passage — are accurately cited and contextually appropriate.
- **Biblical close reading**: The Exodus 32 analysis is impressively detailed, with Bi tracking the sequence of events (God's relenting, Moses's descent, the massacre, God's subsequent plague) to build a textual case that Moses lied. This kind of close reading distinguishes the lecture from mere summary.
- **Scholarly attribution**: Bi credits Maurizio Viroli for the "Moses lied" reading, demonstrating awareness of the scholarly literature rather than presenting interpretations as self-evident.
- **Structural sophistication**: The four-part architecture (easing in, qualifying, contextualizing, critiquing) is pedagogically effective and shows genuine philosophical ambition — Bi is not merely presenting Machiavelli but engaging with him dialectically.
- **Historical range**: The lecture moves fluently between biblical narrative, Roman history (augury, Caudine Forks, Romagna), Renaissance politics (Cesare Borgia), and modern examples (Truman), demonstrating the breadth of Machiavelli's applicability.
- **Intellectual honesty about the Machiavelli paradox**: Bi is transparent about his own position — that studying Machiavelli radicalized him "in the opposite direction" (toward withdrawal from politics) — and engages fairly with the strongest counter-arguments.

**Weaknesses**

- **Lack of citation specificity for some Machiavelli quotes**: While Bi quotes Machiavelli extensively, he often does not specify which chapter or section of The Prince or the Discourses a given passage comes from. The Discourses 2.8 reference is one exception; most other quotes float without precise attribution.
- **The Plato Republic "massacre" claim is significantly overstated**: Bi says Plato calls for a massacre where "all the adults are killed" and "everyone above the age of 10 are led into the fields." The actual passage (Republic 540e-541a) says inhabitants over ten are "sent out into the country," which is ambiguous — it could mean exile or expulsion, not execution. Bi acknowledges it is a "euphemism," but presenting it as equivalent to Moses's massacre is a significant interpretive stretch that deserves more qualification.
- **The Samnites were not "wiped off the face of the earth" in a simple retaliatory act**: The Samnite Wars lasted decades (343-290 BCE) across three separate conflicts. The Samnites were eventually subjugated but the process was far more complex than the immediate retaliation narrative Bi implies. This simplification serves rhetorical purposes but sacrifices historical accuracy.
- **Thin engagement with alternative Machiavelli scholarship**: Beyond Viroli, no other scholars are cited. Major interpretive traditions — Leo Strauss's esoteric reading, Quentin Skinner's republican interpretation, Claude Lefort's democratic reading — are absent. This matters because the lecture's thesis (Machiavelli as idealist-realist hybrid) is contested terrain in the literature.
- **The Truman analogy is rhetorically effective but philosophically thin**: Truman's claim that he "never lost a night's sleep" is presented as evidence that necessary evil need not scar the soul. But this is a contested psychological and moral claim — many historians have questioned whether Truman's public statements reflected his private experience, and the moral status of the atomic bombings remains deeply contested among ethicists.
- **Over-reliance on the "easing you in" rhetoric**: The repeated disclaimers ("I'm still just easing you in," "it gets more scandalous") function as audience management but sometimes substitute for deeper philosophical engagement with the difficulties of each position.

## Counter-Arguments or Blind Spots

1. **The "necessary evil" framework begs the question of who determines necessity**
Bi presents Machiavelli's framework as though necessity is objectively identifiable: Moses had to massacre because God would have killed everyone otherwise; the Israelites had to eliminate natives because revenge was inevitable. But this obscures the most dangerous feature of the "necessary evil" doctrine — it gives the powerful unilateral authority to define what counts as necessary. Every genocidal regime in history has invoked necessity. Machiavelli provides no institutional check on this determination, which is a serious philosophical gap. Severity: significant — this does not refute the framework entirely but reveals that it is far more dangerous in practice than the lecture acknowledges.

2. **The sharp dichotomy between "wars of command" and "wars of habitation" is historically unrealistic**
Bi presents Machiavelli's two-category taxonomy as though it maps cleanly onto real situations. But most historical conflicts involve elements of both, and the line between "taking land" and "seeking obedience" is often blurred. Colonial history is full of cases where wars of command escalated into wars of habitation (and vice versa) depending on shifting political circumstances. The clean binary makes for elegant theory but poor historical description. Severity: moderate — it qualifies Machiavelli's framework without undermining the core thesis about political necessity.

3. **The "glory" mechanism does not reliably produce good outcomes**
Bi argues that glory-seeking is a more dependable psychological foundation for political leadership than altruism because glory-seekers need to benefit the public to earn recognition. But history provides abundant counter-examples: glory-seekers who destroyed their states in pursuit of personal fame (Napoleon, Alexander in his later campaigns), or who pursued glory through conquest rather than institution-building. Machiavelli's own distinction between "glory" and "fame" (Caesar pursued the latter) does some work here, but the line between the two is perilously thin in practice. Severity: moderate to significant — it does not refute the claim that altruism is insufficient but undermines the positive case for glory as a reliable alternative.

4. **The lecture under-engages with the Straussian reading of Machiavelli as genuinely subversive of all morality**
Leo Strauss, in Thoughts on Machiavelli (1958), argued that Machiavelli is not merely advocating evil for the sake of good but is engaged in a deeper philosophical project of undermining the very distinction between good and evil — a "teacher of evil" in the most radical sense. Bi acknowledges the "teacher of evil" label but spends most of the lecture softening it. If Strauss is right, then Bi's three qualifications are precisely the kind of surface reading that Machiavelli intended to mislead readers with. Severity: significant — this represents a major alternative interpretation that the lecture does not adequately address.

5. **The comparison with Plato's Gorgias is incomplete and somewhat one-sided**
Bi frames the Platonic challenge as "isn't your soul corrupted by doing injustice?" and then gives Machiavelli the last word. But the Gorgias argument is more sophisticated than this: Socrates argues not merely that injustice harms the soul but that the soul is the only thing that ultimately matters — that a life of political power without justice is literally not worth living. Machiavelli's response ("I love my fatherland more than my soul") is powerful rhetoric but does not actually engage the metaphysical claim. Plato would say that loving your fatherland more than your soul is itself a form of disordered love — a confusion about what has ultimate value. The lecture leaves this counter-response unexplored. Severity: moderate — it does not undermine Bi's presentation of Machiavelli but does leave the philosophical debate artificially resolved in Machiavelli's favor.

6. **The claim that "good people always make bad leaders" is an unfalsifiable generalization**
This is presented as one of Machiavelli's core teachings, but it is difficult to evaluate because "good" and "bad leader" are defined in ways that make the claim tautological: if a leader succeeds through morally questionable means, they are "great" not "good"; if a moral person fails as a leader, that confirms the thesis; if a moral person succeeds, one can always argue they were secretly Machiavellian. Historical counter-examples (Vaclav Havel, Nelson Mandela in certain phases of his leadership) are not considered. Severity: moderate — the claim has heuristic value but is presented as more universal than the evidence supports.

7. **The entrepreneurship analogy is anachronistic and weakly supported**
Bi claims he has "never met a single successful entrepreneur" primarily motivated by helping the world. This contemporary aside is unsubstantiated anecdote projected onto a 16th-century political philosophy. It conflates political founding with commercial enterprise in ways Machiavelli himself would likely not endorse, given his contempt for commercial republics relative to martial ones. Severity: minor — it is a rhetorical aside rather than a load-bearing argument, but it weakens the intellectual rigor of the lecture.

## Notable Quotes or Concepts Worth Developing

1. **"The ethical foundations of the Judeo-Christian worldview — the Ten Commandments themselves — is a founding murder hidden by a lie."**
(Bi, synthesizing Machiavelli's reading of Exodus 32.) This is a provocative thesis with essay potential: an exploration of how major ethical and legal systems originate in violence they subsequently prohibit. Connections to Walter Benjamin's "Critique of Violence" (the distinction between law-making and law-preserving violence), Rene Girard's scapegoat theory, and Jacques Derrida's "Force of Law."

2. **"Machiavelli points out Moses is evil, not to discredit him, but to empower you."**
(Bi's framing of Machiavelli's rhetorical strategy.) Worth developing into an essay on the difference between debunking and empowering modes of historical critique — how the same factual revelation (Moses lied) can serve opposite purposes depending on the critic's orientation. Connections to Nietzsche's genealogical method, which similarly reveals ignoble origins to liberate rather than condemn.

3. **"Cruelty well used" vs. "cruelty badly used" — Machiavelli's consequentialist ethics of violence**
(Machiavelli, The Prince, Chapter 8.) A rich concept for an essay on the moral philosophy of political violence: can cruelty be "well used"? What are the criteria? How does this compare to modern just war theory (jus in bello proportionality requirements)? Connections to Michael Walzer's Just and Unjust Wars, the ticking time-bomb thought experiment, and Hannah Arendt's On Violence.

4. **"The ferocity of this spectacle left the people at once satisfied and stupefied."**
(Machiavelli, The Prince, Chapter 7, on Remirro de Orco.) An extraordinary sentence that captures the dual psychology of political violence as catharsis and terror. Worth developing into an essay on political spectacle and the aesthetics of state violence. Connections to Guy Debord's Society of the Spectacle, Michel Foucault's Discipline and Punish (the opening execution scene), and the Roman concept of spectaculum.

5. **"Men take certain middle ways that are very harmful, for they do not know how to be either altogether wicked or altogether good."**
(Machiavelli, Discourses 1.26.) The "no halfway crooks" doctrine. An essay could explore this as a general theory of strategic failure — how moderation in extreme situations produces the worst outcomes. Connections to Clausewitz on the escalation logic of war, game theory (commitment problems), and Carl Schmitt's concept of the political as existential friend-enemy distinction.

6. **"I love my fatherland more than my soul."**
(Machiavelli, letter of April 16, 1527.) Perhaps Machiavelli's most revealing personal statement. Worth developing into an essay on patriotism as a form of self-sacrifice that goes beyond physical death to include moral/spiritual corruption. Connections to Kierkegaard's "teleological suspension of the ethical" (Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac), Max Weber's "Ethics of Responsibility" vs. "Ethics of Conviction," and Dietrich Bonhoeffer's theology of "responsible action."

7. **The "Machiavelli paradox" — writing about political evil while living as a model citizen**
(Bi's original observation.) This paradox could anchor an essay on the relationship between political theory and political practice: does the theorist of extreme politics have a duty to practice what they preach? Connections to Plato's reluctant philosopher-king, Marx's bourgeois lifestyle, Heidegger's Nazism as the inverse case (theory of authentic existence, practice of political collaboration).

8. **"For Christians, we are half man, half God. For Machiavelli, we are half man, half beast."**
(Bi's formulation of the anthropological break.) A sharp encapsulation of the philosophical divide that could ground an essay on competing anthropologies and their political consequences. Connections to Pico della Mirandola's Oration on the Dignity of Man (humans as self-creating beings between beast and angel), Hobbes's state of nature, and Nietzsche's "human, all too human."

9. **"Good people always make bad leaders" — the fragility of altruism thesis**
(Bi, synthesizing Machiavelli.) An essay could test this claim against historical evidence and psychological research on moral injury, leadership psychology, and the "dirty hands" problem in political philosophy. Connections to Bernard Williams on moral luck, Thomas Nagel's "War and Massacre," and the empirical literature on psychopathy in leadership.

10. **"Studying Machiavelli has thoroughly radicalized me — but in the opposite direction that he would have hoped for."**
(Bi's personal confession.) This could anchor a reflective essay on how the same philosophical argument can produce diametrically opposed practical conclusions depending on the reader's temperament. Machiavelli writes to inspire political action; Bi reads and is inspired to withdraw. Connections to the ancient debate between the active and contemplative lives (Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics X), Hannah Arendt's The Human Condition, and Epicurean withdrawal.

11. **The clever Roman general and the chickens — lying about the sacred as political necessity**
(Machiavelli, Discourses 1.14.) A vivid illustration of instrumental religion that could ground an essay on civil religion, noble lies, and the political function of belief. Connections to Plato's "noble lie" in Republic III, Rousseau's chapter on civil religion in The Social Contract, and Leo Strauss on esoteric writing and the social utility of religion.

## Distinction: Original vs. Cited Ideas

### Ideas from Machiavelli (via The Prince and the Discourses on Livy)

- The armed prophet doctrine: "All the armed prophets conquered and the unarmed ones were ruined."
- The prince must know how to "enter into evil when forced by necessity" while not departing from good when possible.
- The taxonomy of cruelty: "well used" (concentrated, brief, necessary, turned to utility for subjects) versus "badly used" (escalating over time).
- The wars of command vs. wars of habitation distinction (Discourses 2.8) and the corresponding scale of justified violence.
- The caress-or-eliminate principle: "Men should either be caressed or eliminated."
- The rejection of the middle way: "Men take certain middle ways that are very harmful."
- The Chiron metaphor: the prince must be half man, half beast, combining the lion (force) and the fox (cunning).
- Virtue as force guided by prudence; fortune as feminine chaos to be mastered.
- The necessity of appearing virtuous: "It is not necessary for a prince to have all the virtues, but it is indeed necessary to appear to have them."
- The Cesare Borgia/Remirro de Orco episode as exemplary cruelty well used.
- The Samnite/Caudine Forks cautionary tale about the middle way.
- The praise of Roman generals' instrumental use of augury.
- Moses as political leader (not mere prophet) — "one should not reason about Moses as he was a mere executor of things that had been ordered for him by God."
- The personal confession: "I love my fatherland more than my soul."

### Ideas from Other Thinkers (cited by Bi)

- **Maurizio Viroli** (Machiavelli scholar): The argument that Moses lied about divine authorization for the Levite massacre, based on close textual reading of Exodus 32. Viroli is a Princeton-based scholar whose works include Niccolò's Smile: A Biography of Machiavelli and Machiavelli's God. Bi credits him directly for this interpretive insight. Note: the transcript renders the name as "Maritzo Veroli," likely a transcription error.
- **Plato** (Gorgias): The argument that it is better to suffer injustice than to commit it, because committing injustice harms the soul. Bi uses this as the primary philosophical challenge to Machiavelli in Part 4.
- **Plato** (Republic, 540e-541a): The claim that even Plato's ideal city requires a founding act of expulsion/violence — sending everyone over ten "into the fields." Bi interprets this as a concession to Machiavelli's point about the necessity of violence in founding. This interpretation is contestable: "sent into the fields" may mean exile, not execution.
- **Harry Truman**: The claim that Truman "never lost a single night's sleep" over the atomic bombings, used as evidence that agents of necessary evil need not suffer psychological damage. This is an attested historical quote (from 1959 interviews).
- **Mobb Deep** (referred to as "great American philosophers"): "There is no such thing as halfway crooks" — used to humorously summarize Machiavelli's rejection of the middle way. The line is from "Shook Ones, Part II" (1995).
- **Tupac Shakur**: Mentioned as a reader of Machiavelli in prison who adopted "Makaveli" as a stage name/persona for his posthumous album The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory (1996). A brief aside, not developed into an argument.

### Bi's Own Interpretive Contributions

- **The four-part rhetorical architecture** of the lecture itself — the strategy of "easing the audience in" through Moses before qualifying, contextualizing, and finally critiquing Machiavelli — is Bi's original pedagogical design and is among the lecture's greatest strengths.
- **The "Machiavelli paradox"** — that Machiavelli wrote about evil in politics while living as a model citizen, and that this paradox is the inverse of the typical pattern (writing about virtue while practicing vice) — is Bi's own formulation, though the observation about Machiavelli's personal integrity is well-established in the biographical literature.
- **The distinction between "good" people and "great" people** as Machiavelli's target audience — where the good are motivated by altruism (too fragile to survive evil) and the great are motivated by glory (robust enough to persist) — is Bi's synthetic reading. While it draws on Machiavelli's texts, the explicit framing of altruism as psychologically "too fragile" for political founding is Bi's original contribution.
- **The claim that glory "bridges" self-interest and public benefit** as a "dependable psychological mechanism" — this is Bi's own theoretical formulation, synthesizing Machiavelli's scattered remarks about glory, fame, and reputation into a unified psychological theory.
- **The personal confession that studying Machiavelli radicalized Bi "in the opposite direction"** — toward withdrawal from politics rather than engagement — is an original and intellectually honest rhetorical move. It sets up Machiavelli's strongest counter-argument (you can't truly withdraw; the game finds you) and the letter about loving Florence more than his soul.
- **The entrepreneurship analogy** (successful entrepreneurs are never primarily motivated by altruism) is Bi's own contemporary application, not drawn from Machiavelli or the scholarly literature.
- **The framing of Machiavelli as "idealist in ends, realist in means"** — while related to existing scholarly discussions (e.g., Skinner's republican Machiavelli), the specific formulation and the argument that this tension is what "makes his work so bloody" is Bi's synthetic contribution.
- **The characterization of Plato's Republic founding passage as a "massacre" and a "euphemism"** is Bi's interpretive gloss. The text itself (540e) is ambiguous and most scholars read it as expulsion or exile rather than killing. This is an original but contestable reading that serves Bi's argument about Plato conceding Machiavelli's point.

## Factual Notes Requiring Verification

1. **"Moses said unto them, thus sayeth the Lord God of Israel" — claimed to be one of the few instances where the Bible attributes the divine command through Moses's speech rather than direct narration.** This is a defensible textual observation, but the claim that this is one of the "few instances" in Exodus where Moses attributes God's words rather than the narrator doing so needs verification against a systematic survey of Exodus. The broader pattern in the Pentateuch is more mixed than Bi suggests.

2. **"3,000 Israelites died that day" (Exodus 32:28).** This is accurate per the biblical text.

3. **The claim that God "couldn't have had time" to give the massacre command because "immediately when God relents, Moses speeds down the mountain."** While narratively plausible, biblical chronology is notoriously non-linear, and many scholars caution against reading sequential causation into biblical narrative structure. The argument is suggestive but not conclusive.

4. **Plato's Republic: "All the adults are killed, everyone above the age of 10 are led into the fields."** This is an overstatement. Republic 540e-541a says inhabitants over ten are "sent out into the country" — the standard scholarly reading is exile or expulsion, not execution. Bi's characterization of this as a "euphemism" for killing is his own interpretation, not the consensus view.

5. **"Maurizio Viroli" — rendered in transcript as "Maritzo Veroli."** The correct name is Maurizio Viroli, a political theorist at Princeton University and the University of Texas at Austin. His relevant works include Machiavelli's God (2010) and Niccolò's Smile: A Biography of Machiavelli (2000). The specific argument about Moses's lie should be verified against Viroli's published work — it is consistent with his general interpretive approach but the precise attribution may be from personal conversation rather than a published text.

6. **"Cesare himself was the closest person who came to unifying Italy at the time."** This is a common claim in Machiavelli scholarship but historically debatable. Cesare Borgia's power was always dependent on his father Pope Alexander VI, and his territorial holdings in the Romagna were relatively modest compared to the scale of Italian unification. The claim reflects Machiavelli's admiration more than sober historical assessment.

7. **"Remirro" — the full historical name is Remirro de Orco (also spelled Ramiro de Lorqua).** Bi's account of his execution matches the historical record as presented in The Prince, Chapter 7. The detail about being "split in half" and displayed with the knife and woodblock is accurate to Machiavelli's text, though the actual historical circumstances may have been somewhat different from Machiavelli's dramatic account.

8. **Harry Truman "never lost a single night's sleep" over the atomic bombings.** Truman did say this in 1959 interviews and on multiple other occasions. However, historians including Gar Alperovitz and Barton Bernstein have questioned whether Truman's public statements reflected his private experience. Some evidence suggests more ambivalence than the public persona indicated. Bi presents the quote at face value.

9. **Tupac read Machiavelli "in prison" and "his stage name was actually Machiavelli."** Tupac read The Prince while incarcerated at Clinton Correctional Facility in 1995. "Makaveli" was the stage name used for his posthumous album The Don Killuminati: The 7 Day Theory (1996), not his permanent stage name. Minor imprecision.

10. **The claim that "the Israelites' genocide of the natives" was Machiavelli's endorsed policy.** Machiavelli does discuss Moses and the conquest of Canaan in Discourses 2.8 in the context of wars of habitation. However, the extent to which Machiavelli "endorses" versus "describes" this policy is a matter of scholarly debate. Bi presents it as straightforward endorsement, which is one valid reading but not the only one.

11. **"Machiavelli was born in a very divided Italy where Republican city-states, or some princely city-states as well, are vying for power."** Accurate in general terms. Machiavelli was born in Florence in 1469 during the era of competing Italian city-states. He served as Secretary to the Second Chancery of the Republic of Florence from 1498 to 1512.

12. **The characterization of Machiavelli as having declined "a very lucrative salary by Rome" while writing the Florentine Histories.** This requires verification. Machiavelli was commissioned by Cardinal Giulio de' Medici (later Pope Clement VII) to write the Florentine Histories, and he was compensated for this work. The specific claim about declining a separate "lucrative salary" is not well-attested in the standard biographies and may conflate or simplify the historical record.
